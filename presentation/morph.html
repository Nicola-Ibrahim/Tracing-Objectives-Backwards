The High-Stakes Engineering Landscape

The Shift

Modern engineering has moved from simple components to complex, multi-functional systems requiring advanced optimization.

The Conflict

Designers must balance competing objectives. For example: maximizing thermal efficiency vs. minimizing weight.

Part II • Slide 2

Why is it Hard?

Hadamard’s Criteria for Well-Posedness

1. Existence

Does a solution \( x \) actually exist for your target \( y^* \)? Often, desired targets are physically unreachable.

CORE ISSUE

2. Uniqueness

Is there only ONE solution? Engineering is plagued by the "One-to-Many" mapping problem.

3. Stability

Do small changes in \( y \) lead to small changes in \( x \)? Stability is rare in inverse landscapes.

View Speaker Notes
"To understand the 'headache,' we look at Hadamard’s Criteria. In our case, Uniqueness is the biggest hurdle. Because many designs can produce the same result, a model that only gives one answer—or worse, an average of many answers—is fundamentally flawed."
Part II • Slide 3

The Core Problem: Ill-Posedness

The Mathematical Dilemma

\[ f^{-1}(y^*) \to \{x_1, x_2, \dots\} \]

Because \( f(x) \) is often a complex, non-linear physical simulation, its inverse is Ill-Posed. There is no direct function from \( y \to x \), only a distribution \( p(x|y) \).

Information Loss

The forward process 'collapses' multiple design features into a single performance metric.

Ambiguity

One performance result maps back to countless valid structural configurations.

Part II • Slide 4

The "Average" Trap

Why Regression Fails Inverse Problems

Standard Neural Networks (using MSE loss) attempt to find the mean design. In a non-linear space, the mean of two valid designs is almost always an invalid design.

\( \hat{x} = E[x|y] \quad \text{but} \quad f(\hat{x}) \neq y^* \)

Visual Evidence

"The average of a tuxedo and a swimsuit is neither formal nor waterproof."

View Speaker Notes
"This is the heart of the research. If we use standard regression, the model attempts to minimize error by predicting the middle point between valid clusters. But in physics, the 'middle point' often breaks constraints. We don't want the average; we want the full distribution."
Part III • Slide 1

The Mission: Target-Driven Inverse Exploration

Objective

Enable designers to specify a target \( y^* \) and receive a set of feasible candidates \( \{\hat{x}_1, \dots, \hat{x}_K\} \).

Innovation

Moving away from "optimizing" one specific design to "exploring" a full distribution of potential solutions.

Utility

Providing the designer with a "menu" of valid options, rather than a rigid, single black-box output.

View Speaker Notes
"The goal of this research is to build a bridge between requirements and results. We want a system where a designer provides the 'Target Objectives,' and the model explores the history of data to find every possible design that could achieve that target. We call this 'Inverse Exploration.'"
Part III • Slide 2

Forward Prediction vs. Inverse Mapping

The Known

Forward Problem

\( f(x) \to y \)

Unique, stable, and mathematically well-behaved. Easy for standard neural networks.

The Challenge

Inverse Problem

\( f^{-1}(y^*) \to x \)

Often "ill-posed" and multi-modal. Requires generative mapping.

Capturing the Solution Cloud

Part III • Slide 6

Measuring "Can we hit the target?"

Metric Pillar 1: Plausibility

Plausibility measures how well the generated designs satisfy the user's performance requirements. We calculate the Best-Shot Discrepancy (\( S_{min} \)):

\[ S_{min}(y^*) = \min_{k \in \{1 \dots K\}} \|E(\hat{x}^{(k)}) - y^*\|_2 \]

Goal: We only care how close the closest arrow is to the bullseye.

ECDF Analysis

Empirical Cumulative Distribution Function shows what percentage of targets the model "solves" within tolerance \( \epsilon \).

Part III • Slide 7

Measuring "Can we trust the uncertainty?"

Metric Pillar 2: Reliability

Reliability (or Faithfulness) measures if the model’s predicted uncertainty matches its actual performance. A reliable model is "honest"—it knows exactly how much it doesn't know.

  • PIT Curve

    Diagnostic tool where a uniform diagonal plot indicates perfect calibration.

  • MACE

    Mean Absolute Calibration Error: Quantifies deviation from the ideal diagonal.

  • CSRP

    Global score penalizing both "missing the target" and "being too vague."
