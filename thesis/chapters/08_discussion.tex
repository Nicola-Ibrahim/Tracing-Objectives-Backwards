\chapter{Discussion}\label{chap:discussion}

This chapter synthesizes the empirical findings from Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}, relates them to
the evaluation criteria introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}, and discusses implications,
limitations, and directions for future work.


\section{Interpreting the Findings and Implications}\label{sec:discussion_interpretation}

The inverse models studied in this work are evaluated as conditional samplers that support
target-driven design under a propose--check loop. The experiments in Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}
indicate that, when the training archive is representative of the reachable outcome region, learned
inverse mappings can translate a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\ast}$ into decision candidates whose
forward evaluations approach that target within a finite candidate budget. This result should be
interpreted operationally: the inverse model provides a practical interface for exploration by
producing a short list of plausible decisions conditioned on a desired outcome, rather than
guaranteeing a unique or physically realizable design.


A first theme is the distinction between attainment and uncertainty quality. Budgeted outcome-space
discrepancy is intentionally optimistic: it quantifies whether the sampler can produce at least one
good match within $K$ draws, which matches the user-facing notion of success under limited inspection.
However, attainment alone cannot determine whether the candidate set constitutes a trustworthy
conditional distribution. Decision-space diagnostics therefore play a distinct role: they separate
reliability under repeated conditioning from practical usefulness, and they prevent over-interpreting
``good hits'' that arise from chance or from overly aggressive sampling variance.

A second theme is that model comparison depends on the candidate budget $K$. Changes in attainment as a
function of $K$ reflect how each model allocates probability mass under the conditional distribution:
some models concentrate mass and saturate quickly, while others distribute mass more broadly and
continue to benefit from additional samples. This interaction is central in one-to-many inverse
problems, where multiple distinct decisions can map to similar outcomes. Accordingly, the preferred
model depends on whether the intended workflow prioritizes rapid attainment with small $K$ or broader
coverage that improves with additional candidates.

These interpretations imply a practical evaluation and selection stance. Outcome-space attainment
should be treated as a budgeted utility measure: it answers whether the system can deliver at least
one actionable proposal within a fixed budget, and it should therefore be reported explicitly as a
function of $K$. Decision-space diagnostics should be treated as distribution-quality indicators: when
candidate dispersion informs downstream exploration or uncertainty must be interpretable, calibration
and CRPS-based assessment justify whether the sampled set can be treated as a meaningful conditional
distribution rather than a collection of independent guesses. Taken together, the combined reporting
format supports a workflow in which attainment establishes feasibility under a given $K$, and
reliability and probabilistic accuracy determine whether the resulting candidate set supports informed
exploration rather than ad hoc trial-and-error.

\section{Limitations and Assumptions}\label{sec:discussion_limits}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The evaluation framework defined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} provides a rigorous statistical basis
for assessing inverse models. However, interpreting these results in a physical materials-discovery
setting requires assumptions and comes with limitations that reflect the gap between computational
attainment and experimental reality.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Fidelity of the forward checker.
  The propose--check contract assumes that the evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ functions as a reliable
  ground-truth proxy (e.g., a DFT simulator, an FEA routine, or a high-fidelity surrogate).
  We assume that $\mathcal{E}$ captures the essential physics required to validate the objective
  vector $\vect{y}$. A limitation is that low attainment certifies validity only with respect to the
  checker: if $\mathcal{E}$ omits relevant physical constraints (e.g., thermal stability, oxidation,
  or degradation mechanisms), a design that appears successful computationally may still fail in
  experimental synthesis. In this case, the reported attainment reflects internal consistency rather
  than physical certainty.


  \item Completeness of the design space representation $\mathcal{X}$.
  We assume that the decision vector $\vect{x}$ provides a sufficient and complete description of 
  the material to determine the properties in $\mathcal{Y}$. Under this assumption, the model 
  utilizes its latent space to characterize the manifold of the provided data and map the 
  "one-to-many" relationships inherent in the archive. A limitation arises when real-world 
  performance depends on unobserved physical factors—such as microstructure, grain boundaries, 
  or explicit synthesis pathways—that are absent from the training dataset. Consequently, 
  the model may propose a mathematically consistent candidate $\vect{x}$ that nonetheless 
  remains physically underdetermined. In such regimes, the latent representation successfully 
  resolves the variance present within the observed data but cannot account for the epistemic 
  uncertainty arising from a physically incomplete design space.


  \item Conditioning on the Pareto Boundary.
  Our modeling approach assumes that by training on the comprehensive search history—incorporating both sub-optimal exploratory points and converged solutions—the inverse model can successfully extrapolate to the extreme edges of the objective space. We assume the learned mapping $p(\vect{x} \mid \vect{y})$ remains valid even when the conditioning vector $\vect{y}^\star$ is situated exactly on the empirical Pareto Front. A limitation is that data density is inherently lowest at these performance boundaries, as the optimizer naturally spends less time at the absolute limits of feasible trade-offs compared to the broader exploration region. This sparsity may lead to poorly calibrated uncertainty or "mode collapse" precisely where the user cares the most: the optimal trade-offs. In this case, the reported performance at the boundary may reflect a failure of the model to resolve the sparse local manifold rather than a lack of valid solutions in the decision space.

\section{Future Work}\label{sec:future_work}

Building on the findings in Chapters~\ref{chap:methodology}--\ref{chap:experiments}, several concrete
extensions could strengthen training stability and improve the reliability of target-driven inverse
querying.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Tandem loss for outcome-consistent training.
  Augment inverse-model training with a tandem objective that penalizes forward inconsistency of
  generated candidates. Concretely, the inverse model is trained not only to fit paired data, but also
  to minimize a forward discrepancy term of the form
  $d\!\big(\mathbf{f}(\widehat{\mathbf{x}}),\,\mathbf{y}\big)$ for sampled
  $\widehat{\mathbf{x}}\sim \widehat{q}(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{y})$.
  This encourages candidates to remain outcome-consistent with the conditioning target and can reduce
  the gap between likelihood-based training and downstream attainment.

  \item Conformal checking before candidate generation.
  Introduce a lightweight conformal layer that quantifies whether a requested target $\mathbf{y}^\ast$
  is supported by the training distribution before sampling candidates. Using a calibration split, one
  can build a split-conformal acceptance region in outcome space (e.g., based on residuals or distances
  to a reference set) and reject or flag targets that fall outside the calibrated region. This would
  prevent overconfident querying in unsupported regimes and would provide an explicit, finite-sample
  notion of target plausibility prior to running the inverse sampler.

  \item Pareto-distance aware evaluation of generated outcomes.
  Augment evaluation by measuring how far generated outcomes lie from the empirical Pareto front.
  Concretely, after forward-checking candidates to obtain $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$, report the
  distance from $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ to a reference approximation of the Pareto front (e.g.,
  nearest-point distance in objective space, optionally under the same scaling used for
  $\mathcal{E}_{\mathcal{Y}}$). This provides an additional lens aligned with the intended use case:
  beyond matching a single target, it quantifies whether generated candidates remain Pareto-relevant
  (near the trade-off boundary) or drift into dominated regions. It also supports extrapolation-aware
  reporting by distinguishing errors that remain near the front from those that move far away from the
  achievable trade-off set.
\end{itemize}


\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis established a robust framework for inverse decision mapping in target-driven design, positioning inverse models as conditional probabilistic samplers that support the discovery of high-performance configurations. By investigating the $f_5$ bi-objective landscape, we demonstrated that the relationship between outcomes and decisions is inherently one-to-many, requiring an exploration strategy grounded in a structured propose--check loop. The study confirms that while inverse models can successfully navigate complex search histories, their evaluation requires a dual-perspective approach that accounts for both the precision of the generated outcomes and the integrity of the underlying decision-space manifold.

The central methodological contribution is an evaluation protocol that bifurcates model performance into objective-space plausibility and decision-space faithfulness. Rather than relying on simple point successes, the objective-space analysis utilizes candidate-cloud diagnostics, such as cloud bias and within-set dispersion, to characterize the aggregate geometry of generated outcomes. Complementing this, decision-space diagnostics based on PIT calibration and the Continuous Ranked Probability Score provide essential evidence of a model's statistical reliability. This separation ensures that a model is not merely hitting a target by chance or through excessive variance, but is instead providing a trustworthy representation of the conditional distribution.

Our findings indicate that objective-space alignment alone is insufficient to fully characterize model behavior in ambiguous design regimes. By integrating distributional metrics into the propose--check contract, this work supports a more rigorous selection of inverse models that are aligned with the demands of practical engineering workflows. Future work should focus on hardening this interface through tandem consistency losses, conformal target screening, and Pareto-distance evaluations to ensure that generated candidates remain both trade-off relevant and physically consistent with the desired performance profile.
