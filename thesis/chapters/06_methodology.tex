\chapter{Methodology}\label{chap:methodology}

Earlier chapters established inverse querying as a practical interface for multi-objective design:
a user specifies a target objective vector and expects actionable decision suggestions, despite
non-uniqueness and possible infeasibility. Building on that foundation, the present chapter
specifies the framework adopted to answer such queries and to evaluate inverse models under a
shared and reproducible protocol. The central aim is to make claims about attainment traceable to
a declared evaluation rule, rather than to implicit properties of a particular inverse model.


The methodology follows a model-based inverse design approach. An inverse model is trained offline
from paired data and is used at query time to propose decision candidates; a separate evaluation
stage then checks and ranks candidates in objective space using a stated outcome evaluator and a
task-defined discrepancy. Three commitments are held constant throughout
the empirical study. The method returns a candidate set rather than a single decision, attainment is
defined in objective space through an explicit evaluator and discrepancy, and comparisons use a fixed
candidate budget and a consistent reporting rule.

\section{Problem formulation}\label{sec:problem_formulation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inverse querying is formulated as learning a conditional model over decisions given objectives.
Let $\mathcal{X}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ denote the admissible decision space and
$\mathcal{Y}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}$ the objective space. A query specifies a target
$\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathcal{Y}$. The method returns a set of candidate decisions
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}\subset\mathcal{X}$ together with an ordering induced by
objective-space attainment. The output therefore consists of alternatives and a declared basis for
preferring one alternative over another.

The inverse component is represented as a parametric conditional distribution
$q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$. A distributional inverse can represent multi-modality and is
therefore suitable for one-to-many inverse structure, which is expected when multiple decisions
produce similar objective outcomes under the task discrepancy. 

Training uses paired observations $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ and fits
parameters by regularized conditional maximum likelihood,
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\theta}\in\arg\min_{\theta}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\Big[-\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)\Big] + \lambda\,\Omega(\theta),
\label{eq:cond_mle_inverse}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(\theta)$ is a regularization term and $\lambda\ge 0$ controls its contribution. The
objective depends only on paired data and does not require an analytic forward operator during
learning, which keeps the approach applicable when forward evaluation is available only for
assessment. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020}

At query time, the method draws a fixed-size candidate set,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_generation}
\end{equation}
and assesses attainment in objective space using an outcome evaluator
$\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$ together with a task-defined discrepancy $d(\cdot,\cdot)$.
\cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020} Candidate $k$ receives score
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^\star) \;=\;
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^\star\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_scores}
\end{equation}
which induces a ranking over candidates. Fixing $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ defines the evaluation contract
used throughout the thesis and makes attainment claims traceable to an explicit rule that is shared
across inverse model families. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020}

\section{Rationale and trade-offs}\label{sec:rationale_tradeoffs}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Model-based inverse design is adopted because it aligns with the constraints of inverse querying in
multi-objective settings. Non-uniqueness is a typical feature of the inverse relation, and a method
that returns a single decision can conceal ambiguity and cause comparisons to depend on model-specific
selection behavior. A conditional model addresses this directly by supporting multiple candidates
per target and by moving selection to an explicit objective-space rule. 

The methodology is also selected to support repeated use. Offline training amortizes the cost of
inversion, so query-time computation reduces to sampling under a fixed candidate budget and evaluating
candidates under the declared contract in Section~\ref{sec:problem_formulation}. This design follows
established practice in amortized and simulation-based inference, where conditional density estimation
enables efficient repeated conditioning queries once training is complete. \cite{Papamakarios2021,Cranmer2020SBI}

A further motivation is comparability across benchmarks with heterogeneous forward access. Some
settings provide a trusted simulator, while others permit only surrogate-based or recorded-outcome
evaluation. By defining attainment through a stated evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, the
methodology keeps the success criterion stable while allowing the evaluation mechanism to reflect what
a benchmark provides. 

These choices introduce trade-offs that are important for interpretation. Performance depends on the
quality and fidelity of the evaluator used for checking, and a fixed candidate budget $K$ limits the
probability of identifying a high-quality candidate in strongly multi-modal settings. In addition, if
targets fall outside the region supported by the training archive, the inverse model may propose
candidates that are plausible under the learned distribution but attain the target poorly under the
evaluator. These limitations motivate the controlled evaluation protocol and the reporting strategy
used in the experimental chapter. 

Query-time optimization remains complementary rather than central. When evaluations are reliable and
affordable, black-box refinement procedures such as Bayesian optimization can improve a candidate, but
they introduce additional design choices that complicate fair comparison, including acquisition design,
initialization, and stopping criteria. For this reason, the baseline in
this thesis prioritizes amortized candidate generation with explicit checking and ranking, and treats
optimization-based refinement as an optional extension.

\section{Framework and protocol}\label{sec:framework}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes the workflow used throughout the thesis. The protocol follows
a propose--check structure: an inverse model proposes candidate decisions for a queried target, and a
separate checking stage evaluates and ranks those candidates in objective space under the declared
evaluator and discrepancy. The separation ensures that ambiguity is handled through generation, while
attainment is assessed by an explicit scoring rule.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \begin{adjustbox}{width=\linewidth}
      \input{figures/framework}
  \end{adjustbox}
  \caption[Propose--Check Workflow Framework]{Propose--check workflow used throughout the thesis. An inverse model generates a candidate set for a target objective vector, and an explicit evaluation stage scores and ranks candidates using a stated outcome evaluator and discrepancy.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

The workflow is executed in four stages. First, the inverse proposal model is trained offline from
paired data using the conditional likelihood objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. Second, a query
$\vect{y}^{\star}$ is answered by sampling $K$ candidates from the trained conditional model, as in
\eqref{eq:candidate_generation}. Treating $K$ as fixed is part of the experimental interface and keeps
the output format consistent across model families. Third, each candidate is checked by applying the
evaluator and discrepancy in \eqref{eq:candidate_scores}. The evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ is selected to
match the benchmark and deployment context and may be a simulator, a measurement routine, a surrogate
used for checking, or an offline mapping reserved for evaluation. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020} Fourth,
the method returns candidates ordered by their objective-space scores; when a single decision is
required, the best-ranked candidate under the declared rule is selected.

Two practical implications follow from this protocol. Candidate generation reflects what is supported
by the training archive through $q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$, while attainment is
defined by the pair $(\mathcal{E},d)$. Recording $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ alongside model configuration
therefore makes results auditable and enables fair comparison across inverse model families.

\section{Inverse-model training}\label{sec:training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

With the propose--check protocol fixed in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, implementation begins by
training the inverse proposal model from paired decision--objective examples
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. The training goal is to obtain a conditional
generator $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ that captures the empirical decision--objective relation
while remaining expressive enough to represent one-to-many structure. The modeling background in
Chapter~\ref{chap:Background} introduces the three conditional families used in the thesis and
derives their probabilistic forms. The present section specifies the optimization objectives as used
in the experiments.

Across models, parameter estimation follows a shared principle: fit the conditional distribution to
the observed pairs under the regularized conditional likelihood objective in
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. The role of regularization is collected in $\Omega(\cdot)$ and includes
standard stabilizing terms such as weight decay, together with any model-specific penalties. The
evaluation components, namely the outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, are not used
during training and enter only through the checking rule defined by \eqref{eq:candidate_scores}.

The three instantiations below differ in how they parameterize $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ and
how the conditional log-density in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} is computed or approximated.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mixture density network}\label{subsec:mdn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The mixture density network represents the conditional density as a finite Gaussian mixture with
$\vect{y}$-dependent parameters,
\begin{equation}
  q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\,
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x};\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y}),\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\big),
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})=1,\ \ \pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\ge 0 .
  \label{eq:mdn_density}
\end{equation}
Training substitutes \eqref{eq:mdn_density} into the objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} and
optimises the resulting mixture negative log-likelihood. The implementation enforces the standard
constraints on mixture weights and uses a stable parameterisation of covariance matrices, consistent
with the model definition in Chapter~\ref{chap:Background}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conditional variational autoencoder}\label{subsec:cvae_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The conditional variational autoencoder introduces latent variables to represent conditional
variability. Direct maximisation of $\log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ is replaced by maximising
a variational lower bound using an encoder $q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$ and decoder
$p_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})$. The training objective is the negative evidence lower
bound plus regularisation,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta},\widehat{\phi}\in\arg\min_{\theta,\phi}\;
  -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Big[
    \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)}\log p_{\theta}(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z})
    - \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\,\|\,p(\vect{z})\big)
  \Big]
  +\lambda\,\Omega(\theta,\phi),
  \label{eq:cvae_elbo}
\end{equation}
which provides a tractable surrogate for conditional likelihood training. At query time, latent
variation allows the decoder to generate diverse candidates for a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$,
consistent with the fixed candidate budget used in evaluation.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invertible neural network}\label{subsec:inn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The invertible neural network defines a conditional density through an invertible transformation
between a simple reference latent distribution and the decision, conditioned on the objective
vector. Using a conditional invertible map $T_{\theta}(\cdot;\vect{y})$,
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x} = T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}),\qquad \vect{z}\sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}),
  \label{eq:inn_forward_map}
\end{equation}
the conditional log-density follows from a change-of-variables construction,
\begin{equation}
  \log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \log p(\vect{z}) +
  \log\left|\det \frac{\partial T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y})}{\partial \vect{x}}\right|,
  \qquad
  \vect{z}=T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y}).
  \label{eq:inn_change_of_vars}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:inn_change_of_vars} into \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} yields a direct
likelihood-based objective. Sampling is obtained by drawing $\vect{z}$ from the reference prior and
applying $T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}^{\star})$, which integrates naturally with the fixed candidate
budget used in the propose--check protocol.

In summary, the three model families share the same training goal, namely a conditional generator
$q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ fit from paired data under the objective in
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. They differ in how conditional variability is represented, through
mixture components in MDNs, latent-variable decoding in CVAEs, or invertible latent transport in
INNs. Downstream evaluation treats sampled candidates uniformly via the shared checking rule in
\eqref{eq:candidate_scores}, so empirical comparisons reflect differences in the learned conditional
model rather than differences in the evaluation interface.

\section{Evaluation plan}\label{sec:evaluation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Evaluation follows the propose--check contract defined in Section~\ref{sec:framework}. For each target
$\vect{y}^{\star}$, an inverse model produces $K$ decision candidates
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. A fixed forward checker computes the corresponding checked
objective vectors,
\[
  \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} \;=\; \mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}), \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
\]
where $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}$ denotes the best available evaluator for the
benchmark (simulator, measurement routine, or checking surrogate). All reported metrics are derived
from checked quantities, and $\mathcal{E}$ is not accessed by inverse models at test time beyond
producing candidates. Fixing $\mathcal{E}$, the discrepancy definition $d$, and the candidate budget
$K$ keeps comparisons attributable to inverse modeling choices rather than changes in the evaluation
interface.

Two complementary evaluation views are reported. The first view evaluates the user-facing inverse
querying interface by measuring attainment in objective space under the checker. The second view
evaluates the inverse model as a conditional sampler in design space, focusing on uncertainty
behavior. The separation is deliberate: objective-space metrics quantify whether a target can be
matched under the declared checker, while design-space diagnostics quantify whether sampled candidates
behave like a meaningful conditional distribution over decisions.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Objective-space plausibility diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_objective}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates how well a candidate set can match a target after checking.
Given checked outcomes $\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$, attainment is summarised using
discrepancy scores computed in standardized objective space, so that distances are comparable across
dimensions.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Standardized discrepancy contract}\label{sec:eval_discrepancy_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $\widetilde{\vect{y}}$ denote objectives after standardization using statistics computed on the
training split. Euclidean distance in the standardized space provides a transparent default. When
objective dimensions exhibit strong correlation, a Mahalanobis discrepancy incorporates covariance
structure,
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{Mah}}(\vect{u},\vect{v})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{(\vect{u}-\vect{v})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\vect{u}-\vect{v})},
  \label{eq:mahalanobis_distance}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is estimated on a reference split of outcomes and regularized to ensure
stable inversion. \cite{Mahalanobis1936, DeMaesschalck2000}

Given a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, each candidate receives a checked discrepancy score
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  d\!\big(\widetilde{\mathcal{E}}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\widetilde{\vect{y}}^{\star}\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:per_candidate_discrepancy}
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{\mathcal{E}}(\cdot)$ denotes evaluation followed by standardization in objective
space. The discrepancy $d$ and the checker $\mathcal{E}$ together define the evaluation contract: all
attainment statements in this section are made with respect to the declared $(\mathcal{E},d)$ pair.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Standardized residuals}\label{sec:eval_plausibility_z}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

While $s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ is convenient for ranking candidates, its absolute magnitude is still
hard to interpret without introducing a domain-specific acceptance radius. To provide a user-centric
view that remains consistent across objective types, we express per-objective mismatch in standardized
units relative to variability estimated from the training split.

Let $\mathbf{Y}_{\mathrm{tr}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_{\mathrm{tr}}\times m}$ denote the training-split objective
matrix with entries $y_{i,j}$, where $i\in\{1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{tr}}\}$ indexes training samples and
$j\in\{1,\dots,m\}$ indexes objective components (objective dimensions). For a user target
$\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ and the checked outcome of the $k$-th candidate
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$, define a target-centered standardized residual using a
training-derived scale $\tau_j>0$ as
\begin{equation}
  r_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j - y^{\star}_j}{\tau_j}.
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual}
\end{equation}
Here $\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j$ is the $j$-th objective value of candidate $k$, $y^{\star}_j$ is the $j$-th
component of the user target, and $\tau_j$ is a scale estimated from the training outcomes of objective
$j$.

Collect components into the residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big(r_1^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),\dots,r_m^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{m}.
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual_vector}
\end{equation}
Here $m$ is the number of objectives, and $\vect{r}^{(k)}$ measures how far candidate $k$ deviates from
the target in unitless training-derived units, component by component.

When $d$ is Euclidean in the coordinate-wise standardized space, the checked discrepancy score can be
written directly in terms of the residual vector as
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2.
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid}
\end{equation}
Here $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ is the Euclidean norm, and $s^{(k)}$ is the scalar used to rank candidates
for the given target $\vect{y}^{\star}$.

Under a Mahalanobis discrepancy in standardized coordinates, the corresponding score is
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)^{\top}
  \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)}.
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_mahal}
\end{equation}
Here $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ is the covariance matrix of standardized objectives
estimated on a reference split (and regularized for inversion), and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}$ accounts
for correlation between objective components.

The scale choice enters only through $\tau_j$. The following three options are used depending on the
shape of the training marginal for objective $j$.

The SD-based scale is the default when the training marginal for objective $j$ is approximately
symmetric and unimodal. Define the training-split mean and standard deviation as
\begin{equation}
  \mu_j \;=\; \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{tr}}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{tr}}} y_{i,j},
  \qquad
  \sigma_j \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{tr}}-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{tr}}}(y_{i,j}-\mu_j)^2 }.
  \label{eq:train_mean_sd}
\end{equation}
Here $\mu_j$ and $\sigma_j$ are computed from the $j$-th column of $\mathbf{Y}_{\mathrm{tr}}$.

With $\tau_j=\sigma_j$, the residual in \eqref{eq:target_centered_residual} becomes the SD-based
objective-centered $z$ residual
\begin{equation}
  z_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j - y^{\star}_j}{\sigma_j}.
  \label{eq:z_sd_def}
\end{equation}
Here $z_j^{(k)}$ is dimensionless and reports deviation from the target in units of one training
standard deviation for objective $j$.

This SD choice is equivalent to measuring the difference between standardized coordinates. Define the
standardization map
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Std}_j(u_j) \;=\; \frac{u_j-\mu_j}{\sigma_j},
  \qquad
  \mathrm{Std}(\vect{u}) \;=\; \big(\mathrm{Std}_1(u_1),\dots,\mathrm{Std}_m(u_m)\big).
  \label{eq:std_map_sd}
\end{equation}
Here $\vect{u}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ is any objective vector, and $\mathrm{Std}(\vect{u})$ standardizes each
component using the training statistics $(\mu_j,\sigma_j)$.

Let $\widetilde{\vect{y}}^{(k)}=\mathrm{Std}(\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)})$ and
$\widetilde{\vect{y}}^{\star}=\mathrm{Std}(\vect{y}^{\star})$. Then for each component $j$,
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{y}^{(k)}_j - \widetilde{y}^{\star}_j
  \;=\;
  \frac{\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j-\mu_j}{\sigma_j} - \frac{y^{\star}_j-\mu_j}{\sigma_j}
  \;=\;
  \frac{\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j-y^{\star}_j}{\sigma_j}
  \;=\;
  z_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}).
  \label{eq:z_sd_equals_std_difference}
\end{equation}
Here $\widetilde{y}^{(k)}_j$ and $\widetilde{y}^{\star}_j$ are the standardized coordinates of the
candidate and target in objective component $j$, and the training mean cancels because the comparison
is centered at the target.

When the training outcomes are skewed or heavy-tailed, SD scaling can be dominated by rare extremes.
In such cases, $\sigma_j$ may be inflated by outliers, and SD-based residuals can understate mismatch
for typical targets. Robust scales provide a more stable measurement unit by focusing on the central
mass of the training distribution.

The MAD-based scale is appropriate when outliers are plausible but should not define the measurement
unit for typical deviation. Let $\mathrm{med}_j=\mathrm{median}_{i}\,y_{i,j}$ and define
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MAD}_j \;=\; \mathrm{median}_{i}\, \big|y_{i,j}-\mathrm{med}_j\big|.
  \label{eq:mad_def}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathrm{med}_j$ is the median of the $j$-th training objective column, and $\mathrm{MAD}_j$ is a
robust spread estimator.

To keep MAD-based units comparable to a standard deviation under a normal reference, use the fact that
for $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ the median of $|Z|$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{P}(|Z|\le c)=0.5
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  2\Phi(c)-1=0.5
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  c=\Phi^{-1}(0.75)\approx 0.67449,
  \label{eq:mad_normal_quantile}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function and $\Phi^{-1}(\cdot)$ is
its inverse.

Hence, for a normal reference distribution, $\mathrm{MAD}\approx \Phi^{-1}(0.75)\,\sigma$, and an
SD-comparable MAD scale is
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{\mathrm{MAD}}_j
  \;=\;
  \frac{\mathrm{MAD}_j}{\Phi^{-1}(0.75)}
  \;\approx\;
  1.4826\,\mathrm{MAD}_j.
  \label{eq:mad_scale}
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma^{\mathrm{MAD}}_j$ is used as $\tau_j$ in \eqref{eq:target_centered_residual} when the
objective is heavy-tailed or contaminated by outliers.

The IQR-based scale is appropriate when the training marginal is noticeably skewed and the mid-spread
captures typical variability more faithfully than squared deviations. Let $Q_{0.25,j}$ and $Q_{0.75,j}$
denote the 25th and 75th percentiles of $\{y_{i,j}\}_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{tr}}}$ and define
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IQR}_j \;=\; Q_{0.75,j}-Q_{0.25,j}.
  \label{eq:iqr_def}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathrm{IQR}_j$ measures the spread of the central 50\% of the training outcomes for objective $j$.

For a normal reference with $Y=\mu+\sigma Z$ and $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, quantiles satisfy
$Q_p(Y)=\mu+\sigma\Phi^{-1}(p)$, so
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{IQR}
  \;=\;
  \sigma\big(\Phi^{-1}(0.75)-\Phi^{-1}(0.25)\big)
  \;=\;
  2\sigma\,\Phi^{-1}(0.75)
  \;\approx\;
  1.349\,\sigma,
  \label{eq:iqr_normal_relation}
\end{equation}
using symmetry $\Phi^{-1}(0.25)=-\Phi^{-1}(0.75)$.

An SD-comparable IQR scale is therefore
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{\mathrm{IQR}}_j
  \;=\;
  \frac{\mathrm{IQR}_j}{2\,\Phi^{-1}(0.75)}
  \;\approx\;
  \frac{\mathrm{IQR}_j}{1.349}.
  \label{eq:iqr_scale}
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma^{\mathrm{IQR}}_j$ is used as $\tau_j$ in \eqref{eq:target_centered_residual} when the
objective distribution is skewed and a quantile-based spread is preferred.

Across SD, MAD, and IQR scaling, the same evaluation logic is preserved: the target-centered residual
vector in \eqref{eq:target_centered_residual_vector} expresses mismatch in unitless training-derived
units, scalar discrepancy scores are computed using \eqref{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid} or
\eqref{eq:discrepancy_residual_mahal}, and the same residual vectors are also the inputs to the
candidate-cloud diagnostics. Consequently, $b(\vect{y}^{\star})$ and $v(\vect{y}^{\star})$ measure
systematic offset and within-set spread in the standardized units induced by the selected scale
estimator.




%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Attainment under budget $K$}\label{sec:eval_discrepancy}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The set of scores $\{s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\}_{k=1}^{K}$ is summarized using two statistics.
The first is the minimum checked discrepancy within the candidate set,
\begin{equation}
  D_{\min}^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} \; s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \label{eq:Dmin}
\end{equation}
and the second is the median checked discrepancy across candidates,
\begin{equation}
  D_{\mathrm{med}}^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \mathrm{median}_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} \; s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}).
  \label{eq:Dmed}
\end{equation}
Optionally, the gap
\begin{equation}
  \Delta^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  D_{\mathrm{med}}^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) - D_{\min}^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \label{eq:Dgap}
\end{equation}
summarizes how strongly attainment relies on rare outliers rather than typical candidates.

$D_{\min}^{(K)}$ measures attainment under a finite candidate budget. It answers the direct question
posed by a candidate-based interface: after generating $K$ alternatives and checking them, how close
is the best candidate to the target in objective space? This statistic aligns with deployment-oriented
use, where a single decision is selected as the top-ranked candidate under the declared checker and
discrepancy. It also respects one-to-many structure by crediting methods that can produce at least one
high-quality alternative within the fixed budget.

On its own, $D_{\min}^{(K)}$ can be overly optimistic because it depends only on the best candidate and
ignores the remainder of the set. A method may achieve a small $D_{\min}^{(K)}$ through rare outliers
while producing many candidates that lie far from the target. $D_{\mathrm{med}}^{(K)}$ addresses this
limitation by describing typical candidate quality under the same query. A small $D_{\mathrm{med}}^{(K)}$
indicates that many candidates concentrate near the target, which is desirable when the candidate set
is used for exploration or when repeated querying is expected. A large $D_{\mathrm{med}}^{(K)}$ indicates
that most candidates are not well aligned, and that success may rely on occasional lucky draws.

\subsubsection{Candidate-cloud plausibility}\label{sec:eval_candidate_cloud}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

While the set-level summaries in Section~\ref{sec:eval_discrepancy} support model comparison under a
fixed candidate budget $K$, the objective-centered residuals also provide a direct plausibility view
for user-facing decision support. In particular, standardized residuals enable tolerance rules stated
in comparable units across objectives, such as $\max_j |z_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})| < 1.0$.

Beyond selecting a single design, the aggregate behavior of the checked outcomes
$\{ \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(1)}, \dots, \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(K)} \}$ provides a user-facing plausibility
signal. Working in the objective-centered $z$-space, the scatter of the candidate cloud around
$\vect{y}^{\star}$ indicates whether the model is both centered and precise, centered but diffuse, or
systematically offset.

For each target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, let
$\bar{\vect{z}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\vect{z}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$
denote the mean residual vector. We summarize offset and dispersion using
\begin{equation}
  b(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \frac{\lVert \bar{\vect{z}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}},
  \qquad
  v(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \mathrm{median}_{k}\;
  \frac{\big\lVert \vect{z}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})-\bar{\vect{z}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}},
  \label{eq:z_bias_var}
\end{equation}
where $b$ measures systematic offset from the target and $v$ measures within-set spread in standardized
units. These summaries support informed rejection: rather than guessing whether a raw distance is
acceptable, a user can apply consistent tolerances in standardized units and prioritize high-fidelity
verification when the candidate cloud is diffuse or biased.

offset from diffuse candidate clouds under the declared checker.

\subsection{Design-space uncertainty diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_design}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates whether at least one candidate can reproduce a target
outcome, but it does not reveal whether the inverse model represents the underlying
one-to-many structure in a trustworthy way. Because candidate-based inversion is intended
to act as a conditional sampler over decisions, we also evaluate uncertainty behaviour
directly in decision space. These diagnostics use held-out paired test data
$(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$: we condition on $\vect{y}_i$, generate $K$ decision candidates, and
compare the resulting sample distribution to the ground-truth decision $\vect{x}_i$.

We report two complementary perspectives that answer different questions.
First, reliability (calibration) asks whether the sampler’s stated uncertainty is
statistically consistent with the realized ground truth---informally, whether the model is
``honest'' about how uncertain it is. Second, probabilistic accuracy asks whether the
predictive distribution is practically useful, meaning that it concentrates near the truth
without becoming unnecessarily diffuse. Reporting both is important because a sampler can
be well calibrated yet uninformative (too wide), or sharp yet unreliable (overconfident).


\subsubsection{Reliability via PIT}\label{sec:eval_pit}

Reliability evaluates the uncertainty behaviour of the conditional candidate distribution
under repeated queries. The central question is whether the candidate set provides
meaningful percentiles: across many test cases, do values labelled as ``10th percentile''
actually contain the ground truth about 10\% of the time, ``50th percentile'' about 50\% of
the time, and so on. This is a distribution-level check, not a best-candidate accuracy
check. \cite{Diebold1998}

We assess reliability using the probability integral transform (PIT), applied component-wise
to the empirical conditional distribution represented by the $K$ candidates. For each test
case $i$ and decision dimension $d$, we compute the PIT value as the empirical percentile
rank of the ground-truth component $x_{i,d}$ among the sampled candidates:
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbf{1}\!\left(\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right)}_{\text{percentile rank of }x_{i,d}\text{ among }K\text{ samples}},
  \label{eq:pit_empirical}
\end{equation}
The quantity $z_{i,d}\in[0,1]$ is the empirical CDF of the $K$ sampled candidates evaluated at
the ground-truth value $x_{i,d}$. Equivalently, it is the percentile rank of $x_{i,d}$ among the
samples: for example, $z_{i,d}=0.1$ means roughly 10\% of sampled values lie below $x_{i,d}$.
If the conditional sampler is calibrated for component $d$, then these ranks aggregated over
test cases are approximately uniform on $[0,1]$. Systematic departures from uniformity therefore
diagnose distorted uncertainty under repeated conditioning. \cite{Diebold1998}


PIT is intentionally based on ranks rather than distances. It can therefore look acceptable
even when the samples are very wide, provided the truth lands at the right ranks often
enough across many test cases. For this reason we treat PIT as a reliability diagnostic,
not a measure of how informative the candidate distribution is.

Calibration is visualized using a PIT calibration curve constructed from the empirical CDF
of the aggregated PIT values. Let $\{z_{(\ell)}\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the sorted PIT values
aggregated over test cases and selected dimensions, where $L$ is the total number of PIT
values included. Plotting the empirical CDF of $\{z_{(\ell)}\}$ against the diagonal
$u\mapsto u$ provides a direct baseline for perfect calibration. This view is convenient in
practice because it summarizes miscalibration without relying on histogram bin choices and
it supports direct visual comparison across inverse models and sampling schemes. \cite{Diebold1998}

Systematic departures from the diagonal have interpretable signatures, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:pit_signatures}:

For compact reporting, we summarize deviation from the diagonal using a scalar calibration
error:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CalErr}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\left|z_{(\ell)}-\frac{\ell}{L}\right|.
  \label{eq:calerr}
\end{equation}
This quantity measures the average absolute deviation between the empirical PIT CDF and the
uniform reference CDF evaluated at the corresponding order statistics. Lower values indicate
more reliable uncertainty representation, which matters when candidate dispersion is used to
guide downstream exploration or decision-making. \cite{Diebold1998}


\subsubsection{Probabilistic accuracy via CRPS}\label{sec:eval_crps}

PIT-based reliability checks whether percentiles are meaningful, but it does not determine
whether the candidate distribution is useful for recovering decisions. In particular, a
sampler can be well calibrated while remaining overly diffuse, which makes candidate sets
costly to search and less informative for exploration. We therefore complement PIT with a
strictly proper scoring rule that rewards distributions that are close to the truth while
penalizing unnecessary spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}

We evaluate probabilistic accuracy using the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS).
In simple terms, CRPS asks the following in our setting:
did the candidate distribution put mass near the observed design value $x_{i,d}$, and did it
avoid spreading samples out more than needed? This targets usefulness rather than only
long-run percentile behavior. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007,Hersbach2000}

For each held-out test case $i$ and decision dimension $d$, CRPS compares the sampled
distribution to the ground truth $x_{i,d}$. In expectation form,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CRPS}_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-x_{i,d}|}_{\text{accuracy}}
  \;-\;
  \frac{1}{2}\,
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-X'|}_{\text{dispersion}},
  \label{eq:crps_expectation}
\end{equation}

where $X$ and $X'$ are independent draws from the predictive distribution represented by the
candidate samples. The first expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-x_{i,d}|\,]$, measures typical
distance to the ground truth and therefore captures accuracy: it is smaller when the sampled
distribution is centered near $x_{i,d}$. The second expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-X'|\,]$,
measures typical distance between samples and therefore captures dispersion:
it is larger when the predictive distribution is wide. Because this dispersion term is
subtracted (with factor $1/2$), a distribution that is unnecessarily diffuse increases the
overall CRPS. Low CRPS therefore indicates candidate sets that place mass near the ground
truth without inflating spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}



PIT and CRPS diagnose different failure modes. PIT can appear acceptable even when samples
are extremely wide, because it is driven by ranks. CRPS will penalize such diffuseness.
Conversely, a very sharp sampler can achieve good CRPS on easy cases while still being
systematically overconfident across the dataset, which PIT will expose through U-shaped
departures from the diagonal. For example, a model may achieve strong best-of-$K$ target matching yet be systematically overconfident (sharp but miscalibrated), or it may be reliable but too diffuse to support decisive exploration.
Reporting both diagnostics makes these trade-offs explicit and supports the interpretation of the comparative results \cite{Diebold1998,GneitingRaftery2007}

\subsection{Training pipeline and estimator selection}\label{sec:train_pipeline}

Model training follows a repeatable routine that keeps preprocessing and model selection
consistent across problems. Algorithm~\ref{alg:train_pipeline} summarizes the procedure.
We compute fixed normalization transforms, split the data for validation-based selection,
and train multiple estimator configurations under a fixed search budget.
Rather than selecting models purely by training loss, we select the final estimator using
design-space uncertainty diagnostics computed on held-out paired data:
PIT-based calibration (as a reliability check) and CRPS (as a usefulness check). The selected
estimator and preprocessing transforms are then frozen and used unchanged for candidate
generation at query time.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
  \caption{Training and estimator selection using PIT calibration and CRPS}\label{alg:train_pipeline}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Paired data $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$
    \Require Hyperparameter/model search space $\mathcal{H}$ and search budget $B$
    \Require Candidate budget $K$ for validation diagnostics
    \Ensure Selected inverse estimator $\widehat{q}$ and preprocessing transforms $\mathcal{T}$

    \State $\mathcal{T} \leftarrow \text{compute\_transforms}(\mathcal{D})$ \Comment{Scaling for $\vect{x}$ and $\vect{y}$}
    \State $\mathcal{D}_{T}, \mathcal{D}_{V} \leftarrow \text{split}(\mathcal{T}(\mathcal{D}))$ \Comment{Train/validation split}
    \State $(\mathrm{CRPS}_{\mathrm{best}},\mathrm{CalErr}_{\mathrm{best}}) \leftarrow (+\infty,+\infty)$

    \For{$b=1$ to $B$}
      \State Sample configuration $h_b \in \mathcal{H}$ 
      \State Initialize parameters $\theta_b$ according to $h_b$
      \While{stopping criterion not met}
        \State $\theta_b \leftarrow \text{minimize } \mathcal{L}(\theta_b; \mathcal{D}_{T})$
      \EndWhile

      \State Compute validation diagnostics on $\mathcal{D}_{V}$:
      \State \hspace{1em}Generate $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_{i}\}_{k=1}^{K} \sim \widehat{q}_{\theta_b}(\vect{x}\mid \vect{y}_i)$ for each $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\in\mathcal{D}_V$
      \State \hspace{1em}Estimate $\mathrm{CalErr}_b$ from aggregated PIT values
      \State \hspace{1em}Estimate $\mathrm{CRPS}_b$ from candidate samples

      \If{$(\mathrm{CRPS}_b,\mathrm{CalErr}_b)$ improves selection criterion}
        \State $(\mathrm{CRPS}_{\mathrm{best}},\mathrm{CalErr}_{\mathrm{best}}) \leftarrow (\mathrm{CRPS}_b,\mathrm{CalErr}_b)$
        \State $(h_{\mathrm{best}}, \theta_{\mathrm{best}}) \leftarrow (h_b, \theta_b)$
      \EndIf
    \EndFor

    \State $\widehat{q} \leftarrow \text{refit}(h_{\mathrm{best}}, \mathcal{D}_{T} \cup \mathcal{D}_{V})$
    \State \Return $\widehat{q}, \mathcal{T}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Querying pipeline}\label{sec:query_pipeline}

Given a user-specified target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the selected inverse estimator
generates a fixed-size candidate set by sampling from $\widehat{q}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^{\star})$.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:query_pipeline} summarizes this query-time procedure.
If an evaluator is available to compute $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ for each candidate, a
discrepancy score can be computed and used to rank candidates for presentation; otherwise,
the candidate set is returned without ranking.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
  \caption{Target-driven candidate generation (optional discrepancy-based ranking)}\label{alg:query_pipeline}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Trained inverse estimator $\widehat{q}$, preprocessing transforms $\mathcal{T}$
    \Require Target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$, candidate budget $K$
    \Require Optional evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d(\cdot,\cdot)$
    \Ensure Candidate set (optionally ranked) $\mathcal{R}$

    \State $\vect{y}^{\star} \leftarrow \mathcal{T}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ \Comment{Apply stored transforms}
    \State Generate candidates $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K} \sim \widehat{q}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^{\star})$

    \If{evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ is available}
      \For{$k=1$ to $K$}
        \State $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} \leftarrow \mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})$
        \State $S^{(k)} \leftarrow d(\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}, \vect{y}^{\star})$
      \EndFor
      \State $\mathcal{R} \leftarrow \text{RankCandidates}(\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}, \{S^{(k)}\})$
    \Else
      \State $\mathcal{R} \leftarrow \{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$
    \EndIf

    \State \Return $\mathcal{R}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
