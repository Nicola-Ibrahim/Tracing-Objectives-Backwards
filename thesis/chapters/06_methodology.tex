\chapter{Methodology}\label{chap:methodology}

Earlier chapters established inverse querying as a practical interface for multi-objective design:
a user specifies a target objective vector and expects actionable decision suggestions, despite
non-uniqueness and possible infeasibility. Building on that foundation, the present chapter
specifies the framework adopted to answer such queries and to evaluate inverse models under a
shared and reproducible protocol. The central aim is to make claims about attainment traceable to
a declared evaluation rule, rather than to implicit properties of a particular inverse model.


The methodology follows a model-based inverse design approach. An inverse model is
fit in a model synthesis phase from paired data and is then used during interactive
exploration to propose decision candidates; a separate evaluation stage checks and ranks
candidates in objective space using a stated outcome evaluator and a task-defined discrepancy.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Problem formulation}\label{sec:problem_formulation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inverse querying is formulated as learning a conditional model over decisions given objectives.
Let $\mathcal{X}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ denote the admissible decision space and
$\mathcal{Y}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}$ the objective space. A query specifies a target
$\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathcal{Y}$. The method returns a set of candidate decisions
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}\subset\mathcal{X}$ together with an ordering induced by
objective-space attainment. 

Building on the conceptual framework established in Equation~\eqref{eq:candidate_sampling}, the inverse component is represented as a parametric conditional distribution $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$. Utilizing the likelihood-based principle defined in Equation~\eqref{eq:prob_mle}, training fits parameters by minimizing the empirical negative log-likelihood (NLL) over the dataset:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\theta}\in\arg\min_{\theta}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\Big[-\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)\Big].
\label{eq:cond_mle_methodology}
\end{equation}
While Equation~\eqref{eq:prob_mle} defines the statistical goal of maximizing probability mass, the negative formulation in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} is mathematically equivalent and aligns the objective with standard gradient-based minimization protocols used in deep learning. This objective relies solely on the empirical distribution of the training data and does not require active calls to a forward operator during the model synthesis phase.

At query time, the method instantiates the proposal stage by drawing a fixed-size candidate set:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K.
  \label{eq:candidate_generation_methodology}
\end{equation}
To verify these proposals, we operationalize the scoring logic introduced in Equation~\eqref{eq:verify_score_bg}. Specifically, we assess attainment using an outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$ and a discrepancy function $d:\mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$. Each candidate $k$ receives a score:
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^\star) \;=\;
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^\star\big),
  \label{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}
\end{equation}
which induces a ranking over the set. In this framework, $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ is typically realized as a distance metric, such as the Euclidean ($L_2$) norm, or a task-specific weighted discrepancy. Fixing the triplet $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ defines the evaluation contract used throughout the thesis, ensuring that attainment claims are traceable to the explicit, shared rule established in the foundational theory.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Rationale and trade-offs}\label{sec:rationale_tradeoffs}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The adoption of a model-based, distributional approach is motivated by the inherent one-to-many 
nature of inverse decision mapping. By representing the inverse as $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$, 
the methodology explicitly acknowledges that multiple distinct decisions may be compatible with 
a single target. This avoids the risk of "mode averaging"—a common failure in deterministic 
regression where the model predicts the mean of multiple valid solutions, often resulting in 
an infeasible or non-physical design.



Furthermore, this design supports amortized inference. The computational burden of learning the 
inverse mapping is front-loaded into the synthesis phase. Once trained, the cost of answering 
new queries is reduced to simple sampling and evaluation, which is significantly faster than 
performing a full optimization search for every new target $\vect{y}^\star$. This aligns with 
established practices in simulation-based inference where efficient repeated conditioning is a 
primary requirement \cite{Papamakarios2021}.

A further motivation is comparability across benchmarks with heterogeneous forward access. Some
settings provide a trusted simulator, while others permit only surrogate-based evaluation. By 
defining attainment through a stated evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, the 
methodology keeps the success criterion stable while allowing the evaluation mechanism to reflect 
what a benchmark provides.

These choices introduce trade-offs that are important for interpretation. Performance depends 
heavily on the fidelity of the evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and the diversity of the training data. 
If targets $\vect{y}^\star$ fall outside the region supported by the training archive, the 
inverse model may propose candidates that appear plausible under $q_\theta$ but fail when checked 
against $\mathcal{E}$. Additionally, a fixed candidate budget $K$ represents a trade-off between 
computational cost and the probability of "hitting" a high-quality mode in strongly multimodal 
landscapes.

Query-time optimization remains complementary rather than central. While procedures such as 
Bayesian optimization can refine candidates, they introduce additional design choices that can 
confound fair comparisons between model families. Consequently, this thesis prioritizes 
direct candidate generation with explicit ranking as the primary baseline.

\section{Framework and protocol}\label{sec:framework}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes the workflow used throughout the thesis. The protocol follows
a propose--check structure: an inverse model proposes candidate decisions for a queried target, and a
separate checking stage evaluates and ranks those candidates in objective space under the declared
evaluator and discrepancy. The separation ensures that ambiguity is handled through generation, while
attainment is assessed by an explicit scoring rule.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \begin{adjustbox}{width=\linewidth}
      \input{figures/framework}
  \end{adjustbox}
  \caption[Propose--Check Workflow Framework]{Propose--check workflow used throughout the thesis. An inverse model generates a candidate set for a target objective vector, and an explicit evaluation stage scores and ranks candidates using a stated outcome evaluator.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

The workflow separates model synthesis from interactive exploration. In the model synthesis phase, the
inverse proposal model is fit from paired data using the conditional likelihood objective in
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. When a forward surrogate is used, it is learned and aligned with the
proposal mechanism so that proposal and checking share a consistent representation of the forward
relation. In the interactive exploration phase, a query $\vect{y}^{\star}$
is answered by sampling $K$ candidates from the conditional model, as in \eqref{eq:candidate_generation_methodology}.
Treating $K$ as fixed is part of the experimental interface and keeps the output format consistent
across model families. Each candidate is then checked by applying the evaluator and discrepancy in
\eqref{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}, after which candidates are filtered and ranked in objective space under
the declared rule. The evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ is selected to match the benchmark and deployment
context and may be a trusted simulator, a measurement routine, a surrogate used for checking, or a mapping reserved for evaluation. When a single decision is
required, the best-ranked candidate under the declared rule is selected.

Two practical implications follow from this protocol. Candidate generation reflects what is supported
by the training archive through $q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$, while attainment is
defined by the pair $(\mathcal{E},d)$. Recording $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ alongside model configuration
therefore makes results auditable and enables fair comparison across inverse model families.

\section{Normalization and Data Preparation}\label{sec:normalization}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We begin from a raw paired archive $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{raw}}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. Before fitting any models, we apply a deterministic preprocessing pipeline to ensure that training, candidate generation, and discrepancy-based scoring are performed in a consistent and numerically stable coordinate system. The paired archive is split into training, validation, and test subsets. To prevent information leakage, all preprocessing parameters are estimated using only the training split. We employ a feature-wise min-max scaling—referred to here as a hypercube normalization—which maps both decisions and outcomes into a specified range, typically $[0, 1]^D$. 

The choice of min-max normalization over alternative methods, such as Z-score standardization, is motivated by the specific requirements of the inverse modeling framework. First, it ensures metric fairness; since the evaluation contract relies on a Euclidean discrepancy $d$, it is essential that all objective dimensions span an identical numerical range. Min-max scaling ensures that a 10\% error in one objective is mathematically equivalent to a 10\% error in another, preventing the distance metric from being dominated by variables with naturally larger absolute magnitudes. Second, it promotes generative stability. Deep generative architectures, such as CVAEs and INNs, often utilize bounded activation functions like Sigmoid or Tanh. Mapping the decision space $\mathcal{X}$ to a unit hypercube aligns the target data with these activation ranges, improving gradient flow and preventing numerical instability during backpropagation \cite{Sola1997, Sarle1997}. Finally, bounding the data within $[0, 1]$ defines a clear feasible region for the conditional sampler, which is particularly beneficial for Mixture Density Networks when initializing variance parameters.



We denote the resulting fitted normalization maps by $T_x$ for decisions and $T_y$ for outcomes. Applying these maps yields the normalized pairs:
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{\vect{x}}_i = T_x(\vect{x}_i) = \frac{\vect{x}_i - \vect{x}_{\min}}{\vect{x}_{\max} - \vect{x}_{\min}},
  \qquad 
  \widetilde{\vect{y}}_i = T_y(\vect{y}_i) = \frac{\vect{y}_i - \vect{y}_{\min}}{\vect{y}_{\max} - \vect{y}_{\min}},
  \label{eq:normalization_maps}
\end{equation}
where the vectors $\min$ and $\max$ are determined feature-wise from the training data. For constant features where $\max = \min$, the range is set to unity to maintain numerical stability and avoid division-by-zero errors. 

During inference, model outputs are transformed back to their physical units using the inverse map $T_x^{-1}$. Similarly, the target query $\vect{y}^\star$ is transformed via $T_y$ before the discrepancy $d$ is computed. This ensures that the distance metric is not biased by differing physical units, allowing for a fair and unit-agnostic assessment of attainment across the entire objective space.

\section{Inverse-model training}\label{sec:training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

With the propose--check protocol fixed in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, implementation begins by
training the inverse proposal model from paired decision--objective examples
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. The training goal is to obtain a conditional
generator $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ that captures the empirical decision--objective relation
while remaining expressive enough to represent one-to-many structure. The modeling background in
Chapter~\ref{chap:Background} introduces the three conditional families used in the thesis and
derives their probabilistic forms. The present section specifies the optimization objectives as used
in the experiments.

Across models, parameter estimation follows a shared principle: fit the conditional distribution to
the observed pairs by minimizing the negative log-likelihood as defined in 
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. The evaluation components, namely the outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ 
and discrepancy $d$, are not used during training and enter only through the checking rule defined 
by \eqref{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}.

The three instantiations below differ in how they parameterize $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ and
how the conditional log-density in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} is computed or approximated.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mixture density network}\label{subsec:mdn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The mixture density network represents the conditional density as a finite Gaussian mixture with
$\vect{y}$-dependent parameters,
\begin{equation}
  q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\,
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x};\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y}),\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\big),
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})=1,\ \ \pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\ge 0 .
  \label{eq:mdn_density}
\end{equation}
Training substitutes \eqref{eq:mdn_density} into the objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} and
optimises the resulting mixture negative log-likelihood. The implementation enforces the standard
constraints on mixture weights and uses a stable parameterisation of covariance matrices, consistent
with the model definition in Chapter~\ref{chap:Background}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conditional variational autoencoder}\label{subsec:cvae_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The conditional variational autoencoder introduces latent variables to represent conditional
variability. As direct maximisation of the conditional likelihood is generally intractable, 
the model is trained by minimising the negative evidence lower bound (ELBO), which serves as 
a tractable surrogate for the objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. Utilizing an 
encoder $q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$ and a decoder $p_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})$, 
the training objective is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta},\widehat{\phi}\in\arg\min_{\theta,\phi}\;
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Big[
    - \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)} \big[ \log p_{\theta}(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z}) \big]
    + \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\,\|\,p(\vect{z})\big)
  \Big].
  \label{eq:cvae_elbo_methodology}
\end{equation}
The first term represents the conditional reconstruction loss, ensuring the decoder can 
recover the decision $\vect{x}$ given the correct latent code, while the Kullback--Leibler 
divergence term regularises the latent space against a prior $p(\vect{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. 
At query time, the method draws $\vect{z} \sim p(\vect{z})$ to generate diverse candidates for 
a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, consistent with the candidate budget $K$ used in 
the evaluation protocol.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invertible neural network}\label{subsec:inn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The invertible neural network defines a conditional density through an invertible 
transformation between a simple reference latent distribution and the decision space. 
Using a conditional invertible map $T_{\theta}(\cdot;\vect{y})$ and its inverse 
$T_{\theta}^{-1}(\cdot;\vect{y})$, the relationship is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x} = T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}),\qquad \vect{z} = T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y}),
  \label{eq:inn_forward_map_methodology}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{z}$ follows a standard normal prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$. The 
conditional log-density for any pair $(\vect{x}, \vect{y})$ follows exactly from the 
change-of-variables formula:
\begin{equation}
  \log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \log p(\vect{z}) +
  \log\left|\det \frac{\partial T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y})}{\partial \vect{x}}\right|.
  \label{eq:inn_change_of_vars_methodology}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:inn_change_of_vars_methodology} into the negative log-likelihood 
objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} allows for exact likelihood-based training 
without variational approximations. Sampling is performed by drawing $K$ latent vectors 
from the prior and applying the forward pass $T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}^{\star})$. This 
bijective structure ensures that every point in the latent reservoir corresponds to a unique 
candidate, integrating naturally with the propose--check protocol.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Evaluation plan}\label{sec:evaluation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Evaluation follows the propose--check contract defined in Section~\ref{sec:framework}. For each target
$\vect{y}^{\star}$, an inverse model produces $K$ decision candidates
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. A fixed forward checker computes the corresponding checked
objective vectors,
\[
  \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} \;=\; \mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}), \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
\]
where $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}$ denotes the best available evaluator for the
benchmark (simulator, measurement routine, or checking surrogate). All reported metrics are derived
from checked quantities, and $\mathcal{E}$ is not accessed by inverse models at test time beyond
producing candidates. Fixing $\mathcal{E}$, the discrepancy definition $d$, and the candidate budget
$K$ keeps comparisons attributable to inverse modeling choices rather than changes in the evaluation
interface.

Two complementary evaluation views are reported. The first view evaluates the user-facing inverse
querying interface by measuring attainment in objective space under the checker. The second view
evaluates the inverse model as a conditional sampler in design space, focusing on uncertainty
behavior. The separation is deliberate: objective-space metrics quantify whether a target can be
matched under the declared checker, while design-space diagnostics quantify whether sampled candidates
behave like a meaningful conditional distribution over decisions.


%%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Objective-space plausibility diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_objective}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates how well a checked candidate set can match a requested target.
Given checked outcomes $\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ for a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the
evaluation proceeds in the normalized objective coordinate system defined by the preprocessing maps in
Section~\ref{sec:normalization}. This section keeps the objective-space contract explicit: we diagnose
component-wise mismatch relative to the target, aggregate mismatch into a scalar discrepancy for
ranking, and interpret the resulting candidate cloud in the same normalized units across all targets.
Throughout this section, $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ are understood to be
expressed in the normalized objective coordinates defined in Section~\ref{sec:normalization}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Target-centered residuals}\label{sec:eval_plausibility_z}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For a user target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ and the checked outcome of candidate $k$,
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$, define the target-centered residual in objective
component $j$ as
\begin{equation}
  r_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \widehat{y}^{(k)}_j - y^{\star}_j,
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual}
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j$ is the $j$-th objective value of candidate $k$ after checking and $y^{\star}_j$
is the $j$-th component of the target. Because objectives are evaluated in the normalized coordinate
system, $r_j^{(k)}$ is already expressed in comparable units across objectives: its magnitude can be
read directly as a fraction of the normalized objective range.

Collect components into the residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big(r_1^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),\dots,r_m^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{m}.
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual_vector}
\end{equation}
Each coordinate of $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ corresponds to one objective dimension, so the
vector provides a transparent component-wise diagnostic prior to aggregation. In particular, the sign
of $r_j^{(k)}$ indicates whether candidate $k$ lies above or below the target in objective $j$, while
the magnitude indicates how far it is from the target in the normalized coordinate system.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Discrepancy contract}\label{sec:eval_discrepancy_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Given residual vectors $\{\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\}_{k=1}^{K}$, a discrepancy $d$ aggregates
target-centered mismatch into a scalar that supports ranking and downstream summaries. The default uses
Euclidean aggregation in residual space, based on the $\ell_2$ norm:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2,
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid}
\end{equation}
where $\lVert \cdot \rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm and $K$ is the candidate budget. The score
$s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ is a single nonnegative scalar used to rank candidates for the same target.

When objective dimensions are strongly correlated, Euclidean aggregation can effectively count the same
direction multiple times. In that setting, a Mahalanobis discrepancy incorporates covariance
structure, \cite{Mahalanobis1936,DeMaesschalck2000}
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{Mah}}(\vect{u},\vect{v})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{(\vect{u}-\vect{v})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\vect{u}-\vect{v})},
  \label{eq:mahalanobis_distance}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{u},\vect{v}\in\mathbb{R}^m$ are objective vectors in a common normalized coordinate system
and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ is an estimated covariance matrix on a declared
reference split of checked outcomes (or residuals). We estimate $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ on a declared
reference split using the pooled collection of residual vectors $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$
across targets and candidates in that split. The inverse $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}$ is computed after
regularization to ensure stable inversion when $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is ill-conditioned.

Applied to the target-centered residual vector, the corresponding candidate score is
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)^{\top}
  \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)}.
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_mahal}
\end{equation}
Here $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\in\mathbb{R}^m$ is the residual vector of candidate $k$ relative
to the target. The quadratic form weights directions by the inverse covariance so that correlated
objective directions contribute less to the score than independent directions.

The checker $\mathcal{E}$, the stored normalization maps from Section~\ref{sec:normalization}, and the
declared discrepancy $d$ together define the objective-space evaluation contract. Under this contract,
each candidate $\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}$ is checked, compared to the same target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ in
the same normalized units, and ranked by the same externally stated scoring rule:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  d\!\big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),\,\vect{0}\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K.
  \label{eq:per_candidate_discrepancy}
\end{equation}
Here $\vect{0}\in\mathbb{R}^m$ denotes the zero vector, so the discrepancy is evaluated as a distance
from the residual vector to the origin in target-centered residual space.

When a single decision is required, the best-shot candidate under the declared rule is
\begin{equation}
  k^{\ast}
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \qquad
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{\ast}=\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k^{\ast})}.
  \label{eq:best_shot_def}
\end{equation}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Candidate-cloud plausibility}\label{sec:eval_candidate_cloud}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Scalar discrepancy scores $s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ support ranking within a candidate set, but they
do not by themselves describe how the full set behaves around the requested target. For user-facing
decision support, the aggregate geometry of the checked outcomes
$\{ \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(1)}, \dots, \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(K)} \}$ matters because it indicates whether
the inverse sampler produces a concentrated, target-centered cloud or a diffuse or systematically
shifted set of outcomes.

We therefore evaluate candidate-set behavior in the same target-centered residual space used for
ranking. Let $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ denote the residual vector defined in
\eqref{eq:target_centered_residual_vector}. In this thesis, $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ are expressed in the normalized objective coordinates defined in
Section~\ref{sec:normalization}, so residual magnitudes are directly comparable across objective
dimensions.

For a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, define the within-set mean residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \label{eq:mean_residual_vector_cloud}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the candidate budget.

Candidate-cloud plausibility is summarized using two set-level statistics: systematic offset of the
cloud from the target and within-set dispersion around the cloud mean,
\begin{equation}
  b(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{\lVert \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}},
  \qquad
  v(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \mathrm{median}_{k}\;
  \frac{\big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})-\bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}}.
  \label{eq:residual_bias_dispersion}
\end{equation}
Here $m$ is the number of objectives, $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm, $b$ is the
magnitude of the mean residual (cloud bias) in average per-objective units, and $v$ is a robust
within-set dispersion summary computed as the median distance of residuals to the cloud mean, again
reported per objective via the $\sqrt{m}$ normalization.

The pair $(b,v)$ separates failure modes that are conflated by a single scalar discrepancy: low $b$
with high $v$ indicates a cloud that is centered but diffuse, while high $b$ with low $v$ indicates a
cloud that is concentrated but systematically shifted away from the target. This distinction supports
interpretation of objective-space behavior under a fixed sampling budget $K$.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Design-space uncertainty diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_design}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates whether at least one candidate can reproduce a target
outcome, but it does not reveal whether the inverse model represents the underlying
one-to-many structure in a trustworthy way. Because candidate-based inversion is intended
to act as a conditional sampler over decisions, we also evaluate uncertainty behaviour
directly in decision space. These diagnostics use held-out paired test data
$(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$: we condition on $\vect{y}_i$, generate $K$ decision candidates, and
compare the resulting sample distribution to the ground-truth decision $\vect{x}_i$.

We report two complementary perspectives that answer different questions.
First, reliability (calibration) asks whether the sampler’s stated uncertainty is
statistically consistent with the realized ground truth---informally, whether the model is
``honest'' about how uncertain it is. Second, probabilistic accuracy asks whether the
predictive distribution is practically useful, meaning that it concentrates near the truth
without becoming unnecessarily diffuse. Reporting both is important because a sampler can
be well calibrated yet uninformative (too wide), or sharp yet unreliable (overconfident).


\subsubsection{Reliability via PIT}\label{sec:eval_pit}

Reliability evaluates the uncertainty behaviour of the conditional candidate distribution
under repeated queries. The central question is whether the candidate set provides
meaningful percentiles: across many test cases, do values labelled as ``10th percentile''
actually contain the ground truth about 10\% of the time, ``50th percentile'' about 50\% of
the time, and so on. This is a distribution-level check, not a best-candidate accuracy
check. \cite{Diebold1998}

We assess reliability using the probability integral transform (PIT), applied component-wise
to the empirical conditional distribution represented by the $K$ candidates. For each test
case $i$ and decision dimension $d$, we compute the PIT value as the empirical percentile
rank of the ground-truth component $x_{i,d}$ among the sampled candidates:
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbf{1}\!\left(\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right)}_{\text{percentile rank of }x_{i,d}\text{ among }K\text{ samples}},
  \label{eq:pit_empirical}
\end{equation}
The quantity $z_{i,d}\in[0,1]$ is the empirical CDF of the $K$ sampled candidates evaluated at
the ground-truth value $x_{i,d}$. Equivalently, it is the percentile rank of $x_{i,d}$ among the
samples: for example, $z_{i,d}=0.1$ means roughly 10\% of sampled values lie below $x_{i,d}$.
If the conditional sampler is calibrated for component $d$, then these ranks aggregated over
test cases are approximately uniform on $[0,1]$. Systematic departures from uniformity therefore
diagnose distorted uncertainty under repeated conditioning. 


PIT is intentionally based on ranks rather than distances. It can therefore look acceptable
even when the samples are very wide, provided the truth lands at the right ranks often
enough across many test cases. For this reason we treat PIT as a reliability diagnostic,
not a measure of how informative the candidate distribution is.

Calibration is visualized using a PIT calibration curve constructed from the empirical CDF
of the aggregated PIT values. Let $\{z_{\ell}\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the pooled PIT values aggregated over test cases and
selected dimensions, where $L$ is the total number of PIT values included. Plotting the empirical CDF of $\{z_{(\ell)}\}$ against the diagonal
$u\mapsto u$ provides a direct baseline for perfect calibration. This view is convenient in
practice because it summarizes miscalibration without relying on histogram bin choices and
it supports direct visual comparison across inverse models and sampling schemes. \cite{dawid1984statistical}


Systematic departures from the diagonal have interpretable signatures, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:pit_signatures} and Figure~\ref{fig:pit_signatures}. These signatures are fundamentally driven by the relationship between the PIT histogram's density and the slope of the calibration curve (the CDF). Because the calibration curve is the cumulative integral of the PIT values, its slope at any point is directly proportional to the height of the histogram at that specific percentile:

\begin{itemize}
    \item High Histogram Density (Peaks): When many ground truth observations fall within a specific percentile range, the calibration curve "sprints" or rises rapidly. For instance, a peak at $PIT \approx 0$ causes the curve to shoot above the diagonal immediately.
    \item Low Histogram Density (Valleys): When few observations fall within a range, the calibration curve "crawls" or flattens out. If the histogram is empty near $PIT \approx 0$, the curve lags, falling below the diagonal.
\end{itemize}

For compact reporting, we summarize deviation from the diagonal using the mean absolute
calibration error (MACE). Let $\{z_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the pooled PIT values aggregated
over the selected test cases and decision dimensions. Define the empirical CDF of the aggregated
PIT values as
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{G}(\alpha)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathbf{1}\!\left(z_\ell \le \alpha\right),
  \qquad \alpha\in[0,1].
  \label{eq:pit_ecdf}
\end{equation}
We then compute MACE as the average absolute deviation of this curve from the uniform reference
CDF over a fixed grid:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MACE}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\big|\widehat{G}(\alpha_j)-\alpha_j\big|,
  \label{eq:mace}
\end{equation}
where $\{\alpha_j\}_{j=1}^{J}\subset[0,1]$ is a fixed grid (e.g., equally spaced). This quantity measures the
average absolute deviation between the empirical PIT CDF and the uniform reference CDF across
quantile levels. Lower values indicate more reliable uncertainty representation, which matters
when candidate dispersion is used to guide downstream exploration or decision-making.



\subsubsection{Probabilistic accuracy via CRPS}\label{sec:eval_crps}

PIT-based reliability checks whether percentiles are meaningful, but it does not determine
whether the candidate distribution is useful for recovering decisions. In particular, a
sampler can be well calibrated while remaining overly diffuse, which makes candidate sets
costly to search and less informative for exploration. We therefore complement PIT with a
strictly proper scoring rule that rewards distributions that are close to the truth while
penalizing unnecessary spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}

We evaluate probabilistic accuracy using the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS).
In simple terms, CRPS asks the following in our setting:
did the candidate distribution put mass near the observed design value $x_{i,d}$, and did it
avoid spreading samples out more than needed? This targets usefulness rather than only
long-run percentile behavior. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007,Hersbach2000}

For each held-out test case $i$ and decision dimension $d$, CRPS compares the sampled
distribution to the ground truth $x_{i,d}$. In expectation form,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CRPS}_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-x_{i,d}|}_{\text{accuracy}}
  \;-\;
  \frac{1}{2}\,
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-X'|}_{\text{dispersion}},
  \label{eq:crps_expectation}
\end{equation}

where $X$ and $X'$ are independent draws from the predictive distribution represented by the
candidate samples. The first expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-x_{i,d}|\,]$, measures typical
distance to the ground truth and therefore captures accuracy: it is smaller when the sampled
distribution is centered near $x_{i,d}$. The second expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-X'|\,]$,
measures typical distance between samples and therefore captures dispersion:
it is larger when the predictive distribution is wide. Because this dispersion term is
subtracted (with factor $1/2$), a distribution that is unnecessarily diffuse increases the
overall CRPS. Low CRPS therefore indicates candidate sets that place mass near the ground
truth without inflating spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}



PIT and CRPS diagnose different failure modes. PIT can appear acceptable even when samples
are extremely wide, because it is driven by ranks. CRPS will penalize such diffuseness.
Conversely, a very sharp sampler can achieve good CRPS on easy cases while still being
systematically overconfident across the dataset, which PIT will expose through U-shaped
departures from the diagonal. For example, a model may achieve strong best-of-$K$ target matching yet be systematically overconfident (sharp but miscalibrated), or it may be reliable but too diffuse to support decisive exploration.
Reporting both diagnostics makes these trade-offs explicit and supports the interpretation of the comparative results \cite{Diebold1998}
