\chapter{Methodology}\label{chap:methodology}

Earlier chapters established inverse querying as a practical interface for multi-objective design:
a user specifies a target objective vector and expects actionable decision suggestions, despite
non-uniqueness and possible infeasibility. Building on that foundation, the present chapter
specifies the framework adopted to answer such queries and to evaluate inverse models under a
shared and reproducible protocol. The central aim is to make claims about attainment traceable to
a declared evaluation rule, rather than to implicit properties of a particular inverse model.


The methodology follows a model-based inverse design approach. An inverse model is trained offline
from paired data and is used at query time to propose decision candidates; a separate evaluation
stage then checks and ranks candidates in objective space using a stated outcome evaluator and a
task-defined discrepancy. Three commitments are held constant throughout
the empirical study. The method returns a candidate set rather than a single decision, attainment is
defined in objective space through an explicit evaluator and discrepancy, and comparisons use a fixed
candidate budget and a consistent reporting rule.

\section{Problem formulation}\label{sec:problem_formulation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inverse querying is formulated as learning a conditional model over decisions given objectives.
Let $\mathcal{X}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ denote the admissible decision space and
$\mathcal{Y}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}$ the objective space. A query specifies a target
$\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathcal{Y}$. The method returns a set of candidate decisions
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}\subset\mathcal{X}$ together with an ordering induced by
objective-space attainment. The output therefore consists of alternatives and a declared basis for
preferring one alternative over another.

The inverse component is represented as a parametric conditional distribution
$q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$. A distributional inverse can represent multi-modality and is
therefore suitable for one-to-many inverse structure, which is expected when multiple decisions
produce similar objective outcomes under the task discrepancy. 

Training uses paired observations $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ and fits
parameters by regularized conditional maximum likelihood,
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\theta}\in\arg\min_{\theta}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\Big[-\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)\Big] + \lambda\,\Omega(\theta),
\label{eq:cond_mle_inverse}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(\theta)$ is a regularization term and $\lambda\ge 0$ controls its contribution. The
objective depends only on paired data and does not require an analytic forward operator during
learning, which keeps the approach applicable when forward evaluation is available only for
assessment. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020}

At query time, the method draws a fixed-size candidate set,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_generation}
\end{equation}
and assesses attainment in objective space using an outcome evaluator
$\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$ together with a task-defined discrepancy $d(\cdot,\cdot)$.
\cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020} Candidate $k$ receives score
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^\star) \;=\;
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^\star\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_scores}
\end{equation}
which induces a ranking over candidates. Fixing $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ defines the evaluation contract
used throughout the thesis and makes attainment claims traceable to an explicit rule that is shared
across inverse model families. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020}

\section{Rationale and trade-offs}\label{sec:rationale_tradeoffs}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Model-based inverse design is adopted because it aligns with the constraints of inverse querying in
multi-objective settings. Non-uniqueness is a typical feature of the inverse relation, and a method
that returns a single decision can conceal ambiguity and cause comparisons to depend on model-specific
selection behavior. A conditional model addresses this directly by supporting multiple candidates
per target and by moving selection to an explicit objective-space rule. 

The methodology is also selected to support repeated use. Offline training amortizes the cost of
inversion, so query-time computation reduces to sampling under a fixed candidate budget and evaluating
candidates under the declared contract in Section~\ref{sec:problem_formulation}. This design follows
established practice in amortized and simulation-based inference, where conditional density estimation
enables efficient repeated conditioning queries once training is complete. \cite{Papamakarios2021,Cranmer2020SBI}

A further motivation is comparability across benchmarks with heterogeneous forward access. Some
settings provide a trusted simulator, while others permit only surrogate-based or recorded-outcome
evaluation. By defining attainment through a stated evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, the
methodology keeps the success criterion stable while allowing the evaluation mechanism to reflect what
a benchmark provides. 

These choices introduce trade-offs that are important for interpretation. Performance depends on the
quality and fidelity of the evaluator used for checking, and a fixed candidate budget $K$ limits the
probability of identifying a high-quality candidate in strongly multi-modal settings. In addition, if
targets fall outside the region supported by the training archive, the inverse model may propose
candidates that are plausible under the learned distribution but attain the target poorly under the
evaluator. These limitations motivate the controlled evaluation protocol and the reporting strategy
used in the experimental chapter. 

Query-time optimization remains complementary rather than central. When evaluations are reliable and
affordable, black-box refinement procedures such as Bayesian optimization can improve a candidate, but
they introduce additional design choices that complicate fair comparison, including acquisition design,
initialization, and stopping criteria. For this reason, the baseline in
this thesis prioritizes amortized candidate generation with explicit checking and ranking, and treats
optimization-based refinement as an optional extension.

\section{Framework and protocol}\label{sec:framework}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes the workflow used throughout the thesis. The protocol follows
a propose--check structure: an inverse model proposes candidate decisions for a queried target, and a
separate checking stage evaluates and ranks those candidates in objective space under the declared
evaluator and discrepancy. The separation ensures that ambiguity is handled through generation, while
attainment is assessed by an explicit scoring rule.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \begin{adjustbox}{width=\linewidth}
      \input{figures/framework}
  \end{adjustbox}
  \caption[Propose--Check Workflow Framework]{Propose--check workflow used throughout the thesis. An inverse model generates a candidate set for a target objective vector, and an explicit evaluation stage scores and ranks candidates using a stated outcome evaluator and discrepancy.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

The workflow is executed in four stages. First, the inverse proposal model is trained offline from
paired data using the conditional likelihood objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. Second, a query
$\vect{y}^{\star}$ is answered by sampling $K$ candidates from the trained conditional model, as in
\eqref{eq:candidate_generation}. Treating $K$ as fixed is part of the experimental interface and keeps
the output format consistent across model families. Third, each candidate is checked by applying the
evaluator and discrepancy in \eqref{eq:candidate_scores}. The evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ is selected to
match the benchmark and deployment context and may be a simulator, a measurement routine, a surrogate
used for checking, or an offline mapping reserved for evaluation. \cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020} Fourth,
the method returns candidates ordered by their objective-space scores; when a single decision is
required, the best-ranked candidate under the declared rule is selected.

Two practical implications follow from this protocol. Candidate generation reflects what is supported
by the training archive through $q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$, while attainment is
defined by the pair $(\mathcal{E},d)$. Recording $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ alongside model configuration
therefore makes results auditable and enables fair comparison across inverse model families.

\section{Inverse-model training}\label{sec:training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

With the propose--check protocol fixed in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, implementation begins by
training the inverse proposal model from paired decision--objective examples
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. The training goal is to obtain a conditional
generator $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ that captures the empirical decision--objective relation
while remaining expressive enough to represent one-to-many structure. The modelling background in
Chapter~\ref{chap:Background} introduces the three conditional families used in the thesis and
derives their probabilistic forms. The present section specifies the optimisation objectives as used
in the experiments.

Across models, parameter estimation follows a shared principle: fit the conditional distribution to
the observed pairs under the regularised conditional likelihood objective in
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. The role of regularisation is collected in $\Omega(\cdot)$ and includes
standard stabilising terms such as weight decay, together with any model-specific penalties. The
evaluation components, namely the outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, are not used
during training and enter only through the checking rule defined by \eqref{eq:candidate_scores}.

The three instantiations below differ in how they parameterise $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ and
how the conditional log-density in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} is computed or approximated.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mixture density network}\label{subsec:mdn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The mixture density network represents the conditional density as a finite Gaussian mixture with
$\vect{y}$-dependent parameters,
\begin{equation}
  q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\,
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x};\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y}),\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\big),
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})=1,\ \ \pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\ge 0 .
  \label{eq:mdn_density}
\end{equation}
Training substitutes \eqref{eq:mdn_density} into the objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} and
optimises the resulting mixture negative log-likelihood. The implementation enforces the standard
constraints on mixture weights and uses a stable parameterisation of covariance matrices, consistent
with the model definition in Chapter~\ref{chap:Background}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conditional variational autoencoder}\label{subsec:cvae_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The conditional variational autoencoder introduces latent variables to represent conditional
variability. Direct maximisation of $\log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ is replaced by maximising
a variational lower bound using an encoder $q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$ and decoder
$p_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})$. The training objective is the negative evidence lower
bound plus regularisation,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta},\widehat{\phi}\in\arg\min_{\theta,\phi}\;
  -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Big[
    \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)}\log p_{\theta}(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z})
    - \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\,\|\,p(\vect{z})\big)
  \Big]
  +\lambda\,\Omega(\theta,\phi),
  \label{eq:cvae_elbo}
\end{equation}
which provides a tractable surrogate for conditional likelihood training. At query time, latent
variation allows the decoder to generate diverse candidates for a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$,
consistent with the fixed candidate budget used in evaluation.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invertible neural network}\label{subsec:inn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The invertible neural network defines a conditional density through an invertible transformation
between a simple reference latent distribution and the decision, conditioned on the objective
vector. Using a conditional invertible map $T_{\theta}(\cdot;\vect{y})$,
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x} = T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}),\qquad \vect{z}\sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}),
  \label{eq:inn_forward_map}
\end{equation}
the conditional log-density follows from a change-of-variables construction,
\begin{equation}
  \log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \log p(\vect{z}) +
  \log\left|\det \frac{\partial T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y})}{\partial \vect{x}}\right|,
  \qquad
  \vect{z}=T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y}).
  \label{eq:inn_change_of_vars}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:inn_change_of_vars} into \eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse} yields a direct
likelihood-based objective. Sampling is obtained by drawing $\vect{z}$ from the reference prior and
applying $T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}^{\star})$, which integrates naturally with the fixed candidate
budget used in the propose--check protocol.

In summary, the three model families share the same training goal, namely a conditional generator
$q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ fit from paired data under the objective in
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_inverse}. They differ in how conditional variability is represented, through
mixture components in MDNs, latent-variable decoding in CVAEs, or invertible latent transport in
INNs. Downstream evaluation treats sampled candidates uniformly via the shared checking rule in
\eqref{eq:candidate_scores}, so empirical comparisons reflect differences in the learned conditional
model rather than differences in the evaluation interface.

\section{Evaluation plan}\label{sec:evaluation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The inverse models in this thesis return a candidate set for each target rather than a single point estimate. Evaluation therefore answers a concrete question: given a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and candidates $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$, which candidates most closely satisfy the objective under a stated checking mechanism, and how often does at least one candidate meet a specification-level requirement. This section fixes that checking mechanism so that results remain comparable across model families and datasets.

Evaluation is performed through an outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$, which represents the best available way to assess outcomes for proposed decisions. Depending on the setting, $\mathcal{E}$ may be a trusted forward simulator, an experimental measurement routine, or a surrogate model used purely for checking when an explicit forward model is not available. 

\subsection{Outcome-space checking and ranking}\label{sec:eval_checking}

For each candidate, we compute an outcome-space discrepancy score relative to the user target:
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^{\star}\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_scoring}
\end{equation}
and rank candidates by increasing $r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$. This score is the main evaluation primitive: it is the mechanism by which the forward model or surrogate contributes to validation, and it is the basis for all reported attainment metrics.

To summarise performance in a way that respects the one-to-many nature of the inverse mapping, we report the best-achieved discrepancy within the candidate set:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{E}_{\mathcal{Y}}^{(K)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}}
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^{\star}\big).
  \label{eq:best_of_k_error}
\end{equation}
When qualitative exploration is the goal, the ranked list itself is retained; when a single deployed decision is required, it is chosen as the top-ranked candidate under \eqref{eq:candidate_scoring}. Using $\min_k$ as the reporting convention keeps comparisons consistent while still crediting methods that generate useful diversity.

The discrepancy $d$ is selected to match the outcome semantics and scaling. After standardising outcomes, Euclidean distance provides a transparent default. When outcome dimensions are correlated or have heterogeneous noise, a Mahalanobis discrepancy incorporates covariance structure:
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{Mah}}(\vect{u},\vect{v})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{(\vect{u}-\vect{v})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\vect{u}-\vect{v})},
  \label{eq:mahalanobis_distance}
\end{equation}
with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ estimated from a reference split of outcomes. \cite{Bishop2006PRML}

\subsection{Tolerance-based attainment}\label{sec:eval_tolerance}

Outcome-space distances are informative, but many applications ultimately require a specification-level statement: whether the method can produce at least one candidate within an acceptable tolerance. Using the same discrepancy as in \eqref{eq:candidate_scoring}, we report
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Succ}^{(K)}_{\epsilon}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{I}\!\left[\,
    \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}}
    d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^{\star}\big)
    \le \epsilon
  \right],
  \label{eq:succ_eps_bestofk}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is set by domain tolerances or by a declared experimental specification. Reporting both $\mathcal{E}_{\mathcal{Y}}^{(K)}$ and $\mathrm{Succ}^{(K)}_{\epsilon}$ separates continuous closeness from compliance with a target specification.

\subsection{Operational-domain validator (ODD) for constraint-aware evaluation}\label{sec:eval_odd}

Discrepancy alone does not capture all evaluation needs. In practice, users often impose additional constraints beyond matching $\vect{y}^{\star}$, such as admissible operating regions, safety limits, or validity requirements tied to the training distribution. To make these requirements explicit, we include an operational-domain validator, denoted ODD, that acts as an additional evaluation mechanism.

The ODD produces an in-domain score or acceptance decision for a candidate outcome (and, if needed, for a candidate decision):
\begin{equation}
  s_{\psi}^{(k)} \;=\; \mathrm{ODD}_{\psi}\!\big(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)},\,\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:odd_score}
\end{equation}
where larger $s_{\psi}^{(k)}$ indicates better conformity to the operational domain. In machine-learning terms, this corresponds to out-of-distribution or novelty detection: the validator is trained beforehand to capture the data distribution (and any user-declared admissibility structure), so that candidates outside the supported regime can be flagged or rejected. \cite{Lu2025OODSurvey}

In evaluation, we combine target attainment and operational-domain validity by filtering or re-ranking. A simple filtering rule keeps only candidates that satisfy a validator threshold $\tau$ and any explicit user constraints encoded as a predicate $c(\cdot)\le 0$ in outcome space:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{valid}}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \Big\{\,k:\ s_{\psi}^{(k)}\ge\tau\ \ \text{and}\ \ c\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})\big)\le 0 \Big\}.
  \label{eq:valid_set}
\end{equation}
Ranking is then applied within $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{valid}}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ using the same discrepancy score \eqref{eq:candidate_scoring}. If no candidate passes the validator, the method reports this explicitly and the query is treated as not attained under the declared operational constraints. This makes failure modes interpretable: the method may fail because candidates do not match the target, because they violate user constraints, or because they fall outside the learned operational domain.

The threshold $\tau$ is selected in advance on held-out data to control the strictness of the validator under the training regime. This keeps ODD decisions reproducible and prevents post hoc threshold tuning that would compromise comparability across experiments. The exact ODD model class used (e.g., one-class classification, energy-based scoring, or density-based scoring) is treated as part of the evaluation configuration and is reported alongside the discrepancy definition. \cite{Liu2020EnergyOOD}

\section{Pipelines}\label{sec:pipelines}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Training pipeline}\label{sec:train_pipeline}

Model training follows a repeatable routine that keeps preprocessing and model selection consistent
across problems. Algorithm~\ref{alg:train_pipeline} provides a compact description. In short, we
compute fixed normalisation transforms, split the data for validation-based selection, and fit the
chosen inverse model by minimising its training loss. Hyperparameters are selected under a fixed
search budget using a validation criterion. The trained model and preprocessing transforms are then
frozen and used unchanged in the candidate-generation and assessment steps described next.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
  \caption{Training and model selection for inverse candidate generation}\label{alg:train_pipeline}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Paired data $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$
    \Require Hyperparameter search space $\mathcal{H}$ and search budget $B$
    \Ensure Best fitted inverse model $\widehat{q}$ and preprocessing transforms $\mathcal{T}$

    \State $\mathcal{T} \leftarrow \text{compute\_transforms}(\mathcal{D})$ \Comment{Scaling for $\vect{x}$ and $\vect{y}$}
    \State $\mathcal{D}_{T}, \mathcal{D}_{V} \leftarrow \text{split}(\mathcal{T}(\mathcal{D}))$ \Comment{Train and validation splits}
    \State $S_{\mathrm{best}} \leftarrow +\infty$
    
    \For{$b=1$ to $B$}
      \State Sample configuration $h_b \in \mathcal{H}$ 
      \State Initialize parameters $\theta_b$ according to $h_b$
      \While{stopping criterion not met}
        \State $\theta_b \leftarrow \text{minimize } \mathcal{L}(\theta_b; \mathcal{D}_{T})$
        \State Monitor validation score $S_b$ on $\mathcal{D}_{V}$
      \EndWhile
      \If{$S_b < S_{\mathrm{best}}$}
        \State $S_{\mathrm{best}} \leftarrow S_b$
        \State $(h_{\mathrm{best}}, \theta_{\mathrm{best}}) \leftarrow (h_b, \theta_b)$
      \EndIf
    \EndFor
    
    \State $\widehat{q} \leftarrow \text{refit}(h_{\mathrm{best}}, \mathcal{D}_{T} \cup \mathcal{D}_{V})$ \Comment{Optional: final fit on all data}
    \State \Return $\widehat{q}, \mathcal{T}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Querying pipeline}\label{sec:query_pipeline}

Given a target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the inverse model generates a fixed-size candidate set and
the assessment suite assigns scores used to rank (and, if configured, filter) candidates.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:query_pipeline} summarises this target-driven loop. The same routine is used for
experimental evaluation on held-out targets and for interactive exploration, ensuring that reported
performance reflects a consistent candidate-generation and assessment interface.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
  \caption{Target-driven querying and candidate assessment}\label{alg:query_pipeline}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Trained inverse model $\widehat{q}$, target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$, candidate budget $K$
    \Require Assessment Suite $\mathcal{A}$ (includes Evaluators, ODD, and constraints)
    \Ensure Ranked candidate list $\mathcal{R}$

    \Repeat
      \State Obtain target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$
      \State Generate candidates $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K} \sim \widehat{q}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^{\star})$
      
      \For{$k=1$ to $K$}
        \State $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} \leftarrow \text{Evaluate}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})$ \Comment{Using surrogate or ground truth}
        \State $S^{(k)} \leftarrow \text{Assess}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}, \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}, \vect{y}^{\star})$ \Comment{Discrepancy, ODD, and validity}
      \EndFor
      
      \State $\mathcal{R} \leftarrow \text{RankCandidates}(\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}, \{S^{(k)}\})$ 
      \State \Return top subset of $\mathcal{R}$
    \Until{the exploration session terminates}
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
