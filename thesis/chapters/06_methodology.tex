\chapter{Methodology}\label{chap:methodology}

Earlier chapters established inverse querying as a practical interface for multi-objective design:
a user specifies a target objective vector and expects actionable decision suggestions, despite
non-uniqueness and possible infeasibility. Building on that foundation, the present chapter
specifies the framework adopted to answer such queries and to evaluate inverse models under a
shared and reproducible protocol. The central aim is to make claims about attainment traceable to
a declared evaluation rule, rather than to implicit properties of a particular inverse model.


The methodology follows a model-based inverse design approach. An inverse model is
fit in a model synthesis phase from paired data and is then used during interactive
exploration to propose decision candidates; a separate evaluation stage checks and ranks
candidates in objective space using a stated outcome evaluator and a task-defined discrepancy.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Problem Formulation}\label{sec:problem_formulation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The core objective of this research is to solve the one-to-many inverse mapping problem. Let $\mathcal{X}\subset\mathbb{R}^{n}$ denote the high-dimensional, admissible decision space (design parameters) and $\mathcal{Y}\subset\mathbb{R}^{m}$ the objective space (performance metrics). A query specifies a desired target performance $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathcal{Y}$. 

Because the mapping from $\mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is inherently surjective (multiple distinct designs can yield the exact same performance), the problem cannot be solved with a simple deterministic function. Instead, an inverse query must return a diverse set of candidate decisions $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}\subset\mathcal{X}$, together with an ordering induced by their objective-space attainment. 

To achieve this, this thesis investigates two fundamentally distinct mathematical paradigms for formulating the inverse generator, dictated by the availability of training data:

\subsection{Paradigm 1: Global Parametric Modeling (Data-Rich Environments)}
Building on the conceptual framework established in Equation~\eqref{eq:candidate_sampling}, traditional deep learning approaches (such as MDNs, CVAEs, and INNs) formulate the inverse component as a global parametric conditional distribution $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$. Utilizing the likelihood-based principle defined in Equation~\eqref{eq:prob_mle}, training fits the neural network parameters $\theta$ by minimizing the empirical negative log-likelihood (NLL) over the dataset:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\theta}\in\arg\min_{\theta}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\Big[-\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)\Big].
\label{eq:cond_mle_methodology}
\end{equation}
While Equation~\eqref{eq:prob_mle} defines the statistical goal of maximizing probability mass, the negative formulation in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} aligns the objective with standard gradient-based minimization protocols. At query time, the method draws a fixed-size candidate set directly from the learned distribution:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K.
  \label{eq:candidate_generation_methodology}
\end{equation}
This formulation relies solely on the empirical distribution of the training data and does not require active calls to a forward operator during the synthesis phase. However, it requires a massive dataset ($N$) to accurately shape $q_\theta$.

\subsection{Paradigm 2: Local Geometric \& Bounded Optimization (Data-Sparse Environments)}
When the dataset is strictly constrained ($N \le 100$), minimizing the NLL (Equation \ref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}) across a high-dimensional space leads to severe overfitting and physical hallucinations. Therefore, this thesis proposes formulating the inverse problem not as a global probability distribution, but as a localized geometric interpolation bounded by a fast forward-surrogate operator, $f_{surrogate}: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$. 

Instead of drawing from a global $q_\theta$, candidates are generated by dynamically constructing a local, coherent topological simplex around $\vect{y}^\star$ and sampling via constrained probabilistic rules (e.g., Dirichlet distributions), or via bounded gradient-descent: $\min \|f_{surrogate}(\vect{x}) - \vect{y}^\star\|^2$. This localized formulation forms the mathematical basis for the novel Geometrically Bounded Probabilistic Inversion (GBPI) framework, the mechanics of which are detailed extensively in \ref{sec:gbpi_framework}.

\subsection{Universal Candidate Evaluation}
Regardless of whether the candidates are generated via Paradigm 1 (Deep Learning) or Paradigm 2 (GBPI), they must be evaluated using a universal, mathematically rigorous metric. To verify the proposals, we operationalize the scoring logic introduced in Equation~\eqref{eq:verify_score_bg}. 

Specifically, we assess attainment using an outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{Y}$ (representing the ground-truth physical simulator) and a discrepancy function $d:\mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$. Each candidate $k$ receives a score:
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}(\vect{y}^\star) \;=\;
  d\!\big(\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^\star\big),
  \label{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}
\end{equation}
which induces a definitive ranking over the set. In this framework, $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ is typically realized as a distance metric, such as the Euclidean ($L_2$) norm, or a task-specific weighted discrepancy. Fixing the triplet $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ defines the evaluation contract used throughout the thesis, ensuring that attainment claims for both traditional and novel algorithms are traceable to an explicit, shared mathematical rule.

\section{Rationale and trade-offs}\label{sec:rationale_tradeoffs}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The adoption of a model-based, distributional approach is motivated by the inherent one-to-many 
nature of inverse decision mapping. By representing the inverse as $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$, 
the methodology explicitly acknowledges that multiple distinct decisions may be compatible with 
a single target. This avoids the risk of "mode averaging"—a common failure in deterministic 
regression where the model predicts the mean of multiple valid solutions, often resulting in 
an infeasible or non-physical design.



Furthermore, this design supports amortized inference. The computational burden of learning the 
inverse mapping is front-loaded into the synthesis phase. Once trained, the cost of answering 
new queries is reduced to simple sampling and evaluation, which is significantly faster than 
performing a full optimization search for every new target $\vect{y}^\star$. This aligns with 
established practices in simulation-based inference where efficient repeated conditioning is a 
primary requirement \cite{Papamakarios2021}.

A further motivation is comparability across benchmarks with heterogeneous forward access. Some
settings provide a trusted simulator, while others permit only surrogate-based evaluation. By 
defining attainment through a stated evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and discrepancy $d$, the 
methodology keeps the success criterion stable while allowing the evaluation mechanism to reflect 
what a benchmark provides.

These choices introduce trade-offs that are important for interpretation. Performance depends 
heavily on the fidelity of the evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and the diversity of the training data. 
If targets $\vect{y}^\star$ fall outside the region supported by the training archive, the 
inverse model may propose candidates that appear plausible under $q_\theta$ but fail when checked 
against $\mathcal{E}$. Additionally, a fixed candidate budget $K$ represents a trade-off between 
computational cost and the probability of "hitting" a high-quality mode in strongly multimodal 
landscapes.

Query-time optimization remains complementary rather than central. While procedures such as 
Bayesian optimization can refine candidates, they introduce additional design choices that can 
confound fair comparisons between model families. Consequently, this thesis prioritizes 
direct candidate generation with explicit ranking as the primary baseline.

\section{Rationale and Trade-Offs}\label{sec:rationale_tradeoffs}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The adoption of these distinct inverse mapping paradigms is fundamentally motivated by the inherent one-to-many nature of inverse decision mapping. Both paradigms explicitly acknowledge that multiple distinct physical decisions may be compatible with a single performance target. This foundational assumption avoids the catastrophic risk of ``mode averaging'' (referred to as the ``Centroid Trap'' in geometric contexts)---a common failure in standard deterministic regression where a model predicts the mean of multiple valid but distinct solutions, resulting in an infeasible or non-physical design.

However, the choice between the Global Parametric paradigm (Deep Learning) and the Local Geometric paradigm (GBPI) introduces distinct rationales and structural trade-offs:

\subsection{Trade-Offs in Global Parametric Models (CVAEs, INNs)}
The primary rationale for utilizing a global parametric distribution $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ is to support \textit{amortized inference}. The immense computational burden of learning the inverse mapping is entirely front-loaded into the offline training phase. Once the network is trained, the cost of answering new queries is reduced to simple, instantaneous sampling, aligning with established practices in simulation-based inference \cite{Papamakarios2021}.

\textbf{The Trade-Off:} This approach is extremely data-hungry. Performance depends heavily on the diversity and volume of the training data. If the dataset is highly sparse ($N \le 100$), the global $q_\theta$ distribution tends to over-smooth the objective space. When a target $\vect{y}^\star$ falls outside heavily sampled regions or between distinct physical archetypes, these models will confidently hallucinate candidates that appear mathematically plausible under $q_\theta$, but fail catastrophically when checked against the true physical evaluator $\mathcal{E}$. 

\subsection{Trade-Offs in Local Geometric Inversion (GBPI)}
The GBPI framework is motivated explicitly by the failure of deep learning models in data-sparse environments. By abandoning a global learned parameter ($\theta$) in favor of exact topological meshing (Delaunay triangulation) and strict mathematical boundaries ($D_{max}$), GBPI guarantees that generated candidates remain physically coherent. Furthermore, when faced with out-of-distribution (OOD) targets where parametric models hallucinate, GBPI proactively halts interpolation and defaults to mathematically bounded surrogate optimization. 

\textbf{The Trade-Off:} The geometric approach trades the smooth, global generalization of deep learning for strict, localized physical safety. Its primary limitation is its reliance on the offline pre-computation of the topological mesh, which scales poorly if the objective space dimensionality ($m$) becomes excessively high. Furthermore, its generative capability relies heavily on the fidelity of the lightweight forward surrogate to act as an accurate filter against Pareto Sag. 

\subsection{Standardized Benchmarking Rationale}
A final, critical motivation for this dual-paradigm formulation is ensuring fair comparability across highly heterogeneous algorithms. Comparing a probabilistic neural network to a geometrically bounded optimization framework is historically difficult. 

By defining ultimate attainment exclusively through a shared, independent evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ and a standardized discrepancy function $d$, this methodology explicitly isolates the candidate generation mechanism from the evaluation mechanism. Utilizing a fixed candidate budget $K$ enforces a strict trade-off between computational cost and the probability of ``hitting'' a high-quality mode. This ensures that any claims of algorithmic superiority between the baselines and the novel GBPI framework are grounded in an identical, traceable mathematical contract.

\section{Framework and Protocol}\label{sec:framework}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Figure~\ref{fig:framework} summarizes the universal workflow used throughout the thesis. To ensure a fair comparison across fundamentally different algorithmic families, the protocol follows a strict propose--check structure: an inverse model proposes a set of candidate decisions for a queried target, and a separate, independent checking stage evaluates and ranks those candidates in the objective space under a declared evaluator and discrepancy metric. This separation ensures that one-to-many ambiguity is handled exclusively by the generative mechanism, while ultimate attainment is assessed by an objective, mathematical scoring rule.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \begin{adjustbox}{width=\linewidth}
      \input{figures/framework}
  \end{adjustbox}
  \caption[Propose--Check Workflow Framework]{Propose--check workflow used throughout the thesis. An inverse model (either parametric or geometric) generates a candidate set for a target objective vector, and an explicit evaluation stage scores and ranks candidates using a stated outcome evaluator.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

The workflow strictly separates offline model synthesis from online interactive exploration. In the \textit{model synthesis phase}, the specific algorithmic engine is prepared. For global parametric models (e.g., CVAEs, INNs), this involves fitting the proposal model from paired data using the conditional likelihood objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. For the geometric GBPI framework, this involves pre-computing the Delaunay mesh, training the RBF surrogate, and establishing the coherence threshold ($D_{max}$). 

In the \textit{interactive exploration phase}, a query $\vect{y}^{\star}$ is answered by generating $K$ candidates—either by sampling from the learned parametric conditional model $q_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star)$, or by routing through GBPI's bounded generative pathways. Treating the budget $K$ as fixed is a critical part of the experimental interface, keeping the output format consistent regardless of the underlying model family. 

Each candidate is then checked by applying the evaluator and discrepancy defined in \eqref{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}, after which candidates are filtered and ranked in objective space. The evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ is selected to match the benchmark and deployment context; it may be a trusted simulator, a measurement routine, or a high-fidelity surrogate reserved strictly for evaluation. When a single optimal decision is required, the best-ranked candidate under the declared rule is selected. Recording the evaluation triplet $(K,\mathcal{E},d)$ alongside model configuration makes results fully auditable and enables rigorous, unbiased comparisons across distinct inverse model paradigms.

\section{Normalization and Data Preparation}\label{sec:normalization}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We begin from a raw paired archive $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{raw}}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. Before fitting any models or building any topological meshes, we apply a deterministic preprocessing pipeline to ensure that training, candidate generation, and discrepancy-based scoring are performed in a consistent and numerically stable coordinate system. The paired archive is split into training, validation, and test subsets. To prevent information leakage, all preprocessing parameters are estimated using only the training split. We employ a feature-wise min-max scaling—referred to here as a hypercube normalization—which maps both decisions and outcomes into a uniform $[0, 1]$ range. 

The choice of min-max normalization over alternative methods (such as Z-score standardization) is an absolute mathematical necessity for the dual-paradigm framework utilized in this thesis:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Metric Fairness and Attainment Scoring: Since the evaluation contract relies on a Euclidean discrepancy $d$, it is essential that all objective dimensions span an identical numerical range. Min-max scaling ensures that a 10\% error in one objective is mathematically equivalent to a 10\% error in another, preventing the distance metric from being dominated by variables with naturally larger absolute magnitudes.
    \item Parametric Generative Stability: Deep generative architectures, such as CVAEs and INNs, often utilize bounded activation functions like Sigmoid or Tanh. Mapping the decision space $\mathcal{X}$ to a unit hypercube aligns the target data with these activation ranges, improving gradient flow and preventing numerical instability during backpropagation \cite{Sola1997, Sarle1997}. It also defines a clear feasible boundary for initializing Mixture Density Networks (MDNs).
    \item Geometric and Topological Unbiasedness (GBPI): For the GBPI framework, normalization is the bedrock of physical safety. Because GBPI relies heavily on K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) to calculate the Coherence Threshold ($D_{max}$), the decision space must be perfectly isotropic. Without min-max normalization, physical parameters with massive scales (e.g., thousands of RPMs) would entirely dominate fractional parameters (e.g., geometric ratios), rendering local neighborhood calculations and spatial topologies mathematically meaningless.
\end{enumerate}

We denote the resulting fitted normalization maps by $T_x$ for decisions and $T_y$ for outcomes. Applying these maps yields the normalized pairs:
\begin{equation}
  \widetilde{\vect{x}}_i = T_x(\vect{x}_i) = \frac{\vect{x}_i - \vect{x}_{\min}}{\vect{x}_{\max} - \vect{x}_{\min}},
  \qquad 
  \widetilde{\vect{y}}_i = T_y(\vect{y}_i) = \frac{\vect{y}_i - \vect{y}_{\min}}{\vect{y}_{\max} - \vect{y}_{\min}},
  \label{eq:normalization_maps}
\end{equation}
where the vectors $\min$ and $\max$ are determined feature-wise from the training data. For constant features where $\max = \min$, the range is set to unity to maintain numerical stability and avoid division-by-zero errors. 

During inference, generated model outputs are transformed back to their original physical units using the inverse map $T_x^{-1}$. Similarly, the target query $\vect{y}^\star$ is transformed via $T_y$ before the discrepancy $d$ is computed. This guarantees that distance metrics, interpolation weights, and generative boundaries are completely agnostic to arbitrary physical units.

\section{Inverse-model training}\label{sec:training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

With the propose--check protocol fixed in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, implementation begins by
training the inverse proposal model from paired decision--objective examples
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. The training goal is to obtain a conditional
generator $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ that captures the empirical decision--objective relation
while remaining expressive enough to represent one-to-many structure. The modeling background in
Chapter~\ref{chap:Background} introduces the three conditional families used in the thesis and
derives their probabilistic forms. The present section specifies the optimization objectives as used
in the experiments.

Across models, parameter estimation follows a shared principle: fit the conditional distribution to
the observed pairs by minimizing the negative log-likelihood as defined in 
\eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. The evaluation components, namely the outcome evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ 
and discrepancy $d$, are not used during training and enter only through the checking rule defined 
by \eqref{eq:candidate_scores_methodology}.

The three instantiations below differ in how they parameterize $q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ and
how the conditional log-density in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} is computed or approximated.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mixture density network}\label{subsec:mdn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The mixture density network represents the conditional density as a finite Gaussian mixture with
$\vect{y}$-dependent parameters,
\begin{equation}
  q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\,
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x};\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y}),\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\big),
  \qquad
  \sum_{j=1}^{M}\pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})=1,\ \ \pi_{\theta,j}(\vect{y})\ge 0 .
  \label{eq:mdn_density}
\end{equation}
Training substitutes \eqref{eq:mdn_density} into the objective in \eqref{eq:mdn_opt_problem} and
optimizes the resulting mixture negative log-likelihood. The implementation enforces the standard
constraints on mixture weights and uses a stable parameterisation of covariance matrices, consistent
with the model definition in Section~\ref{sec:mdn}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conditional variational autoencoder}\label{subsec:cvae_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The conditional variational autoencoder introduces latent variables to represent conditional
variability. As direct maximization of the conditional likelihood is generally intractable, 
the model is trained by minimizing the negative evidence lower bound (ELBO), which serves as 
a tractable surrogate for the objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology}. Utilizing an 
encoder $q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$ and a decoder $p_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})$, 
the training objective is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta},\widehat{\phi}\in\arg\min_{\theta,\phi}\;
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\Big[
    - \mathbb{E}_{q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)} \big[ \log p_{\theta}(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z}) \big]
    + \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_{\phi}(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\,\|\,p(\vect{z})\big)
  \Big].
  \label{eq:cvae_elbo_methodology}
\end{equation}
The first term represents the conditional reconstruction loss, ensuring the decoder can 
recover the decision $\vect{x}$ given the correct latent code, while the Kullback--Leibler 
divergence term regularises the latent space against a prior $p(\vect{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$. 
At query time, the method draws $\vect{z} \sim p(\vect{z})$ to generate diverse candidates for 
a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, consistent with the candidate budget $K$ used in 
the evaluation protocol.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Invertible neural network}\label{subsec:inn_training}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The invertible neural network defines a conditional density through an invertible 
transformation between a simple reference latent distribution and the decision space. 
Using a conditional invertible map $T_{\theta}(\cdot;\vect{y})$ and its inverse 
$T_{\theta}^{-1}(\cdot;\vect{y})$, the relationship is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x} = T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}),\qquad \vect{z} = T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y}),
  \label{eq:inn_forward_map_methodology}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{z}$ follows a standard normal prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I})$. The 
conditional log-density for any pair $(\vect{x}, \vect{y})$ follows exactly from the 
change-of-variables formula:
\begin{equation}
  \log q_{\theta}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}) =
  \log p(\vect{z}) +
  \log\left|\det \frac{\partial T_{\theta}^{-1}(\vect{x};\vect{y})}{\partial \vect{x}}\right|.
  \label{eq:inn_change_of_vars_methodology}
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:inn_change_of_vars_methodology} into the negative log-likelihood 
objective in \eqref{eq:cond_mle_methodology} allows for exact likelihood-based training 
without variational approximations. Sampling is performed by drawing $K$ latent vectors 
from the prior and applying the forward pass $T_{\theta}(\vect{z};\vect{y}^{\star})$. This 
bijective structure ensures that every point in the latent reservoir corresponds to a unique 
candidate, integrating naturally with the propose--check protocol.


\section{Geometrically Bounded Probabilistic Inversion (GBPI)}
\label{sec:gbpi_framework}

While deep learning approaches such as Conditional Variational Autoencoders (CVAEs) and Invertible Neural Networks (INNs) offer robust solutions to the one-to-many inverse mapping problem, they inherently require large volumes of training data to accurately capture underlying probability distributions. To address scenarios where data is fundamentally limited, this research proposes a novel computational architecture: the Geometrically Bounded Probabilistic Inversion (GBPI) framework. 

GBPI is specifically engineered to operate under highly constrained environments characterized by extremely sparse datasets ($N \le 100$), high-dimensional decision spaces featuring complex inputs with varying scales, and strict real-time execution constraints requiring millisecond-level latency. By hybridizing topological meshing, probabilistic sampling, and lightweight surrogate modeling, GBPI safely navigates the decision space without falling victim to common multi-modal mapping pitfalls such as the ``Centroid Trap'' or ``Pareto Sag''. The complete routing architecture, detailing the transition from offline pre-processing to the real-time generative and optimization pathways, is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:gbpi_workflow}.

\subsection{System Initialization (Offline Phase)}
\label{subsec:gbpi_offline_phase}
Before any real-time user interaction occurs, the system executes two critical offline computations to prepare the mapping environment:

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Objective Space Meshing (Delaunay Triangulation):} To enable instantaneous real-time mapping from the continuous performance space back to discrete known designs, the 2D objective space ($Y$) must be topologically partitioned. The system applies Delaunay Triangulation \cite{barber1996quickhull} to the complete set of objective performance coordinates $\mathcal{Y} = \{\mathbf{y}_1, \mathbf{y}_2, \dots, \mathbf{y}_N\}$. 
    
    This geometric algorithm constructs a continuous, non-overlapping mesh of convex simplices (triangles) that connect the sparse dataset points, maximizing the minimum angle of the triangles to avoid narrow, distorted interpolations. This step essentially builds a mathematical map of the currently attainable performance region (the convex hull of the dataset), as visually demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:delaunay_meshing}. 
    
    Pre-computing this topological mesh entirely offline is a structural requirement for maintaining millisecond-level latency during real-time user interaction. By building the spatial data structures in advance, the system can execute $O(\log N)$ point-location queries to rapidly identify which specific triangle encloses a user's arbitrary performance target ($y^*$). The three vertices of that enclosing simplex then instantly serve as the foundational performance ``anchors'' for the online generative mapping.

    \begin{figure}[!htbp]
        \centering
      \begin{adjustbox}{width=0.8\linewidth}
          \input{figures/delaunay_meshing}
      \end{adjustbox}
    \caption{Objective Space Meshing via Delaunay Triangulation. The offline phase constructs a continuous topological map of the known dataset, explicitly defining the boundaries of the attainable performance region (Convex Hull) and breaking the internal space into discrete, mathematically navigable simplices.}
    \label{fig:delaunay_meshing}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Surrogate Model Training:} A highly lightweight micro-surrogate model—specifically a Radial Basis Function (RBF) network \cite{buhmann2003radial}—is trained to act as a forward predictor mapping the decision space to the objective space ($f_{RBF}: X \to Y$). Due to the extreme sparsity of the data ($N \le 100$), this training completes in milliseconds. Unlike deep neural networks, the RBF requires minimal data to construct a smooth interpolation surface, serving as an instant, real-time physical validator for the generative phases.
    
    \item \textbf{Coherence Threshold Calibration ($D_{max}$):} The most critical offline step is establishing a rigorous mathematical definition of a ``safe'' physical neighborhood. This prevents the generative algorithm from hallucinating impossible designs. In inverse problems, a phenomenon known as multi-modality frequently occurs: two designs might achieve nearly identical performance (extreme proximity in the $Y$-space) while utilizing entirely different underlying physical strategies (massive separation in the $X$-space). 
    
    To safely interpolate, the system must quantify ``coherence''—the assumption that small, continuous variations in performance correspond to safe, continuous variations in physical parameters. To measure this baseline spatial density, the system employs a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) distance metric \cite{cover1967nearest} on the normalized decision space $\mathcal{X} = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N\}$.
    
    For every configuration $\mathbf{x}_i$ in the dataset, the algorithm identifies the set of its $k$ closest neighbors, denoted as $\mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x}_i)$. The pairwise Euclidean distance between $\mathbf{x}_i$ and each of its neighbors $\mathbf{x}_j \in \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x}_i)$ is calculated as:
    \begin{equation}
        d(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{m=1}^{M} (x_{i,m} - x_{j,m})^2}
    \end{equation}
    where $M$ is the dimensionality of the decision space. A universal set of all local neighborhood distances across the entire dataset, $\mathcal{D}_{KNN}$, is then aggregated:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{D}_{KNN} = \Big\{ d(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \mid i \in \{1, \dots, N\}, \mathbf{x}_j \in \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{x}_i) \Big\}
    \end{equation}
    
    To establish the absolute boundary of a single physical archetype, the system calculates the Dynamic Coherence Threshold ($D_{max}$) by extracting the \textbf{95th percentile} of the distribution $\mathcal{D}_{KNN}$:
    \begin{equation}
        D_{max} = P_{95}(\mathcal{D}_{KNN})
    \end{equation}
    
    As visualized in Figure \ref{fig:dmax_distribution}, the statistical and physical justification for selecting the 95th percentile threshold is twofold:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Capturing Intra-modal Variation ($\le D_{max}$):} The vast majority (the lower 95\%) of local distances represent standard, continuous engineering adjustments within a single physical archetype (e.g., slightly altering the wingspan of a specific drone chassis without changing its fundamental topology). $D_{max}$ perfectly bounds this continuous, safe region.
        \item \textbf{Isolating Inter-modal Voids ($> D_{max}$):} In highly sparse datasets, the top 5\% of nearest-neighbor distances represent statistical outliers. Physically, these outliers correspond to massive, discontinuous leaps across the decision space—bridging the empty topological voids between fundamentally distinct engineering archetypes (e.g., jumping from a quadcopter topology to a fixed-wing topology). 
    \end{itemize}

    By formally establishing $D_{max}$ at this percentile, the GBPI framework creates a definitive mathematical ``speed limit.'' It grants the algorithm maximum freedom to interpolate within known physical families while proactively hard-coding a boundary that prevents it from blindly averaging across unmanufacturable voids.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Real-Time Generation Pipeline (Online Phase)}
\label{subsec:gbpi_online_phase}

Once the offline mathematical environment is established, the GBPI framework transitions to the real-time generation phase. This phase is triggered instantly whenever a user interacts with the system to request a novel design. To ensure that the output is both physically safe and highly accurate without exceeding millisecond-level latency, the pipeline executes the following sequential steps:

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Step 1: Target Localization:} 
    The generation process begins by translating the user's continuous intent into a discrete mathematical coordinate system anchored by known physical designs. When the user dynamically selects an arbitrary target objective ($y^*$) in the 2D Objective Space ($Y$), the system queries the pre-computed Delaunay mesh to locate the specific triangle containing $y^*$. The three vertices of this enclosing triangle become the performance ``Anchors'' ($y_A, y_B, y_C$). The framework then calculates the exact deterministic Barycentric weights $(\bar{w}_A, \bar{w}_B, \bar{w}_C)$, which mathematically quantify the geometric proximity of $y^*$ relative to each respective anchor point. This initial geometric mapping is demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:target_localization}.

    \begin{figure}[htbp]
        \centering
        \begin{adjustbox}{width=0.8\linewidth}
          \input{figures/target_localization}
      \end{adjustbox}
        \caption[Phase 1: Target Localization in Objective Space]{Visualization of Phase 1: Target Localization. The continuous 2D Objective Space ($Y$) is meshed via Delaunay Triangulation. The arbitrary user target ($y^*$) is located within a specific simplex, and its geometric proximity to the known anchor points ($y_A, y_B, y_C$) is quantified by the exact Barycentric weights ($\bar{w}_A, \bar{w}_B, \bar{w}_C$).}
        \label{fig:target_localization}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Step 2: The Local Coherence Check (The Safeguard):} 
    Before attempting to map the objective weights back into the high-dimensional decision space, GBPI enforces a strict physical safeguard. Its primary function is to prevent the ``Centroid Trap''—a critical failure where linearly averaging two fundamentally different parent designs yields a physically nonsensical configuration. 
    
    If a bounding simplex is found in Step 1, the system retrieves the decision configurations ($x_A, x_B, x_C$) corresponding to the anchors and calculates their pairwise Euclidean distances. The region is evaluated against the Coherence Gate:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{Coherent Region:} If all pairwise distances are $\le D_{max}$, the anchors share the same physical strategy. The region is deemed safe for geometric interpolation.
        \item \textit{Incoherent Region:} If any distance is $> D_{max}$, the anchors represent fundamentally different engineering strategies. To avoid non-linear topological voids—which can be further mapped and rigorously detected using tools like Topological Data Analysis (TDA) and the Vietoris-Rips complex \cite{carlsson2009topology}—the system refuses interpolation to guarantee physical safety.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Step 3: Algorithmic Routing:} 
    Based on the localization and coherence evaluation, the system dynamically routes the user's query into one of two distinct mathematical pathways:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Pathway A: Probabilistic Generative Mapping.} Triggered exclusively when the bounding region is mathematically verified as coherent. Instead of yielding a single deterministic output, the Barycentric weights are utilized as the mean vector for a Dirichlet Distribution \cite{bishop2006pattern}. The system samples a cloud of $K$ new weight vectors distributed tightly around the exact user request. These sampled weights are linearly inverted back into the decision space via matrix multiplication ($X_{candidates} = W_{sampled} \times X_{anchors}$). As depicted in Figure \ref{fig:dirichlet_sampling}, the system invents $K$ novel, diverse configurations that are bounded strictly within the safe, locally coherent manifold.

          \end{adjustbox}

The algorithm samples $K$ probabilistic weight vectors, generating a dense, diverse cloud of novel configurations ($X_{candidates}$) bounded entirely within the safe, established physical manifold.}
            \label{fig:dirichlet_sampling}
        \end{figure}
        
        \item \textbf{Pathway B: Surrogate-Assisted Trust-Region Optimization.} Triggered when the targeted region crosses distinct physical modes (incoherent) or requires extrapolation beyond the known mesh (no simplex found). Proactively abandoning interpolation, the system designates the single closest known anchor to the user target as the \textit{Base Design}. To prevent the optimizer from wandering into invalid topological voids, a strict Trust Region Boundary (e.g., a $5\%$ maximum deviation limit) is enforced around the Base Design. Utilizing the pre-trained RBF surrogate from the offline phase, the system executes a rapid gradient descent to progressively optimize the base design toward $y^*$, subject to the strict constraint that it remains within the established bounds. Figure \ref{fig:trust_region_opt} visually outlines this bounded optimization process.
        
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Step 4: Surrogate Filtering and Ranking:} 
    Due to the non-linear nature of real-world physical laws, linear interpolation from Pathway A may occasionally suffer from ``Pareto Sag'' (where predictions slightly deviate from the true front). Therefore, a final quality assurance filter is necessary. 
    
    All generated candidate configurations ($X_{candidates}$), regardless of their originating pathway, are passed through the RBF micro-surrogate for instant validation. The surrogate-predicted performances ($\hat{y}$) are compared directly against the user's exact requested target ($y^*$), calculating the residual error. Candidates exhibiting high residual errors are immediately discarded, and the framework rigorously ranks the remaining physically coherent and mathematically accurate configurations for presentation to the end-user.

    \end{figure}

\end{enumerate}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Evaluation plan}\label{sec:evaluation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Evaluation follows the propose--check contract defined in Section~\ref{sec:framework}. For each target
$\vect{y}^{\star}$, an inverse model produces $K$ decision candidates
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. A fixed forward checker computes the corresponding checked
objective vectors,
\[
  \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} \;=\; \mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}), \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
\]
where $\mathcal{E}:\mathcal{X}\rightarrow\mathcal{Y}$ denotes the best available evaluator for the
benchmark (simulator, measurement routine, or checking surrogate). All reported metrics are derived
from checked quantities, and $\mathcal{E}$ is not accessed by inverse models at test time beyond
producing candidates. Fixing $\mathcal{E}$, the discrepancy definition $d$, and the candidate budget
$K$ keeps comparisons attributable to inverse modeling choices rather than changes in the evaluation
interface.

Two complementary evaluation views are reported. The first view evaluates the user-facing inverse
querying interface by measuring attainment in objective space under the checker. The second view
evaluates the inverse model as a conditional sampler in design space, focusing on uncertainty
behavior. The separation is deliberate: objective-space metrics quantify whether a target can be
matched under the declared checker, while design-space diagnostics quantify whether the generated candidates behave as a physically meaningful and coherent set of decisions.


%%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Objective-space plausibility diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_objective}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates how well a checked candidate set can match a requested target.
Given checked outcomes $\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ for a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the
evaluation proceeds in the normalized objective coordinate system defined by the preprocessing maps in
Section~\ref{sec:normalization}. This section keeps the objective-space contract explicit: we diagnose
component-wise mismatch relative to the target, aggregate mismatch into a scalar discrepancy for
ranking, and interpret the resulting candidate cloud in the same normalized units across all targets.
Throughout this section, $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ are understood to be
expressed in the normalized objective coordinates defined in Section~\ref{sec:normalization}.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Target-centered residuals}\label{sec:eval_plausibility_z}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For a user target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ and the checked outcome of candidate $k$,
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$, define the target-centered residual in objective
component $j$ as
\begin{equation}
  r_j^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \widehat{y}^{(k)}_j - y^{\star}_j,
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual}
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{y}^{(k)}_j$ is the $j$-th objective value of candidate $k$ after checking and $y^{\star}_j$
is the $j$-th component of the target. Because objectives are evaluated in the normalized coordinate
system, $r_j^{(k)}$ is already expressed in comparable units across objectives: its magnitude can be
read directly as a fraction of the normalized objective range.

Collect components into the residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big(r_1^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),\dots,r_m^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{m}.
  \label{eq:target_centered_residual_vector}
\end{equation}
Each coordinate of $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ corresponds to one objective dimension, so the
vector provides a transparent component-wise diagnostic prior to aggregation. In particular, the sign
of $r_j^{(k)}$ indicates whether candidate $k$ lies above or below the target in objective $j$, while
the magnitude indicates how far it is from the target in the normalized coordinate system.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Discrepancy contract}\label{sec:eval_discrepancy_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Given residual vectors $\{\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\}_{k=1}^{K}$, a discrepancy $d$ aggregates
target-centered mismatch into a scalar that supports ranking and downstream summaries. The default uses
Euclidean aggregation in residual space, based on the $\ell_2$ norm:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{m}\Big(r^{(k)}_{j}(\vect{y}^{\star})\Big)^{2}},
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid}
\end{equation}
where $\lVert \cdot \rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm and $K$ is the candidate budget. The score
$s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ is a single nonnegative scalar used to rank candidates for the same target.

When objective dimensions are strongly correlated, Euclidean aggregation can effectively count the same
direction multiple times. In that setting, a Mahalanobis discrepancy incorporates covariance
structure, \cite{Mahalanobis1936,DeMaesschalck2000}
\begin{equation}
  d_{\mathrm{Mah}}(\vect{u},\vect{v})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{(\vect{u}-\vect{v})^{\top}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\vect{u}-\vect{v})},
  \label{eq:mahalanobis_distance}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{u},\vect{v}\in\mathbb{R}^m$ are objective vectors in a common normalized coordinate system
and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ is an estimated covariance matrix on a declared
reference split of checked outcomes (or residuals). We estimate $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ on a declared
reference split using the pooled collection of residual vectors $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$
across targets and candidates in that split. The inverse $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}$ is computed after
regularization to ensure stable inversion when $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is ill-conditioned.

Applied to the target-centered residual vector, the corresponding candidate score is
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)^{\top}
  \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}
  \big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\big)}.
  \label{eq:discrepancy_residual_mahal}
\end{equation}
Here $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\in\mathbb{R}^m$ is the residual vector of candidate $k$ relative
to the target. The quadratic form weights directions by the inverse covariance so that correlated
objective directions contribute less to the score than independent directions.

The checker $\mathcal{E}$, the stored normalization maps from Section~\ref{sec:normalization}, and the
declared discrepancy $d$ together define the objective-space evaluation contract. Under this contract,
each candidate $\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}$ is checked, compared to the same target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ in
the same normalized units, and ranked by the same externally stated scoring rule:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  d\!\big(\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),\,\vect{0}\big),
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K.
  \label{eq:per_candidate_discrepancy}
\end{equation}
Here $\vect{0}\in\mathbb{R}^m$ denotes the zero vector, so the discrepancy is evaluated as a distance
from the residual vector to the origin in target-centered residual space.

When a single decision is required, the best-shot candidate under the declared rule is
\begin{equation}
  k^{\ast}
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \qquad
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{\ast}=\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k^{\ast})}.
  \label{eq:best_shot_def}
\end{equation}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Candidate-cloud plausibility}\label{sec:eval_candidate_cloud}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Scalar discrepancy scores $s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ support ranking within a candidate set, but they
do not by themselves describe how the full set behaves around the requested target. For user-facing
decision support, the aggregate geometry of the checked outcomes
$\{ \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(1)}, \dots, \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(K)} \}$ matters because it indicates whether the generative framework produces a concentrated, target-centered cloud or a diffuse or systematically
shifted set of outcomes.

We therefore evaluate candidate-set behavior in the same target-centered residual space used for
ranking. Let $\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ denote the residual vector defined in
\eqref{eq:target_centered_residual_vector}. In this thesis, $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}$ are expressed in the normalized objective coordinates defined in
Section~\ref{sec:normalization}, so residual magnitudes are directly comparable across objective
dimensions.

For a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, define the within-set mean residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \label{eq:mean_residual_vector_cloud}
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the candidate budget.

Candidate-cloud plausibility is summarized using two set-level statistics: systematic offset of the
cloud from the target and within-set dispersion around the cloud mean,
\begin{equation}
  b(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{\lVert \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}},
  \qquad
  v(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \mathrm{median}_{k}\;
  \frac{\big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})-\bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}}.
  \label{eq:residual_bias_dispersion}
\end{equation}
Here $m$ is the number of objectives, $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm, $b$ is the
magnitude of the mean residual (cloud bias) in average per-objective units, and $v$ is a robust
within-set dispersion summary computed as the median distance of residuals to the cloud mean, again
reported per objective via the $\sqrt{m}$ normalization.

The pair $(b,v)$ separates failure modes that are conflated by a single scalar discrepancy: low $b$
with high $v$ indicates a cloud that is centered but diffuse, while high $b$ with low $v$ indicates a
cloud that is concentrated but systematically shifted away from the target. This distinction supports
interpretation of objective-space behavior under a fixed sampling budget $K$.

scatter relative to the target in target-centered residual units.

\subsection{Design-space uncertainty diagnostics}\label{sec:eval_design}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space attainment evaluates whether at least one candidate can reproduce a target
outcome, but it does not reveal whether the inverse model represents the underlying
one-to-many structure in a trustworthy way. Because candidate-based inversion is intended
to act as a conditional sampler over decisions, we also evaluate uncertainty behaviour
directly in decision space. These diagnostics use held-out paired test data
$(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$: we condition on $\vect{y}_i$, generate $K$ decision candidates, and
compare the resulting sample distribution to the ground-truth decision $\vect{x}_i$.

We report two complementary perspectives that answer different questions.
First, reliability (calibration) asks whether the sampler’s stated uncertainty is
statistically consistent with the realized ground truth---informally, whether the model is
``honest'' about how uncertain it is. Second, probabilistic accuracy asks whether the
predictive distribution is practically useful, meaning that it concentrates near the truth
without becoming unnecessarily diffuse. Reporting both is important because a sampler can
be well calibrated yet uninformative (too wide), or sharp yet unreliable (overconfident).


\subsubsection{Reliability via PIT}\label{sec:eval_pit}

Reliability evaluates the uncertainty behaviour of the conditional candidate distribution
under repeated queries. The central question is whether the candidate set provides
meaningful percentiles: across many test cases, do values labelled as ``10th percentile''
actually contain the ground truth about 10\% of the time, ``50th percentile'' about 50\% of
the time, and so on. This is a distribution-level check, not a best-candidate accuracy
check. \cite{Diebold1998}



To assess this reliability, we employ the Probability Integral Transform (PIT) applied component-wise to the empirical conditional distribution. For each test case $i$ and each decision dimension $d \in \{1, \dots, D\}$, let
\[
  \widehat{x}^{(1)}_{i,d}, \dots, \widehat{x}^{(K)}_{i,d}
\]
denote the $K$ sampled values of component $d$, and let $x_{i,d}$ denote the corresponding ground-truth component. The PIT value is computed as the empirical percentile rank of the ground-truth component among the sampled candidates:
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,d} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbb{I}\!\left(\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right)}_{\text{percentile rank of }x_{i,d}\text{ among }K\text{ samples}},
  \label{eq:pit_empirical}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbb{I}(\cdot)$ is the indicator function. The quantity $z_{i,d}\in[0,1]$ is the empirical CDF of the $K$ sampled candidates evaluated at
the ground-truth value $x_{i,d}$. Equivalently, it is the percentile rank of $x_{i,d}$ among the
samples: for example, $z_{i,d}=0.1$ indicates that 10\% of the sampled candidates lie below the actual ground-truth value $x_{i,d}$.
If the conditional sampler is calibrated for component $d$, then these ranks aggregated over
test cases are approximately uniform on $[0,1]$. Systematic departures from uniformity therefore
diagnose distorted uncertainty under repeated conditioning. 


PIT is intentionally based on ranks rather than distances. It can therefore look acceptable
even when the samples are very wide, provided the truth lands at the right ranks often
enough across many test cases. For this reason we treat PIT as a reliability diagnostic,
not a measure of how informative the candidate distribution is.

Calibration is visualized using a PIT calibration curve constructed from the empirical CDF
of the aggregated PIT values. Let $\{z_{\ell}\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the pooled PIT values aggregated over test cases and
selected dimensions, where $L$ is the total number of PIT values included. Plotting the empirical CDF of $\{z_{(\ell)}\}$ against the diagonal
$u\mapsto u$ provides a direct baseline for perfect calibration. This view is convenient in
practice because it summarizes miscalibration without relying on histogram bin choices and
it supports direct visual comparison across inverse models and sampling schemes. \cite{dawid1984statistical}


Systematic departures from the diagonal have interpretable signatures, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:pit_signatures} and Figure~\ref{fig:pit_signatures}. These signatures are fundamentally driven by the relationship between the PIT histogram's density and the slope of the calibration curve (the CDF). Because the calibration curve is the cumulative integral of the PIT values, its slope at any point is directly proportional to the height of the histogram at that specific percentile:

\begin{itemize}
    \item High Histogram Density (Peaks): When many ground truth observations fall within a specific percentile range, the calibration curve "sprints" or rises rapidly. For instance, a peak at $PIT \approx 0$ causes the curve to shoot above the diagonal immediately.
    \item Low Histogram Density (Valleys): When few observations fall within a range, the calibration curve "crawls" or flattens out. If the histogram is empty near $PIT \approx 0$, the curve lags, falling below the diagonal.
\end{itemize}

For compact reporting, we summarize deviation from the diagonal using the mean absolute
calibration error (MACE). Let $\{z_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the pooled PIT values aggregated
over the selected test cases and decision dimensions. Define the empirical CDF of the aggregated
PIT values as
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{G}(\alpha)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathbf{1}\!\left(z_\ell \le \alpha\right),
  \qquad \alpha\in[0,1].
  \label{eq:pit_ecdf}
\end{equation}
We then compute MACE as the average absolute deviation of this curve from the uniform reference
CDF over a fixed grid:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MACE}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\big|\widehat{G}(\alpha_j)-\alpha_j\big|,
  \label{eq:mace}
\end{equation}
where $\{\alpha_j\}_{j=1}^{J}\subset[0,1]$ is a fixed grid (e.g., equally spaced). This quantity measures the
average absolute deviation between the empirical PIT CDF and the uniform reference CDF across
quantile levels. Lower values indicate more reliable uncertainty representation, which matters
when candidate dispersion is used to guide downstream exploration or decision-making.



\subsubsection{Probabilistic accuracy via CRPS}\label{sec:eval_crps}

PIT-based reliability checks whether percentiles are meaningful, but it does not determine
whether the candidate distribution is useful for recovering decisions. In particular, a
sampler can be well calibrated while remaining overly diffuse, which makes candidate sets
costly to search and less informative for exploration. We therefore complement PIT with a
strictly proper scoring rule that rewards distributions that are close to the truth while
penalizing unnecessary spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}

We evaluate probabilistic accuracy using the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS).
In simple terms, CRPS asks the following in our setting:
did the candidate distribution put mass near the observed design value $x_{i,d}$, and did it
avoid spreading samples out more than needed? This targets usefulness rather than only
long-run percentile behavior. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007,Hersbach2000}

For each held-out test case $i$ and decision dimension $d$, CRPS compares the sampled
distribution to the ground truth $x_{i,d}$. In expectation form,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CRPS}_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-x_{i,d}|}_{\text{accuracy}}
  \;-\;
  \frac{1}{2}\,
  \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\,|X-X'|}_{\text{dispersion}},
  \label{eq:crps_expectation}
\end{equation}

where $X$ and $X'$ are independent draws from the predictive distribution represented by the
candidate samples. The first expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-x_{i,d}|\,]$, measures typical
distance to the ground truth and therefore captures accuracy: it is smaller when the sampled
distribution is centered near $x_{i,d}$. The second expectation, $\mathbb{E}[\,|X-X'|\,]$,
measures typical distance between samples and therefore captures dispersion:
it is larger when the predictive distribution is wide. Because this dispersion term is
subtracted (with factor $1/2$), a distribution that is unnecessarily diffuse increases the
overall CRPS. Low CRPS therefore indicates candidate sets that place mass near the ground
truth without inflating spread. \cite{GneitingRaftery2007}



PIT and CRPS diagnose different failure modes. PIT can appear acceptable even when samples
are extremely wide, because it is driven by ranks. CRPS will penalize such diffuseness.
Conversely, a very sharp sampler can achieve good CRPS on easy cases while still being
systematically overconfident across the dataset, which PIT will expose through U-shaped
departures from the diagonal. For example, a model may achieve strong best-of-$K$ target matching yet be systematically overconfident (sharp but miscalibrated), or it may be reliable but too diffuse to support decisive exploration.
Reporting both diagnostics makes these trade-offs explicit and supports the interpretation of the comparative results \cite{Diebold1998}


\subsubsection{Interpretive Signatures of PIT and CRPS}
\label{sec:diagnostic_signatures}

To provide a comprehensive view of model behavior and facilitate a deeper understanding of how predictive distributions manifest in decision space, Figure \ref{fig:diagnostic_signatures_grid} illustrates various edge cases in probabilistic modeling. By analyzing the relationship between the spatial distribution of generated samples and the target $\vect{y}^\star$, we can diagnose specific failure modes that standard accuracy metrics might overlook.  

The evaluation relies on two complementary perspectives: the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS), which quantifies the total error by penalizing both lack of accuracy (distance from target) and lack of sharpness (diffuse variance); and the Probability Integral Transform (PIT), which serves as an "honesty check" to determine if the model's self-reported uncertainty spread is statistically consistent with the observed data.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Ideal Performance (Top-Left): Characterized by Low CRPS and a Flat PIT. The model is both sharp and honest: samples are tightly clustered around the target $\vect{y}^\star$, and the model’s internal uncertainty perfectly matches the true dispersion of the objective space.
    
    \item Diffuse but Calibrated (Top-Right): This scenario shows a Flat PIT but High CRPS. While the model is "honest" about its uncertainty, its predictions are too vague to be useful. The samples cover the target correctly on average, but the high variance makes it difficult to pinpoint a precise design.

    \item Overconfidence (Second Row): This represents the "Overconfident" failure mode. In the Low CRPS case, the model is precise but slightly off-target; because its distribution is too narrow (U-shaped PIT), it fails to realize it has missed the target. In the High CRPS case (Overconfident Bias), the model is "sure" of a result that is far removed from the ground truth, indicating a fundamental failure in the learned mapping.

    \item Underconfidence (Third Row): These cases show a Hill-shaped PIT. The model is effectively "hedging its bets" by producing a distribution that is wider than necessary. Even when the samples are centered on the target (Underconfident Hill), the lack of sharpness results in a poor CRPS, indicating that the model is being overly conservative.

    \item Systematic Bias (Bottom Row): These signatures reveal a displacement of the entire sample cloud. Whether the predictions are sharp (Sharp Bias) or inconsistent (Large Bias), the displacement from $\vect{y}^\star$ causes the PIT to skew. This suggests the model has failed to integrate the conditional constraints, leading to a systematic drift where the true target consistently falls in the extreme tails of the predicted distribution.
\end{itemize}

Through this framework, we can identify whether a model’s poor performance stems from a lack of predictive capacity (Bias), an inability to refine its search (Sharpness), or a fundamental misunderstanding of its own error margins (Calibration).
