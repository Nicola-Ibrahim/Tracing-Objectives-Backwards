\chapter{Background and Motivation}\label{chap:Background}


The Related Work chapter reviewed a range of approaches that, despite differing in mechanism, confront the same underlying difficulty: the mapping from decisions to objectives is comparatively easy to evaluate, while the reverse direction is generally not a well-behaved inversion. Inverse decision mapping begins with this asymmetry. Forward models can be executed by analysis, simulation, or experiment; in contrast, recovering decisions from specified outcomes must contend with non-uniqueness, sensitivity to perturbations, and, in some cases, unattainable targets~\cite{Stuart2010}. The consequence is practical as well as theoretical: inverse design is more naturally treated as an inference task than as an algebraic operation.


Classical inverse-problems theory addresses this ill-posedness by injecting additional information that stabilizes the reconstruction, for example through explicit regularization or Bayesian formulations that encode admissible structure and temper sensitivity to noise and modeling mismatch~\cite{Stuart2010}. In design settings, this viewpoint clarifies why inverse answers depend on modeling choices: the solution is determined not only by the specified outcome but also by the assumptions used to restrict, prioritize, or stabilize candidate decisions.


Recent work shifts how these assumptions are represented in practice. Rather than encoding all structure through handcrafted analytic penalties alone, learning-based approaches use collections of observed decision--outcome pairs to capture recurring patterns in feasible designs and to support fast inverse querying at deployment~\cite{Liu2025Review}. Reviews of data-driven inversion consistently emphasize hybrid pipelines, where a learned component proposes candidate decisions while a trusted forward operator anchors validity by enforcing data consistency and enabling post-hoc verification~\cite{Liu2025Review}. In a multi-objective setting, this forward anchoring has an additional role: it defines how candidate decisions are judged against a user target in objective space, which provides a common basis for ranking and selection even when the inverse model is imperfect.



This propose-and-verify perspective is especially important when the inverse relationship is one-to-many. Multiple distinct decisions may satisfy the same specification exactly, or satisfy it approximately with different trade-offs, so reporting a single decision can hide relevant ambiguity. A more operational objective is therefore to produce a set of diverse candidates for a given target and to let forward verification identify which candidates meet the target under the chosen discrepancy measure~\cite{Liu2025Review}. This requirement, in turn, places pressure on the data used to support inverse querying: paired examples $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$ must cover the outcome region where queries will be posed, because limited coverage forces extrapolation and can lead to unreliable candidates~\cite{Liu2025Review}. The remainder of this chapter builds the foundations needed for this setting and prepares the modeling and evaluation choices used in the subsequent methodology.



\section{Towards Inverse Design}\label{sec:inverse_data_eval}

We now make the propose-and-verify viewpoint precise for inverse querying in multi-objective design. Let $\calX\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ denote the admissible decision space (including any bound and constraint structure) and let $\vect{f}\colon\calX\rightarrow\calY\subset\mathbb{R}^m$ denote the forward map that returns objective outcomes. An inverse query specifies a target outcome $\vect{y}^\star\in\calY$, and the task is to return one or more admissible decisions whose forward evaluations attain the target under a deployment discrepancy. Because the inverse relation is typically one-to-many, the central question is not how to compute a single inverse, but how to generate and exploit a set of plausible candidates that is supported by data and can be verified against the task definition.


We assume access to paired observations $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, where $\vect{x}_i\in\calX$ and $\vect{y}_i$ is the corresponding outcome (for example $\vect{y}_i=\vect{f}(\vect{x}_i)$ when a forward operator is available). The practical shift adopted in this thesis is to learn an inverse proposal mechanism from $\mathcal{D}$, while reserving correctness for forward-grounded verification. This separation reflects modern perspectives on learned inverse problems, where the learned component proposes candidates and the task definition is enforced through constraints and consistency checks rather than by deterministic inversion alone~\cite{Ongie2020}.


We express this workflow through a propose-and-verify abstraction. Given a target $\vect{y}^\star$, the inverse model generates a candidate set by sampling from a learned conditional mechanism,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),\qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:propose}
\end{equation}
where $q_\theta$ is trained on $\mathcal{D}$ to capture the observed relationship between outcomes and admissible decisions. The verification stage then filters candidates for feasibility and ranks them by target attainment. Writing $\calF(\vect{x})$ for the feasibility predicate induced by the design constraints in $\calX$, and $\mathcal{L}(\cdot,\cdot)$ for the deployment discrepancy in outcome space, the selected candidate index is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{k}\in\arg\min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}}
  \ \mathcal{L}\!\big(\vect{f}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\vect{y}^\star\big)
  \quad \text{subject to }\calF(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}).
  \label{eq:verify_select}
\end{equation}
This formulation makes responsibilities explicit. The inverse model $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ is responsible for proposing diverse candidates that are consistent with patterns in $\mathcal{D}$, while $\vect{f}$, $\mathcal{L}$, and $\calF$ define what it means to satisfy the design intent at deployment.



At this stage of the thesis, the internal form of $q_\theta$ is intentionally left open. The requirement is functional: $q_\theta$ must represent multi-modality induced by one-to-many inverse relations and must support efficient sampling to produce candidate sets of practical size. Conditional density estimators and generative models provide concrete realizations of this requirement, including flow-based constructions and score-based or diffusion-style generators, which can be used as conditional proposal mechanisms in inverse settings~\cite{Papamakarios2021}. The subsequent sections introduce the modeling viewpoints used in this thesis and specify how these choices interact with forward checks and feasibility filtering.


Finally, this workflow clarifies the role of data without requiring the inverse model to be the sole authority on validity. The learned conditional captures regularities present in the observed joint behavior of decisions and outcomes, while verification ensures that the final recommendation remains disciplined by feasibility in $\calX$ and consistency in $\calY$. In this way, inverse design becomes guided exploration in decision space driven by learned proposals and anchored by forward evaluation, which motivates the modeling and estimation choices developed next.



\subsection{Challenges}\label{sec:inverse_challenges}

As inverse design has matured across scientific and engineering domains, the practical question has shifted from whether inverse querying is possible to whether it is reliable, informative, and cost-effective when embedded in real workflows. Learning-enabled inversion sharpens this question because the output of a query is typically a \emph{set} of plausible decisions rather than a single recommendation, and the usefulness of that set must be judged under limited evaluation budgets and imperfect models. The propose-and-verify stance adopted in this thesis is intended to make these reliability questions explicit by separating candidate generation from the acceptance criteria applied at deployment.


A first challenge is that utility is defined at the level of a candidate set. When an inverse model returns multiple proposals for the same target, point-wise error summaries provide limited insight because the inverse relation is commonly one-to-many. What matters operationally is whether the returned set contains at least one admissible candidate that attains the target under the deployment discrepancy, and whether the set exposes meaningful alternatives rather than near-duplicates. This motivates evaluating inverse querying in terms that mirror deployment use: target attainment after verification, adherence to feasibility constraints, and the degree to which the candidate set represents distinct solutions implied by the inverse relation, rather than the accuracy of a single reconstructed decision.


A second challenge is that verification cannot automatically be treated as an oracle. The selection rule in \eqref{eq:verify_select} inherits any bias, under-resolution, or mismatch present in the forward evaluator, whether the evaluator is the true forward operator, an approximate simulator, or a learned surrogate used for cost reasons. Classical inverse-problems perspectives emphasize that noise and modeling discrepancy can dominate inference outcomes if they are ignored~\cite{Stuart2010}. In a propose-and-verify pipeline, a defensible interpretation is therefore to treat verification as a disciplined filter whose limitations remain visible, so that ranking and acceptance decisions can be justified even when the verifier is imperfect, rather than assuming that forward checks eliminate all error.

A third challenge is generalization under distribution shift. Training data typically covers only a subset of the joint behavior of decisions and outcomes, and changes in target regimes, operating conditions, or even the effective admissible region of $\calX$ can push queries outside the support where $q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ is reliable. Learned inverse-problem surveys highlight that such shifts can yield outputs that appear plausible while being systematically incorrect, particularly when the model has no mechanism to recognize extrapolation~\cite{Ongie2020}. This motivates treating the learned proposer as amortized guidance for exploration and relying on feasibility filtering and forward checks, together with targeted additional evaluation when necessary, to defend against out-of-distribution requests.


Finally, representation and computation determine what inverse querying can deliver in practice. Candidate throughput depends on how $\calX$ is parameterized, how efficiently $q_\theta$ can generate candidates, and how expensive verification is, creating coupled trade-offs between expressivity, tractability, and fidelity. Probabilistic modeling perspectives make these trade-offs explicit: richer conditional mechanisms can better represent multi-modal inverse relations, but may increase sampling cost and complicate reliable selection~\cite{Papamakarios2021}. For this thesis, this motivates analyzing proposal quality, verifier fidelity, and sampling strategy jointly, since improvements in one component can be negated by bottlenecks in another.

Together, these challenges motivate organizing inverse-design methods by how they allocate inductive bias between proposal and verification, and by how they manage set-level utility, mismatch, generalization risk, uncertainty, and computational cost under realistic budgets.



\subsection{Strategies}

Inverse design starts from a target response and searches the (typically vast and constrained) space of compositions, structures, and processes for feasible solutions. Relative to forward modeling, the inverse direction is often ill-posed in the sense that multiple distinct designs can correspond to the same target response, and available evaluations are commonly limited by simulation or experimental cost~\cite{KabanikhinSurvey}. These properties shape what is practical: inverse methods must balance exploration, modeling assumptions, and verification cost in order to deliver actionable candidate designs.

Across materials and photonics, three complementary strategy families are commonly distinguished: exploration-based methods, which prioritize sequential probing with minimal reliance on labeled datasets; model-based methods, which learn surrogate structure--property relationships (including inverse or generative components) to enable fast querying; and optimization-based methods, which formulate inverse design explicitly as an optimization problem over design variables and solve it with numerical search~\cite{Liu2025Review, Melati2025_InverseDesignPIC_Tutorial}. The remainder of this section summarizes their core ideas, typical algorithms, representative applications, and practical trade-offs, using the propose-and-verify lens developed earlier to highlight where each family places its main inductive bias.



% Inverse design starts from a target response and searches the (typically vast and constrained) space of compositions, structures, and processes for feasible solutions. Compared with forward modeling, inverse problems are often ill-posed (one-to-many mappings) and data-limited, amplifying concerns around generalization, uncertainty, and reproducibility in machine-learning (ML) workflows~\cite{KabanikhinSurvey}. In practice, three complementary strategy families are prevalent: exploration-based (agent- or heuristic-driven search with minimal labels), model-based (learning bidirectional or generative structure–property surrogates), and optimization-based (treating inverse design as an explicit optimization over design variables) \cite{Liu2025Review, Melati2025_InverseDesignPIC_Tutorial}. Below we outline their core ideas, typical algorithms, representative materials applications, and practical trade-offs.

\subsubsection{Exploration-based inverse design:}

Exploration-based strategies iteratively probe design regions to discover candidates that satisfy target criteria under sparse supervision. In this family, the proposer is realized by an agent or heuristic search policy, while verification is provided by a simulator, experiment, or surrogate evaluator. Figure~\ref{fig:exploration_based_inverse} illustrates this interaction: an exploration mechanism proposes new designs, a forward evaluator scores them, and the resulting feedback drives the next proposals.

Reinforcement learning (RL) agents cast inverse design as sequential decision-making, where actions modify a design and rewards reflect forward-evaluated performance. Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) provides look-ahead by expanding a decision tree of candidate modifications, and population heuristics such as Particle Swarm Optimisation (PSO) supply robust global search when gradients are unavailable. For photonic device geometry, Hwang et~al.\ combined an Advantage Actor--Critic agent (IDEA) with a critic-value-based branch tree (CVBT) to promote diversified high-performing candidates~\cite{Hwang2022_ASOC}. CASTING adapts MCTS to continuous actions for materials discovery, improving exploration efficiency in high-dimensional spaces~\cite{Banik2023_NPJCM}, and MCTS has also been used to accelerate copolymer sequence design under combinatorial growth of the search space~\cite{Patra2020_Nanoscale}. When physics solvers are expensive, or gradients are difficult to obtain, PSO remains a practical global optimizer; for example, PSO coupled with self-consistent field theory (SCFT) has been used to target bulk multiblock-copolymer morphologies that match prescribed patterns~\cite{Khadilkar2017_Macromolecules, Wang2018_SoftComput}. RL has likewise been used to guide rule-based combinatorial chemistry toward extreme-property targets that are challenging for distribution-learning approaches to reach reliably~\cite{Kim2024_ChemSci}.

A recurring strength of exploration-first methods is that they can operate without a bijective inverse map and can naturally generate diverse candidates; the main limitations are the number of forward evaluations required, sensitivity to reward or scoring design, and the need for additional mechanisms when hard constraints must be enforced.


\subsubsection{Model-based inverse design:}

Model-based strategies learn mappings from data (and, where available, physics structure) so that a target property specification can be translated into candidate designs with minimal online search. Figure~\ref{fig:model_based_inverse} summarizes the common workflow: a learned inverse or generative component proposes candidates conditioned on the target, and a forward evaluator is used to enforce property consistency, filter infeasible outputs, and support ranking.

  \label{fig:model_based_inverse}
\end{figure}



A widely used pattern is forward--inverse coupling. Liu et~al.\ proposed a tandem architecture in which an inverse network is trained through a cascaded forward predictor, so that the inverse component is rewarded for producing designs whose predicted properties match the target, even when the inverse mapping is non-unique~\cite{Liu2018_ACSPhotonics}. Alloy design workflows operationalize the same idea by pairing a property-to-composition proposer with a more reliable composition-to-property predictor and iterating proposal and validation until the target tolerance is met. Machine-learning design system (MLDS) pipelines have used this property-oriented strategy to identify candidate compositions for copper and aluminium alloys and then validate them using the forward predictor and subsequent screening~\cite{Wang2019_NPJCM,Jiang2022_JMST}.

Surrogate modeling provides a second route. Here, a regression surrogate approximates an expensive simulator or experiment-derived response, and inverse design is carried out by embedding that surrogate inside a numerical search procedure. In lithium-ion cathodes, Liow et~al.\ trained an inverse-design surrogate over expert-derived synthesis descriptors to infer processing parameters that achieve high discharge capacity, reporting forward-evaluated performance consistent with the target specification~\cite{Liow2022_NanoEnergy}. When data are scarce, transfer learning can stabilize the forward surrogate and improve downstream inversion; for example, TLOpt combines a transfer-learned predictor with global optimization to suggest compositions that match a desired optical absorption spectrum~\cite{Dong2021_CMS}.

A third model-based route uses generative representations to provide a structured design manifold. Variational autoencoders and related latent-variable models map designs into a continuous latent space that supports smooth interpolation and guided search. Kim et~al.\ used a variational autoencoder to construct a continuous microstructure space and to explore microstructures associated with desired mechanical behavior~\cite{Kim2021_MatDes}. Generative pipelines have also been demonstrated for inverse alloy design, including workflows targeting refractory high-entropy alloys~\cite{Debnath2021_JMI}, and GAN-based inverse mappers have been used to design soft composite beams that morph into prescribed shapes under actuation~\cite{Brzin2024_EAAI}.

Model-based methods are attractive because they offer amortized inference once trained and can reduce the number of expensive evaluations required online. Their practical weaknesses mirror the challenges discussed earlier: they must manage non-uniqueness through appropriate conditioning and validation, remain robust to distribution shift when queries move outside the data-supported region, and avoid over-reliance on approximate forward evaluators when verification fidelity is limited.



\subsubsection{Optimization-based inverse design:}

Optimization-based strategies pose inverse design as an explicit numerical optimization problem over decision variables, where the objective measures mismatch to a target response (for example, error to a desired spectrum) and constraints encode admissibility. Figure~\ref{fig:optimization_based_inverse} depicts the common loop: an optimizer proposes new designs, a forward evaluator scores them, and the resulting feedback guides subsequent proposals until a stopping criterion is met.

Bayesian optimization (BO) is often used when evaluations are expensive and gradients are unavailable. It builds a probabilistic surrogate of the objective and uses an acquisition rule to decide which candidate to evaluate next. In crystal discovery, Zuo et~al.\ combined BO with a graph-based energy model and symmetry-constrained relaxation to screen a large transition-metal boride and carbide space for ultra-incompressible hard materials, reporting synthesis and mechanical characterization consistent with the model-guided screening~\cite{Zuo2021_MaterialsToday}. Active-learning loops based on BO have likewise been used to reduce experimental iteration in alloy development; for example, Mi et~al.\ report a Bayesian-optimization active-learning workflow for designing Mg--Mn-based wrought magnesium alloys with experimentally measured mechanical properties~\cite{Mi2024_JMagAlloys}. Related Bayesian formulations have also been demonstrated for inverse design of acoustic metamaterials, where a Gauss--Bayesian model is used to propose designs for prescribed absorption targets and the resulting designs are validated experimentally~\cite{Zheng2020_JAP}. For molecular and materials discovery, deep-learning frameworks can also be coupled to optimization objectives and constraint logic to enable attribute-driven inverse design under specified property targets~\cite{Tagade2019_NPJCM}.


Genetic algorithms remain a practical baseline, particularly for discrete, mixed, or heavily constrained decision spaces, and they are commonly hybridized with surrogate predictors to reduce evaluation cost. In photonics, Ren et~al.\ combine a genetic algorithm with a deep neural network to optimize device geometries while reducing the amount of training data required compared with earlier learning-based inverse design approaches~\cite{Ren2021_PhotonicsResearch}.

When the governing physics is differentiable, topology optimization provides a powerful PDE-constrained inverse-design tool through adjoint sensitivities. This family is widely adopted in nanophotonics, where adjoint gradients enable large-scale continuous optimization under physical constraints and manufacturing considerations~\cite{Christiansen2021_JOSAB,Jensen2011_LPR}. In exchange for this efficiency, gradient-based methods depend on reliable differentiable solvers and well-posed adjoint formulations, and their outcomes remain sensitive to parameterization and local minima.




In practice, hybrid pipelines are common because they combine complementary strengths. Exploration policies can query model-based surrogates to reduce evaluation cost, latent representations learned by generative models can be searched using BO or evolutionary optimizers, and forward--inverse couplings can screen property-to-design proposals through a trusted forward evaluator before simulation or experiment. The preferred pathway depends on the data regime, evaluation cost, constraint structure, and whether gradients of the forward model are accessible. \section{Data Acquisition}\label{sec:database}


Learning-based inverse querying relies on paired examples
\(\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}\), where outcomes are generated by a forward process,
\begin{equation}
  \vect{y}_i \;=\; \vect{f}(\vect{x}_i) \;+\; \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i,
  \qquad \vect{x}_i\in\calX,\ \vect{y}_i\in\calY .
  \label{eq:database_forward}
\end{equation}
Here \(\vect{f}\) denotes the mechanism that produces objectives from decisions (for example a simulator, an analysis model, or an experimental evaluation protocol), and \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_i\) collects measurement noise and any modelling discrepancy between the evaluator and the idealised forward relation. We treat \(\mathcal{D}\) as an empirical sample of the joint behaviour of decisions and outcomes, without committing to a single data-generation philosophy.

In practice, suitable pairs arise from two broad channels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Curated datasets: historical logs, experimental archives, or precomputed simulation sets, including design-of-experiments sweeps aimed at broad coverage of \(\calX\) and the induced outcome region in \(\calY\).
  \item Optimisation traces: decision--outcome pairs collected as a by-product of exploratory search, such as multi-objective optimisation runs, surrogate-assisted procedures, or Bayesian optimisation loops, which naturally sample trade-offs across objectives and constraints.
\end{itemize}

Because inverse decision mapping is typically one-to-many, diversity in \(\mathcal{D}\) is valuable. However, reliability is local in outcome space: a learned inverse model is most dependable when targets \(\vect{y}^\star\) lie within, or near, the outcome region represented by the data. When queries move outside this data-supported region, the proposer is forced to extrapolate and may return candidates that appear plausible but fail feasibility checks or do not attain the target under forward verification.




\section{Modeling Background}\label{sec:modelling_background}

Model-based approaches treat inverse decision mapping as data-driven inference. Rather than assuming that the reverse relation can be written as a stable, single-valued inverse of the forward map, they learn statistical structure from paired observations and use it to answer objective-space queries. This viewpoint aligns with inverse-problems theory: the inverse direction is commonly ill-posed, in the sense that solutions may be non-unique, sensitive to perturbations, or absent for some targets~\cite{Stuart2010,Ongie2020}. In decision mapping this behavior is typical, because distinct decisions can induce outcomes that are indistinguishable at the resolution implied by modeling error and measurement noise.

We describe the setting through a forward relation between a decision $\vect{x}\in\calX$ and an outcome $\vect{y}\in\calY$,
\begin{equation}
  \vect{y} \;=\; \vect{f}(\vect{x}) \;+\; \boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
  \label{eq:forward}
\end{equation}
and an inverse query that specifies a target outcome $\vect{y}^\star$ and asks for admissible decisions whose forward response matches it within the deployment notion of discrepancy,
\begin{equation}
  \text{find }\vect{x}\in\calX \text{ such that } \mathcal{L}\!\big(\vect{f}(\vect{x}),\vect{y}^\star\big)\ \text{is small}.
  \label{eq:inverse_query}
\end{equation}
This formulation makes explicit that inverse decision mapping is defined relative to the task metric $\mathcal{L}$ and the uncertainty term $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, not solely by algebraic invertibility of $\vect{f}$~\cite{Stuart2010}.

A first modeling view represents the inverse as a deterministic map $g:\calY\to\calX$, returning a single decision for a given target. This is attractive for its simplicity and speed, but it is restrictive when the inverse relation is one-to-many: multiple distinct decisions may be compatible with the same target, and a single output necessarily imposes an implicit selection rule~\cite{Ongie2020}. This motivates moving from single-point reconstruction to candidate-set generation.

A second view models the inverse relation as a conditional distribution over decisions given outcomes. In Bayesian inverse problems, this appears as a posterior over $\vect{x}$ conditioned on observations, while in data-driven settings it is natural to learn a conditional density from paired samples so that multi-modality is represented directly through conditional variability and sampling~\cite{Stuart2010,Papamakarios2021}. Under this view, an inverse query provides a mechanism for generating multiple candidates,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)} \sim q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star),\qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:candidate_sampling}
\end{equation}
rather than a single reconstruction.

Both views are typically coupled to forward-grounded verification. Candidates, whether produced by a point map or by sampling, are assessed using the same deployment discrepancy in objective space,
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}\big(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\big)
  \;=\;
  \mathcal{L}\!\big(\vect{f}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\,\vect{y}^\star\big),
  \label{eq:verify_score_bg}
\end{equation}
and can then be filtered for feasibility and ranked according to the selection rule introduced earlier in \eqref{eq:verify_select}. This coupling clarifies the modeling division adopted throughout the thesis: learning proposes, while forward consistency defines acceptance.

A persistent limitation remains independent of the chosen model class. Any learned inverse, whether point-valued or distributional, is supported by the regions of $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$ space represented in the available data; queries outside that region can yield candidates that appear plausible but fail when checked forward~\cite{Arridge2019,Ongie2020}. The following sections develop the probabilistic viewpoint and specific estimators, including mixture-based and latent-variable conditional models, within this propose-and-verify framework.



\subsection{Probabilistic View}


A probabilistic view replaces single-point inverse predictions with conditional distributions over decisions, which makes non-uniqueness explicit and provides a direct mechanism for representing one-to-many inverse relations. This matters in inverse decision mapping because ambiguity is often structural: multiple distinct decisions can satisfy the same target outcome within the resolution implied by the task discrepancy, and small perturbations in the target or the forward evaluation can change which decisions appear preferable. Modeling a conditional distribution therefore avoids forcing an arbitrary single choice and instead supports the generation of multiple plausible candidates for the same query.

Given paired observations $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$, a generic likelihood-based principle learns a conditional distribution by maximizing a regularized conditional log-likelihood,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta}
  \;\in\;
  \arg\max_{\theta}
  \Bigg[
    \sum_{i=1}^{N}\log p_{\theta}\!\big(\vect{x}_i \mid \vect{y}_i\big)
    \;-\;
    \gamma\,\mathcal{R}(\theta)
  \Bigg],
  \label{eq:prob_generic}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}(\theta)$ controls complexity and encodes modelling assumptions, and $\gamma\ge 0$ balances fit and regularization~\cite{Murphy2022PML1}. The purpose of \eqref{eq:prob_generic} is not to prescribe a specific estimator, but to highlight the modeling consequence: the output of inference is a distribution over decisions conditioned on a target, rather than a single deterministic proposal.

This distributional output supports candidate-set generation through sampling. For a query $\vect{y}^{\star}$, one draws a collection of candidates,
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x}^{(r)} \sim p_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^{\star}),\qquad r=1,\dots,R,
  \label{eq:prob_sampling}
\end{equation}
which instantiates the proposal stage of the inverse pipeline. Candidates are then ranked or filtered using the deployment discrepancy and feasibility checks, so that selection is defined by the design intent rather than by the inverse model alone. Sampling therefore separates plausibility from choice: the learned conditional determines which candidates are supported by the data, while the task metric and constraints determine which of those candidates best satisfy the target. This separation is especially valuable when the conditional is multi-modal, because it allows the procedure to surface qualitatively different alternatives instead of collapsing them into a single averaged solution.


Uncertainty is not an auxiliary statistic in this view but a functional part of the inverse pipeline. Conditional variability in $p_{\widehat{\theta}}(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^{\star})$ reflects how the model allocates probability mass across decision regions for the specified target and can guide practical choices such as how many candidates to generate and how aggressively to prune them during verification. At the same time, this variability may reflect both structural ambiguity and limitations of the learned model under finite data and misspecification, so its interpretation should be tied to forward verification and empirical checks. Likelihood-based objectives provide a principled way to fit conditional distributions and, as proper scoring rules, reward placing probability mass on outcomes realized in the data; however, well-calibrated uncertainty typically requires additional modeling choices and diagnostic validation rather than following automatically from likelihood optimization~\cite{Murphy2022PML1}.



\subsection{Mixture Density Networks}\label{sec:mdn}

Mixture density networks (MDNs) provide a direct way to model one-to-many inverse relations by learning a conditional distribution over decisions. For a fixed target outcome $\vect{y}$, the model does not return a single decision $\vect{x}$; instead, it represents several alternative solution families and how strongly each family is supported for that target. Figure~\ref{fig:mdn_architecture} illustrates this structure: a shared network takes $\vect{y}$ as input and outputs mixture weights together with component parameters, which can then be used to sample multiple decision candidates for the same query.

A shared backbone outputs mixture weights $\pi_k(\vect{y})$ and component parameters $(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y}),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y}))$, enabling multimodal sampling of decision candidates $\vect{x}$ for a fixed target.}
  \label{fig:mdn_architecture}
\end{figure}

Formally, an MDN represents the conditional density as a mixture. For a $K$-component Gaussian mixture,
\begin{equation}
  q_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})
  \;=\;
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k(\vect{y};\theta)\,
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x}\,;\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y};\theta),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y};\theta)\big),
  \label{eq:mdn_conditional}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_k(\vect{y};\theta)\ge 0$ and $\sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k(\vect{y};\theta)=1$.
Here $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y})$ locates the centre of the $k$th solution family in decision space, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y})$ describes how widely that family spreads, and $\pi_k(\vect{y})$ indicates how prominent that family is for the given target. A neural network produces these quantities from $\vect{y}$, using standard constraints such as a softmax for $\pi_k$ and positive-definite parameterizations for $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$. In higher-dimensional decision spaces, it is common to restrict $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$ to diagonal or structured forms for tractability and numerical stability.

Training aims to make observed decisions likely under the predicted conditional distribution. Given paired data $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$, MDNs are learned by maximum conditional likelihood, equivalently by minimizing the negative log-likelihood
\begin{equation}
  \theta^\star
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{\theta}\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{MDN}}(\theta)
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{\theta}\left(
  -\sum_{i=1}^{N}\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)
  \right).
  \label{eq:mdn_opt_problem}
\end{equation}
The per-sample contribution expands to
\begin{equation}
  -\log q_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i)
  \;=\;
  -\log
  \Bigg(
    \sum_{k=1}^{K}\pi_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\,
    \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x}_i;\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\big)
  \Bigg).
  \label{eq:mdn_nll}
\end{equation}
This expression gives the main intuition for why MDNs represent multiple modes. The conditional probability of $\vect{x}_i$ is a sum over components, so the loss decreases whenever at least one component assigns high density near $\vect{x}_i$ for the given $\vect{y}_i$. Over the dataset, different components can therefore specialize to different solution families instead of forcing a single averaged prediction~\cite{bishop1994mixture}. In practice, \eqref{eq:mdn_nll} is evaluated with numerically stable log-sum-exp computations.

A convenient way to see how training allocates responsibility across components is through the soft assignments
\begin{equation}
  \gamma_{i,k}
  \;=\;
  \frac{
    \pi_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\,
    \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x}_i;\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\big)
  }{
    \sum_{j=1}^{K}\pi_j(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\,
    \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x}_i;\boldsymbol{\mu}_j(\vect{y}_i;\theta),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j(\vect{y}_i;\theta)\big)
  }.
  \label{eq:mdn_responsibilities}
\end{equation}
Each $\gamma_{i,k}$ measures how strongly component $k$ explains the observation $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$ under the current parameters. Components with larger responsibility for a sample receive a stronger update that moves $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y}_i)$ toward $\vect{x}_i$ and adjusts $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y}_i)$ to reflect local variation. Repeating this across samples produces a competition-and-specialization effect in which different components become associated with different modes of the conditional distribution.

Once trained, candidate generation follows directly from the mixture representation. For a query $\vect{y}^\star$, one samples a component index
$k \sim \mathrm{Categorical}(\pi_1(\vect{y}^\star),\dots,\pi_K(\vect{y}^\star))$
and then samples a decision
$\vect{x}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k(\vect{y}^\star),\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k(\vect{y}^\star))$.
Repeating this procedure yields a candidate set whose diversity depends on both the mixture weights and the component spreads. MDNs therefore provide an explicit, likelihood-trained mechanism for multimodal conditional candidate generation~\cite{bishop1994mixture}.

In practice, model quality depends on the choice of $K$ and on stabilizing the component spreads. Without appropriate constraints or regularization, mixtures may develop collapsed components with very small variances or may leave some components effectively unused. These behaviors motivate common design choices such as restricting covariance structure, adding mild variance floors, or regularizing mixture parameters to maintain robust sampling behavior.

% In inverse design, this capability is valuable because the property-to-decision map is rarely one-to-one. Different compositions, microstructures, or process conditions can yield similar target properties, and an MDN can return multiple distinct candidates for the same target property vector. The mixture weights then indicate which candidate families are emphasized by the learned conditional, which supports downstream screening and constraint checking.


\subsection{Conditional Variational Autoencoders}\label{sec:cvae}


Conditional variational autoencoders (CVAEs) represent conditional variability through a latent variable $\vect{z}\in\mathbb{R}^d$. Instead of modeling the conditional density $p(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$ directly, a CVAE introduces $\vect{z}$ as a compact source of variation and defines the conditional model by marginalizing $\vect{z}$ ~\cite{Sohn2015CVAE},
\begin{equation}
  p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})
  \;=\;
  \int p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})\,p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y})\,\mathrm{d}\vect{z}.
  \label{eq:cvae_marginal}
\end{equation}
This construction is useful for inverse decision mapping because holding the target $\vect{y}$ fixed while varying $\vect{z}$ yields multiple candidates that remain tied to the same conditioning context. Figure~\ref{fig:cvae_architecture} summaries both the training pathway, where an encoder infers $\vect{z}$ from $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$, and the deployment pathway, where candidates are generated by sampling $\vect{z}$ and decoding with $\vect{y}^\star$ fixed.


\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \input{figures/cvae_architecture}
  \caption[CVAE architecture]{Conditional VAE for candidate generation. An encoder learns a latent representation from $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$ during training, and a decoder generates $\vect{x}$ from $(\vect{y},\vect{z})$; diversity comes from sampling $\vect{z}$ while holding $\vect{y}^{\star}$ fixed.}
  \label{fig:cvae_architecture}
\end{figure}


In practice, the decoder $p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})$ and the prior $p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y})$ are parameterized by neural networks. A common choice for continuous decision variables is a Gaussian decoder,
\[
  p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})
  \;=\;
  \mathcal{N}\!\big(\vect{x};\,g_\theta(\vect{y},\vect{z}),\,\sigma_x^2 \mathbf{I}\big),
\]
so that the reconstruction term corresponds (up to constants) to a weighted squared error between $\vect{x}$ and the decoder output $g_\theta(\vect{y},\vect{z})$. For the prior, one may use a standard normal independent of $\vect{y}$, or a learned Gaussian whose parameters depend on $\vect{y}$; conditional priors can better match heterogeneous conditional variability in inverse mappings~\cite{Sohn2015CVAE}.



Because the marginal likelihood in \eqref{eq:cvae_marginal} is generally intractable, training proceeds by maximizing an evidence lower bound (ELBO) using an approximate posterior (encoder) $q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$:
\begin{equation}
  \log p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})
  \;\ge\;
  \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})}\!\big[\log p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y},\vect{z})\big]
  \;-\;
  \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})\ \|\ p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y})\big),
  \label{eq:cvae_elbo}
\end{equation}
so the optimization target is the negative of the right-hand side summed over the dataset. The first term encourages the decoder to explain $\vect{x}$ well given $(\vect{y},\vect{z})$, while the KL term regularizes the encoder toward the prior so that sampling remains meaningful and consistent with the generative model~\cite{Sohn2015CVAE}.



Candidate generation follows directly from the generative structure. For a query $\vect{y}^\star$, sample $\vect{z}\sim p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y}^\star)$ and then decode $\vect{x}\sim p_\theta(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y}^\star,\vect{z})$, repeating to obtain multiple candidates. Conditioning fixes the target context, while variation in $\vect{z}$ induces diversity in $\vect{x}$ under a fixed $\vect{y}^\star$.


For implementation, it is convenient to write the bound per data point and then sum over the dataset, which yields the standard negative-ELBO training loss.


To make the optimization explicit, define the per-sample ELBO
\[
  \mathcal{J}_i(\theta,\phi)
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)}\!\big[\log p_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z})\big]
  \;-\;
  \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\ \|\ p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y}_i)\big),
\]
and the training problem
\begin{equation}
  (\theta^\star,\phi^\star)
  \;=\;
  \arg\max_{\theta,\phi}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathcal{J}_i(\theta,\phi)
  \;\equiv\;
  \arg\min_{\theta,\phi}\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CVAE}}(\theta,\phi),
  \label{eq:cvae_opt_problem}
\end{equation}
where the negative-ELBO loss is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CVAE}}(\theta,\phi)
  \;=\;
  \sum_{i=1}^{N}
  \left(
    -\,\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)}\!\big[\log p_\theta(\vect{x}_i\mid\vect{y}_i,\vect{z})\big]
    \;+\;
    \mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\ \|\ p_\theta(\vect{z}\mid\vect{y}_i)\big)
  \right).
  \label{eq:cvae_loss}
\end{equation}



A standard parameterization uses a Gaussian encoder
$q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})=\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\vect{x},\vect{y}),\mathrm{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\phi(\vect{x},\vect{y})))$.
Directly sampling $\vect{z}\sim q_\phi(\vect{z}\mid\vect{x},\vect{y})$ would make it difficult to differentiate the loss with respect to $\phi$, because the sampling operation introduces randomness inside the computation graph. The reparameterization trick resolves this by moving the randomness into an auxiliary noise variable that does not depend on $\phi$. Concretely, instead of viewing $\vect{z}$ as a random draw from a distribution whose parameters depend on $\phi$, we write $\vect{z}$ as a deterministic function of $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$ and a standard normal noise sample,
\[
  \vect{z}
  \;=\;
  \boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\vect{x},\vect{y})
  \;+\;
  \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi(\vect{x},\vect{y})\odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon},
  \qquad
  \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{I}).
\]
This representation is equivalent in distribution: drawing $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ and transforming it produces the same $\vect{z}$ distribution as sampling from the encoder. The practical benefit is that $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ is independent of the network parameters, so the stochasticity is injected from outside and the mapping from $(\vect{x},\vect{y})$ to $\vect{z}$ becomes differentiable with respect to $\phi$. As a result, the expectation in Eq.~\eqref{eq:cvae_loss} can be approximated by Monte Carlo samples of $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ while still allowing gradients to propagate through $\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi$ during backpropagation~\cite{Rezende2014VAE}. In practice, a small number of samples per data point is sufficient, and $(\theta,\phi)$ are optimized using stochastic gradient methods.


% In inverse design problems, the property-to-decision relation is typically nonlinear and non-unique: distinct structures or process parameters can satisfy the same target property vector. CVAEs are attractive in this setting because they learn a structured latent representation that supports one-to-many conditional generation. Holding $\vect{y}^\star$ fixed while sampling $\vect{z}$ yields diverse candidates that remain tied to the target context, which supports inverse exploration by surfacing alternative design routes for downstream screening and constraint checking. As with other conditional generators, CVAE samples serve as proposals that are subsequently filtered and ranked by feasibility checks and forward verification under the deployment discrepancy.

\subsection{Invertible Neural Networks}\label{sec:inn}

Invertible neural networks enforce an exact bijection by augmenting the target with latent variables that capture the information not determined by the conditioning signal. This is particularly useful in inverse design settings, where the forward map can be well-defined but information-losing: many distinct designs can yield essentially the same target response. Rather than forcing a single best guess in such one-to-many regimes, INNs introduce a latent variable $\vect{z}$ to represent the missing degrees of freedom and to make sampling of alternative solutions explicit.

Figure~\ref{fig:inn_architecture} provides the high-level view: the network learns an invertible transformation that maps a decision $\vect{x}$ to an outcome prediction together with a latent code, and inverse querying is performed by fixing the target $\vect{y}^\star$, sampling $\vect{z}$, and applying the inverse pass to recover candidates in decision space.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \input{figures/inn_architecture}
  \caption[INN architecture]{Invertible neural network with latent augmentation. The model learns a bijection between $\vect{x}$ and $(\vect{y},\vect{z})$, so candidates are generated by fixing $\vect{y}^{\star}$, sampling $\vect{z}$, and applying the inverse pass to obtain $\vect{x}$.}
  \label{fig:inn_architecture}
\end{figure}


A common construction defines an invertible map between $\vect{x}$ and the pair $(\vect{y},\vect{z})$,
\begin{equation}
  (\widehat{\vect{y}},\vect{z}) \;=\; f_\theta(\vect{x}),
  \qquad
  \vect{x} \;=\; f_\theta^{-1}(\vect{y},\vect{z}),
  \label{eq:inn_bijection}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{z}$ is assigned a simple reference distribution, typically $\mathcal{N}(\vect{0},\mathbf{I})$.
To make such an invertible mapping well-defined, dimensions must match, meaning $\dim(\vect{x})=\dim(\vect{y})+\dim(\vect{z})$. When this does not hold directly, implementations commonly pad $\vect{x}$ with additional channels (often zeros) so that the overall transformation remains bijective under the chosen architecture~\cite{Ardizzone2019INN}. The practical goal is then to learn parameters $\theta$ such that: (i) the forward pass predicts outcomes consistently, $\widehat{\vect{y}}\approx \vect{y}$; and (ii) the latent output behaves like a clean reservoir of remaining variability, matching the chosen base distribution and avoiding spurious dependence on $\vect{y}$ that should be explained by the forward relation.

Training couples a supervised term that forces the forward pass to match the observed $\vect{y}$ with a distribution-matching term that shapes the latent variables. One representative objective is
\begin{equation}
  \min_\theta\;
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{INN}}(\theta)
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,
    \|\vect{y}-f_{\theta,y}(\vect{x})\|_2^2
  \right]
  \;+\;
  \lambda_z\,
  \mathrm{MMD}\!\Big(q_\theta(\vect{y},\vect{z}),\; p(\vect{y})\,p(\vect{z})\Big),
  \label{eq:inn_loss}
\end{equation}
where $q_\theta(\vect{y},\vect{z})$ denotes the joint distribution induced by pushing data through the forward direction of the network and $\mathrm{MMD}$ is a kernel-based discrepancy between distributions. In words, the first term enforces forward consistency, while the second term encourages the induced joint to behave like an independent product $p(\vect{y})p(\vect{z})$, so that the network explains what it can through $\vect{y}$ and delegates remaining variability to $\vect{z}$ shaped to match a simple prior. In practice, the distribution-matching term is often applied so that gradients do not propagate through the $\vect{y}$ coordinates of $(\widehat{\vect{y}},\vect{z})=f_\theta(\vect{x})$, which helps the MMD term primarily shape $\vect{z}$ without degrading forward prediction quality~\cite{Ardizzone2019INN}. The same reference also describes an additional convergence aid: a further MMD term that matches inverse-generated samples $\vect{x}=f_\theta^{-1}(\vect{y},\tilde{\vect{z}})$ to the empirical distribution of $\vect{x}$; this term is redundant at optimum but can improve stability during training~\cite{Ardizzone2019INN}.

A standard definition is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MMD}^2(P,Q)
  \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_{u,u'\sim P}[k(u,u')]
  \;+\;
  \mathbb{E}_{v,v'\sim Q}[k(v,v')]
  \;-\;
  2\,\mathbb{E}_{u\sim P,\ v\sim Q}[k(u,v)],
  \label{eq:mmd_def}
\end{equation}
for a characteristic kernel $k$. In implementation, we minimize an empirical approximation of \eqref{eq:inn_loss} over a dataset $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ using minibatches $\mathcal{B}$:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\theta}
  \;=\;
  \arg\min_{\theta}\;
  \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{i\in\mathcal{B}}
  \|\vect{y}_i-f_{\theta,y}(\vect{x}_i)\|_2^2
  \;+\;
  \lambda_z\,
  \widehat{\mathrm{MMD}}^2\!\Big(\{(\widehat{\vect{y}}_i,\vect{z}_i)\}_{i\in\mathcal{B}},\ \{(\vect{y}_i,\tilde{\vect{z}}_i)\}_{i\in\mathcal{B}}\Big),
  \label{eq:inn_empirical_objective}
\end{equation}
where $(\widehat{\vect{y}}_i,\vect{z}_i)=f_\theta(\vect{x}_i)$ are samples from the model-induced joint and $\tilde{\vect{z}}_i\sim\mathcal{N}(\vect{0},\mathbf{I})$. To approximate samples from the product distribution $p(\vect{y})p(\vect{z})$, one can pair observed $\vect{y}_i$ with independently drawn $\tilde{\vect{z}}_i$ (or equivalently, shuffle $\vect{z}$ across the batch to break dependence). A commonly used unbiased minibatch estimator is
\begin{align}
  \widehat{\mathrm{MMD}}^2(P,Q)
  \;=\;&
  \frac{1}{m(m-1)}\sum_{i\neq j} k(u_i,u_j)
  \;+\;
  \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\sum_{i\neq j} k(v_i,v_j)
  \;-\;
  \frac{2}{mn}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n} k(u_i,v_j),
  \label{eq:mmd_unbiased_estimator}
\end{align}
with $\{u_i\}_{i=1}^{m}\sim P$ and $\{v_j\}_{j=1}^{n}\sim Q$. Because all terms are differentiable in $\theta$ through $u_i=f_\theta(\vect{x}_i)$, we optimize \eqref{eq:inn_empirical_objective} with standard stochastic gradient methods by backpropagating through the forward pass,
\begin{equation}
  \theta_{t+1}
  \;=\;
  \theta_t \;-\; \eta_t\,\nabla_{\theta}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{INN}}(\theta_t),
  \label{eq:inn_sgd_update}
\end{equation}
where $\eta_t$ is the step size (in practice typically via an adaptive optimiser).

It is worth noting that many INN architectures are built from coupling blocks that make both $f_\theta$ and $f_\theta^{-1}$ cheap to evaluate. In related flow-based formulations, one may also replace distribution matching with an explicit likelihood objective derived from the change-of-variables formula; the appeal is the same: a tractable training signal, exact sampling, and a latent space whose structure controls diversity.

Candidate generation is explicit and does not require approximate inference: for a query $\vect{y}^\star$, sample $\vect{z}\sim \mathcal{N}(\vect{0},\mathbf{I})$ and compute $\vect{x}=f_\theta^{-1}(\vect{y}^\star,\vect{z})$, repeating to obtain a diverse candidate set. The invertibility constraint makes the mapping from latent variation to candidate diversity transparent and supports stable sampling for fixed conditioning. In inverse design contexts such as materials or photonic structures, where distinct microstructures can match the same target property vector, this latent-augmented inverse provides a principled way to return multiple high-fidelity alternatives rather than collapsing to an averaged, non-physical design~\cite{Ardizzone2019INN}.

Together they provide a concise, implementation-agnostic summary of decision quality that is easy to interpret and straightforward to reproduce.
