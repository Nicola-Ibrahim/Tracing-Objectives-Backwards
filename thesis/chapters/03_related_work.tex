\chapter{Related Work}\label{chap:related}

This chapter reviews research that reverses the conventional direction of multi-objective design. Instead of evaluating objective values produced by candidate decisions, these works begin with desired outcomes in objective space and reason backward toward decisions that may realize them. We organize the literature around three main ways inversion is used in practice: methods that embed inverse reasoning inside evolutionary multi-objective optimization, approaches that learn inverse surrogates for post-hoc and interactive querying, and probabilistic or generative formulations that explicitly represent the one-to-many nature of inverse mappings \cite{Gholamnezhad2022,Cheng2015IMMOEA,Debnath2021JMI}. This framing supports the thesis goal of tracing objectives backwards in a way that remains usable for decision support, not only for optimization performance.

We organize the discussion around approaches that embed inverse reasoning within evolutionary search, learn inverse surrogates from data for post-hoc or interactive use, adopt probabilistic or generative views to reflect non-uniqueness and uncertainty, and formalize inverse multi-objective inference to address robustness and identifiability. The goal is to clarify the assumptions these works make about data, feasibility, and uncertainty, and to distill what kind of guidance they ultimately offer to a practitioner who sets targets and seeks viable designs.

A first strand integrates inverse modeling directly into the optimisation loop so that inversion becomes a search operator rather than a post-processing tool. Early evolutionary multi-objective optimization (EMO) procedures search in decision space through variation and selection, while model-based approaches broaden this template via estimation-of-distribution algorithms, forward surrogates, and inverse models that map from objective space back to decision space \cite{Gholamnezhad2022}. IM-MOEA established this inverse-in-loop idea by fitting Gaussian-process inverse models on non-dominated solutions and applying random grouping to decompose a multivariate inverse relation into simpler components, enabling objective-space sampling to generate new decision candidates \cite{Cheng2015IMMOEA}. Decomposition-based and constrained extensions (IM-MOEA/D and IM-C-MOEA/D) preserve the same principle while aligning generation with structured subproblems and explicit constraint handling \cite{FariasA21}. For many-objective problems, subsequent work replaces Gaussian processes with random-forest regression and introduces uniform reference points to map non-dominated objective vectors more evenly back into decision space, reporting improvements in both convergence and diversity on benchmark suites \cite{Gholamnezhad2022}. These methods show that inverse reasoning can accelerate exploration, but they remain tightly coupled to population dynamics and iteration budgets, which motivates the thesis emphasis on inversion that can be queried independently of a full optimization run.

Subsequent model-based approaches are commonly grouped into three strands: estimation-of-distribution algorithms (EDAs), surrogate (forward) modeling, and inverse modeling, where a mapping is learned from objective space back to decision space \cite{Gholamnezhad2022}. Inverse modeling within the optimization loop was established by IM-MOEA, which builds Gaussian-process (GP) inverse models on non-dominated solutions and uses a random grouping strategy to decompose the multivariate inverse into univariate components before sampling objectives to generate new decisions \cite{Cheng2015IMMOEA}. Decomposition-based and constrained extensions (IM-MOEA/D and IM-C-MOEA/D) adapt this idea to structured subproblems and constraint handling \cite{Farias2024IMCMOEAD}. For many-objective problems (MaOPs), recent work replaces GP with random-forest (RF) regression and introduces uniform reference points to map all non-dominated points from the Pareto front (PF) to the decision space (PS), reporting gains in both convergence and diversity on standard benchmarks \cite{Gholamnezhad2022}.


A second strand treats inversion as an offline capability trained once from archived decision--outcome pairs and then used interactively to answer user-specified targets. This changes the center of gravity from improving an optimizer’s internal search to supporting rapid, repeated queries at evaluation time, which is closer to how practitioners use design tools in exploratory workflows. However, because the inverse map is generally non-unique and data coverage may be uneven, trustworthy use depends on pairing inverse proposals with feasibility screening and forward validation, whether through simulation or a forward surrogate \cite{Gholamnezhad2022}. This is directly aligned with the thesis objective: enabling real-time inverse exploration while keeping the loop grounded through checks that protect against infeasible or target-inconsistent recommendations.



% A complementary direction uses deep generative models to learn conditional distributions over decisions given target outcomes, enabling the sampling of diverse candidates rather than regressing a single solution. Variational autoencoders and conditional generative adversarial networks capture the one-to-many relation and can produce novel, target-consistent proposals. Representative demonstrations include conditional generation for high-entropy refractory alloys \cite{Debnath2021JMI}, guidance on reversible/periodic encodings for inorganic solids \cite{Chen2021JMI}, encoder–decoder models for photonic and mechanical metamaterials with semi-supervised training \cite{Ma2019AdvMater}, rapid pipelines that translate prescribed mechanical responses into printable architectures \cite{Ha2023NatComm}, and frameworks that generate bulk metallic glasses aligned with target properties \cite{Zhou2023NPJCM}. These studies highlight the appeal of generative inverse modeling—diversity and novelty—while underscoring practical cautions such as representation invertibility, mode collapse, and the need to vet samples with predictive or experimental checks.


A complementary line of work addresses non-uniqueness more explicitly by learning conditional distributions over decisions given target objective values, rather than predicting a single decision per target. Conditional variational autoencoders and conditional generative adversarial networks are commonly used to represent one-to-many mappings and to sample diverse candidates that meet the same target specification \cite{Debnath2021JMI,Ma2019AdvMater}. Representative demonstrations include conditional generation for high-entropy refractory alloys \cite{Debnath2021JMI}, treatment of reversible and periodic representations for inorganic solids \cite{Chen2021JMI}, encoder--decoder models for photonic and mechanical metamaterials with semi-supervised training \cite{Ma2019AdvMater}, pipelines mapping prescribed mechanical responses to manufacturable architectures \cite{Ha2023NatComm}, and generative frameworks for bulk metallic glasses aligned with target properties \cite{Zhou2023NPJCM}. Across these studies, the central benefit is that sampling naturally returns sets of alternatives, while recurring cautions concern representation validity, training pathologies such as mode collapse, and the need to verify generated designs using predictive or experimental checks \cite{Debnath2021JMI,Ma2019AdvMater,Ha2023NatComm}. This evidence reinforces the thesis stance that inverse exploration should prioritise diversity but must remain coupled to verification mechanisms to be dependable.


Inverse multi-objective optimization (IMOP) addresses a different inverse task and is therefore informative for scope-setting. Rather than mapping a new target outcome $\mathbf{y}^\ast$ to a decision, IMOP starts from observed (approximately) Pareto-optimal decisions attributed to a decision maker and infers parameters $\theta$ of a parametric multiobjective decision-making problem \cite{DongZeng2020ICML,DongZeng2021AAAI}. A distributionally robust formulation, WRO--IMOP, constructs a Wasserstein ambiguity set around the empirical distribution of observed decisions and minimises worst-case expected loss over that set, yielding finite-sample excess-risk guarantees and supporting semi-infinite reformulations solved via a cutting-plane method with finite-iteration convergence \cite{DongZeng2020ICML,DongZeng2021AAAI}. While this line does not directly provide target-to-decision answers, it formalises robustness and identifiability concerns that also arise in inverse exploration when data is limited or when observed solutions only approximately reflect Pareto behaviour \cite{DongZeng2020ICML,DongZeng2021AAAI}. This perspective informs the thesis by motivating safeguards and uncertainty-aware reasoning around inverse suggestions, even when the primary goal is target-driven querying.

A distributionally robust formulation, WRO–IMOP, places a Wasserstein ball around the empirical distribution of observed decisions and minimizes the worst–case expected loss over that ambiguity set. This yields finite–sample excess–risk guarantees with sublinear convergence rates, admits semi–infinite reformulations, and is solved via a cutting–plane algorithm with finite–iteration convergence; demonstrations include a synthetic multiobjective quadratic program and a real portfolio selection task \cite{DongZeng2020ICML,DongZeng2021AAAI}. While IMOP does not map a new target outcome $\mathbf{y}^\ast$ directly to a decision, it informs preference inference and robustness under model misspecification and limited data.


Overall, the literature differentiates mainly by where inversion is performed and how non-uniqueness is handled. Inverse-in-loop EMO uses inverse models to steer the search process. Offline inverse surrogates emphasize interactive querying and thereby elevate the role of feasibility and forward validation. In contrast, probabilistic or generative approaches model the one-to-many nature of inverse relations by sampling diverse candidate sets. In line with this landscape, this thesis adopts the following pathway: it trains an inverse model once, answers objective-space queries, and closes the loop through feasibility filtering and forward checks when available, thereby translating inverse design ideas into practical decision support.
