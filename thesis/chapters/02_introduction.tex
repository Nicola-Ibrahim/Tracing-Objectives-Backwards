\chapter{Introduction}

Design and analysis problems in science and engineering are increasingly driven by computational models: we choose a set of controllable variables, evaluate the resulting behavior through analysis or simulation, and then iterate until the observed performance is acceptable. In many modern settings this workflow is complicated by three factors. First, the relationship between settings and outcomes is nonlinear and high-dimensional, so local intuition is often unreliable. Second, performance is rarely captured by a single number: designers must balance multiple objectives and constraints, and the desired outcome is better described as a target pattern of metrics than as one optimal point. Third, forward evaluation can be expensive, which makes repeated trial-and-error and repeated end-to-end optimization costly when targets change or when interactive exploration is required.

These pressures shift attention from forward prediction to target-driven design. Rather than asking only what a given configuration produces, practitioners often begin with a required or observed outcome profile and ask which configurations could plausibly realize it, and whether multiple distinct configurations can achieve essentially the same goal. This “backward” perspective is central to inverse design and underpins the thesis focus on tracing objectives backwards from outcome space to decision space.

Across science and engineering, we often specify or observe effects and then ask what causes or controllable settings could have produced them. Inverse problems formalize this reversal: the forward direction from parameters to outcomes may be clear through modeling or simulation, yet the reverse direction from outcomes back to plausible parameters can be ambiguous, unstable, or computationally demanding. This tension motivates a large body of work on regularization and probabilistic formulations that restore robustness and represent uncertainty when direct inversion is not available or not meaningful.


The same structure appears in many engineered systems where performance is easy to measure or specify in the outcome domain, but difficult to express directly in terms of underlying parameters. Signal-processing design provides familiar examples: one may prescribe a frequency-response template and seek filter coefficients, specify a beam pattern and seek array weights, target a rate--distortion operating point and seek encoder parameters, or work backwards from desired detection behavior to system settings. In these cases, forward evaluation is typically straightforward, while the inverse step---turning an outcome specification into actionable decisions---often determines practical usability.

To describe this setting, let $\calX$ denote the space of controllable decisions $\vect{x}$ and let $\calY$ denote the space of measurable or computable performance descriptors $\vect{y}$. A forward model provides a mapping
\begin{equation}
  f:\calX \to \calY,\qquad \vect{y}=f(\vect{x}),
\end{equation}
implemented by analysis, simulation, or experiment. Inverse design asks the complementary question: given a target pattern $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\calY$, identify decisions $\vect{x}\in\calX$ whose forward outcomes match $\vect{y}^{\star}$ closely enough for the application. This inverse relationship is rarely a straightforward inversion. Hadamard characterizes a problem as well posed when a solution exists, is unique, and depends continuously on the data; inverse problems frequently violate one or more of these conditions~\cite{KabanikhinSurvey}. Instability is particularly common: small errors in measured or simulated outcomes, or small changes in the target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, can correspond to large changes in inferred decisions. Practical inverse design therefore introduces additional structure to obtain robust solutions, most commonly through regularization that encodes preferences and stabilizes inference, or through statistical formulations that represent uncertainty and non-uniqueness directly~\cite{Stuart2010}.


When an explicit inverse is unavailable, many workflows answer inverse questions indirectly. A designer perturbs $\vect{x}$, re-evaluates the forward model, and repeats until the observed outcomes satisfy the specification. While effective in low-dimensional and cheap-to-evaluate settings, this strategy becomes expensive when forward evaluations are costly and offers limited insight into the inverse relationship itself. In particular, it does not naturally indicate when a target is infeasible, when multiple distinct designs satisfy essentially the same outcome profile, or how sensitive candidate designs are to small changes in the specification.


This thesis studies an alternative approach that makes inverse exploration more direct by learning from forward-generated data. Given representative examples $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ produced by the forward model, we learn an inverse surrogate
\begin{equation}
  \hat{g}:\calY \to \calX,
\end{equation}
that can be queried with a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ to propose candidate decisions intended to realize that target. Modern machine learning models are well suited to this role because they can approximate complex, high-dimensional nonlinear relationships from data and provide fast proposals at query time. When the inverse relationship is inherently multi-valued, probabilistic and generative modeling ideas offer a principled way to represent families of plausible decisions and to express ambiguity arising from noise, modeling mismatch, or limited data, rather than collapsing the problem to a single design point.



The inverse viewpoint becomes especially relevant in multi-criteria design, where outcomes are vectors of competing performance measures. In such settings, designers specify preferred trade-offs in objective space and then seek decisions that realize them. This naturally connects inverse querying to the geometry of the attainable objective set and, in particular, to the Pareto front that captures non-dominated trade-offs among objectives. Figure~\ref{fig:decision_objective_mapping} illustrates the relationship between the decision space and the corresponding objective space, and emphasizes how target specifications are posed and interpreted in multi-objective settings.

\section{Problem statement and purpose}

Multi-criteria design tasks require translating preferences expressed in outcome space into concrete decisions in decision space. Let $\calX$ denote the decision space and let $\calY$ denote the outcome space. A forward map $f:\calX\to\calY$ relates decisions to outcomes. The inverse decision-mapping problem asks for a rule that, given a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\calY$, returns one or more candidate decisions $\widehat{\vect{x}}\in\calX$ whose forward outcomes $f(\widehat{\vect{x}})$ align with $\vect{y}^{\star}$ under an application-relevant notion of discrepancy.

The difficulty is that, while the forward direction is typically accessible through modeling or simulation, the reverse relationship from outcomes to decisions is not directly available. In multi-objective settings, this reverse relationship can also be highly nonlinear and many-to-one: similar outcome vectors may arise from materially different decisions. These structural features make the inverse task substantially more challenging than forward evaluation and motivate methods that can support target-driven querying of the design space.

The purpose of this thesis is to develop and evaluate a methodology for inverse decision mapping from objective space to decision space in multi-objective problems. The methodology is intended to accept user-specified target objective vectors and return candidate decisions that correspond to the requested objectives when assessed through the forward map.


\section{Research questions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item How can a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, not observed in the available data, be used to infer one or more candidate decision vectors $\widehat{\vect{x}}$ that correspond to the requested objectives?
  \item How can the quality of inferred decisions $\widehat{\vect{x}}$ be evaluated with respect to $\vect{y}^{\star}$ using forward evaluation or other feedback signals in a way that is explicit and reproducible?
  \item Which modeling approaches are most effective for learning the inverse relationship from objective space to decision space, and how does their performance depend on the amount and structure of available data?
\end{enumerate}


\section{Proposed approach and contributions}

The approach starts from a dataset of paired examples $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$
generated by a forward process $\vect{y}=f(\vect{x})$ (or a consistent proxy). After applying consistent preprocessing in decision and outcome spaces, an inverse model is trained to accept a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ and to return candidate decisions in $\calX$. Because the inverse relationship is commonly one-to-many, a single query produces a set of proposals $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^K$, where $K$ controls the number of candidates and diversity is obtained through sampling or model stochasticity. Each proposal is then checked through forward verification by computing $f(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})$ and measuring agreement with the target using an outcome-space discrepancy measure $d_{\calY}(f(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}),\vect{y}^{\star})$. Results are reported using set-level summaries that reflect what a user receives from an inverse query, including best-achieved discrepancy over $K$ proposals and tolerance-based success rates across targets.

The thesis makes three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A precise formulation of inverse querying for multi-objective targets, specifying the query input, the form of the returned candidate set, and the role of forward verification in assessing candidate alignment.
  \item A reproducible experimental protocol for objective-to-decision inverse mapping, including preprocessing conventions, querying procedures, and set-based performance reporting suitable for controlled comparison.
  \item An empirical comparison of inverse-modeling strategies for learning outcome-to-decision relationships, clarifying how modeling choices affect performance under different amounts and structures of available data.
\end{itemize}





\section{Assumptions, limitations, and delimitations}
\label{sec:assumptions-limitations-and-delimitations}

The assumptions in this section apply to the inverse decision-mapping setting defined in the preceding sections, where a forward process $f:\calX\to\calY$ relates decisions to objective vectors and inverse queries are posed in objective space.

We assume access to a finite dataset of representative paired examples $\mathcal{D}=\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ generated by the forward process (or a trusted proxy consistent with it over the domain of interest). We assume that $\mathcal{D}$ provides meaningful coverage of the objective-space region in which targets are queried, and that a task-relevant discrepancy measure $d_{\calY}(\cdot,\cdot)$ is available for comparing objective vectors. We also assume that admissible decision ranges can be expressed through simple bound constraints that can be enforced during candidate generation.

The primary limitation is coverage. Targets outside, or close to the boundary of, the objective region represented in $\mathcal{D}$ can induce extrapolation and degrade the reliability of inferred decisions. A related limitation is distribution shift between the examples used to learn the inverse relationship and the targets posed at use time. Finally, because multiple decisions can correspond to similar objective vectors, learned inverse models may not recover all distinct solution alternatives, especially when the available data under-represents certain regions or modes of the decision space.

We delimit the empirical scope to a small set of benchmark problems and publicly available datasets selected to support controlled comparison and reproducible evaluation, including objective vectors of dimension $m\geq 2$. Where visualization or analysis benefits from low-dimensional representations, results may be presented using two-dimensional views or projections; however, the methodology and evaluation criteria are stated for general $m$-dimensional objective spaces. Although the modeling and evaluation principles are intended to extend beyond these delimitations, the claims in this thesis are supported only within the studied settings.

\section{Thesis structure}
The remainder of this dissertation is organized as follows. Chapter~2 reviews related work on inverse problems and inverse design, with emphasis on approaches that map from outcomes to decisions and on the challenges posed by non-uniqueness and uncertainty. Chapter~3 develops the motivation and background for the thesis, introducing the problem setting, core definitions, and the assumptions that shape the empirical scope. Chapter~4 presents the proposed methodology for inverse querying, including data preparation, the inverse-modeling setup, candidate-set generation, and the forward-verification protocol used to assess candidate quality. Chapter~5 reports the experimental study on the selected benchmark problems, describing the evaluation metrics and analyzing performance as a function of the number of generated candidates and the properties of the queried targets. Chapter~6 discusses the main findings, their implications for inverse querying in multi-objective problems, and the limitations of the studied setting, and it outlines directions for future work.
