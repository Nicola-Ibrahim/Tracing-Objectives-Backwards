\chapter{Introduction}

Design and analysis problems in science and engineering are increasingly driven by computational models: we choose a set of controllable variables, evaluate the resulting behavior through analysis or simulation, and then iterate until the observed performance is acceptable. In many modern settings this workflow is complicated by three factors. First, the relationship between settings and outcomes is nonlinear and high-dimensional, so local intuition is often unreliable. Second, performance is rarely captured by a single number: designers must balance multiple objectives and constraints, and the desired outcome is better described as a target pattern of metrics than as one optimal point. Third, forward evaluation can be expensive, which makes repeated trial-and-error and repeated end-to-end optimization costly when targets change or when interactive exploration is required.

These pressures shift attention from forward prediction to target-driven design. Rather than asking only what a given configuration produces, practitioners often begin with a required or observed outcome profile and ask which configurations could plausibly realize it, and whether multiple distinct configurations can achieve essentially the same goal. This “backward” perspective is central to inverse design and underpins the thesis focus on tracing objectives backwards from outcome space to decision space.

Across science and engineering, we often specify or observe effects and then ask what causes or controllable settings could have produced them. Inverse problems formalize this reversal: the forward direction from parameters to outcomes may be clear through modeling or simulation, yet the reverse direction from outcomes back to plausible parameters can be ambiguous, unstable, or computationally demanding. This tension motivates a large body of work on regularization and probabilistic formulations that restore robustness and represent uncertainty when direct inversion is not available or not meaningful.


The same structure appears in many engineered systems where performance is easy to measure or specify in the outcome domain, but difficult to express directly in terms of underlying parameters. Signal-processing design provides familiar examples: one may prescribe a frequency-response template and seek filter coefficients, specify a beam pattern and seek array weights, target a rate--distortion operating point and seek encoder parameters, or work backwards from desired detection behavior to system settings. In these cases, forward evaluation is typically straightforward, while the inverse step---turning an outcome specification into actionable decisions---often determines practical usability.

To describe this setting, let $\calX$ denote the space of controllable decisions $\vect{x}$ and let $\calY$ denote the space of measurable or computable performance descriptors $\vect{y}$. A forward model provides a mapping
\begin{equation}
  f:\calX \to \calY,\qquad \vect{y}=f(\vect{x}),
\end{equation}
implemented by analysis, simulation, or experiment. Inverse design asks the complementary question: given a target pattern $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\calY$, identify decisions $\vect{x}\in\calX$ whose forward outcomes match $\vect{y}^{\star}$ closely enough for the application. This inverse relationship is rarely a straightforward inversion. Hadamard characterizes a problem as well posed when a solution exists, is unique, and depends continuously on the data; inverse problems frequently violate one or more of these conditions~\cite{KabanikhinSurvey}. Instability is particularly common: small errors in measured or simulated outcomes, or small changes in the target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, can correspond to large changes in inferred decisions. Practical inverse design therefore introduces additional structure to obtain robust solutions, most commonly through regularization that encodes preferences and stabilizes inference, or through statistical formulations that represent uncertainty and non-uniqueness directly~\cite{Stuart2010}.


When an explicit inverse is unavailable, many workflows answer inverse questions indirectly. A designer perturbs $\vect{x}$, re-evaluates the forward model, and repeats until the observed outcomes satisfy the specification. While effective in low-dimensional and cheap-to-evaluate settings, this strategy becomes expensive when forward evaluations are costly and offers limited insight into the inverse relationship itself. In particular, it does not naturally indicate when a target is infeasible, when multiple distinct designs satisfy essentially the same outcome profile, or how sensitive candidate designs are to small changes in the specification.


This thesis studies an alternative approach that makes inverse exploration more direct by learning from forward-generated data. Given representative examples $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ produced by the forward model, we learn an inverse surrogate
\begin{equation}
  \hat{g}:\calY \to \calX,
\end{equation}
that can be queried with a target $\vect{y}^{\star}$ to propose candidate decisions intended to realize that target. Modern machine learning models are well suited to this role because they can approximate complex, high-dimensional nonlinear relationships from data and provide fast proposals at query time. When the inverse relationship is inherently multi-valued, probabilistic and generative modeling ideas offer a principled way to represent families of plausible decisions and to express ambiguity arising from noise, modeling mismatch, or limited data, rather than collapsing the problem to a single design point.



The inverse viewpoint becomes especially relevant in multi-criteria design, where outcomes are vectors of competing performance measures. In such settings, designers specify preferred trade-offs in objective space and then seek decisions that realize them. This naturally connects inverse querying to the geometry of the attainable objective set and, in particular, to the Pareto front that captures non-dominated trade-offs among objectives. Figure~\ref{fig:decision_objective_mapping} illustrates the relationship between the decision space and the corresponding objective space, and emphasizes how target specifications are posed and interpreted in multi-objective settings.

\section{Problem Statement and Purpose}

Multi-criteria design tasks require translating preferences expressed in outcome space into concrete decisions in decision space. Let $\calX$ denote the decision space and let $\calY$ denote the outcome space. A forward map $f:\calX\to\calY$ relates decisions to outcomes. The inverse decision-mapping problem asks for a rule that, given a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\calY$, returns one or more candidate decisions $\widehat{\vect{x}}\in\calX$ whose forward outcomes $f(\widehat{\vect{x}})$ align with $\vect{y}^{\star}$ under an application-relevant notion of discrepancy.

While forward modeling is typically accessible through simulation, surrogate modeling, or analytical approximation, the reverse relationship from outcomes to decisions is not directly available. In multi-objective settings, this inverse relationship is often nonlinear and many-to-one: similar outcome vectors may arise from materially different decisions. As a result, a unique analytical inverse rarely exists, and naive inversion strategies can lead to unstable or ambiguous solutions. Existing forward-oriented approaches therefore do not directly support target-driven querying of the decision space. These structural characteristics make the inverse task substantially more challenging than forward evaluation and motivate the development of a dedicated inverse modeling strategy.

In response to these challenges, this thesis develops and evaluates a methodology for inverse decision mapping from objective space to decision space in multi-objective problems. The proposed framework is designed to accept user-specified target objective vectors and return candidate decisions that reproduce the requested objectives when assessed through the forward map. The central objective is to construct an approach that supports reliable target attainment while accounting for the nonlinear and potentially multimodal structure of the inverse relationship.



\section{Research questions}
To address the fundamental challenges of autonomous inverse design under data sparsity, this thesis investigates the following primary research questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item How can a user-specified target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, not observed in the available data, be used to infer one or more candidate decision vectors $\widehat{\vect{x}}$ that correspond to the requested objective?
  \item How can the quality of inferred decisions $\widehat{\vect{x}}$ be evaluated with respect to $\vect{y}^{\star}$ using forward evaluation or other feedback signals in a way that is explicit and reproducible?
  \item Which modeling approaches are most effective for learning the inverse relationship from objective space to decision space?
\end{enumerate}


\section{Proposed Approach and Contributions}

This thesis approaches inverse decision mapping through a fundamental paradigm shift: transitioning from unconstrained parametric probability to geometrically bounded inverse mapping. Given the data sparsity typical of physical engineering tasks, standard deep generative models struggle to reliably approximate the inverse relationship, often hallucinating solutions across invalid topological voids. To address this, the proposed methodology constructs a data-driven geometric framework that maps user-specified target outcomes back to physically plausible decision vectors, strictly bounded by the known data manifold.

Because the inverse relationship is typically strongly nonlinear and one-to-many, the proposed framework does not aim to return a single deterministic point estimate. Instead, it treats inverse mapping as a multi-modal querying process: for a desired performance target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\calY$, the algorithm generates a set of $K$ candidate decisions $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^K$. By anchoring this generation process to exact local geometric structures and dynamically bounded search regions, the framework ensures that these diverse candidates remain mathematically safe and physically viable.

Each generated proposal is subsequently subjected to a verification contract. By evaluating a forward proxy $f(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})$ and computing an objective-space discrepancy
\[
d_{\calY}\!\left(f(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}), \vect{y}^{\star}\right),
\]
this explicit verification step separates mathematical prediction from actual physical validity. It ensures that model performance is assessed with respect to true target attainment rather than internal training losses.

Evaluation is conducted at the level of candidate sets, mirroring the practical information available to an engineer. Instead of relying on single-point averages that obscure true performance in multimodal spaces, the assessment utilizes set-level diagnostics to quantify true target attainment and structural reliability. This approach explicitly measures whether the framework can reliably satisfy strict physical tolerances while accounting for the inherent variance of the design space.

The primary contributions of this thesis are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A Geometrically Bounded Inverse Framework: The formulation of a novel inverse design algorithm that overcomes the limitations of deep generative models under data sparsity by replacing unconstrained global density estimation with exact local geometry.
    \item Safe, Target-Driven Querying: A generative candidate-proposal mechanism that produces diverse, mathematically constrained decision sets, ensuring that generated solutions do not extrapolate into invalid physical voids.
    \item Dual-Perspective Evaluation Protocol: A forward-verified testing standard that rigorously separates objective-space plausibility (the physical validity of the generated designs) from decision-space reliability (the statistical honesty of the model's uncertainty).
    \item Set-Level Assessment Methodology: An engineering-focused evaluation strategy that reflects the practical usage of autonomous design systems, quantifying true physical attainment without relying on misleading point-estimate averages.
\end{itemize}




\section{Assumptions, Limitations, and Delimitations}
\label{sec:assumptions-limitations-and-delimitations}

The assumptions in this section apply to the overarching problem of inverse decision-mapping under extreme data sparsity, where a forward physical process relates decision variables to objective outcomes.

We assume access to a finite, restricted dataset of paired examples (e.g., $N=100$). Because generating true physical data is often prohibitively expensive in real-world engineering, we assume that this limited dataset must serve as the sole foundational map for the design space. We further assume that the underlying physical relationship between decisions and objectives exhibits sufficient spatial continuity to be modeled. If the inverse relationship were entirely chaotic or discontinuous, reliable approximation would be mathematically impossible from such a sparse sample. Additionally, a task-relevant discrepancy measure is assumed available for comparing target objective vectors against generated outcomes.

The primary limitation of this research is the inherent reliance on computational proxies. Because evaluating the true physical forward process thousands of times is assumed to be impossible, model evaluation and candidate verification must rely on internal computational surrogates fitted to the sparse data. Consequently, computational target attainment guarantees mathematical alignment with the observed data manifold, but cannot absolutely guarantee real-world physical synthesis without further experimental validation. A secondary fundamental limitation is the "curse of dimensionality." As the dimensionality of the decision space increases, the volume of topological voids between sparse data points grows exponentially, limiting the scalability of exact geometric solutions to low-to-medium dimensional spaces.

We delimit the empirical scope of this thesis to a defined set of rigorous synthetic benchmarks (specifically, multi-objective landscapes like $f_5$). These environments were deliberately selected to support controlled, reproducible evaluation of dimensional scaling and data sparsity constraints. Furthermore, while traditional deep generative models (such as Variational Autoencoders and Normalizing Flows) are utilized throughout this work, their inclusion is strictly delimited to serving as comparative baselines to highlight the vulnerabilities of continuous probabilistic modeling in data-starved regimes. Although the geometric framework and evaluation principles proposed herein are intended to extend to broader engineering tasks, the empirical claims of this thesis are supported strictly within the boundaries of the studied sparse-data settings.

\section{Thesis structure}
The remainder of this dissertation is organized as follows. Chapter~2 reviews related work on inverse problems and inverse design, with emphasis on approaches that map from outcomes to decisions and on the challenges posed by non-uniqueness and uncertainty. Chapter~3 develops the motivation and background for the thesis, introducing the problem setting, core definitions, and the assumptions that shape the empirical scope. Chapter~4 presents the proposed methodology for inverse querying, including data preparation, the inverse-modeling architectures, and the protocol for generating and verifying candidate sets. Chapter~5 reports the experimental study on the selected benchmark problems, describing the diagnostic suite used for evaluation and analyzing model performance across measures of attainment, statistical reliability, and probabilistic accuracy. Chapter~6 synthesizes the main findings, discusses their implications for inverse querying in complex decision spaces, addresses the limitations of the studied setting, and outlines directions for future work.
