First, we tune each inverse-model family with an intra-model ablation study, using outcome-space target matching to identify a best-performing configuration within that family. We then freeze these best-in-class configurations and compare models head-to-head under identical data splits, target sets, and sampling protocol.

\section{bi-objective case}\label{sec:exp_coco}

\subsection{Multi-objective optimization}
A multi-objective optimisation problem (MOP) is defined by decision variables
$\vect{x}=(x_1,\dots,x_n)\in\calX\subset\R^n$ and an objective vector
$\vect{f}=(f_1,\dots,f_m)\colon \calX\to\calY\subset\R^m$. We consider minimization of all objectives.
A general constrained MOP is written as
\begin{align}
\min_{\vect{x}\in\calX}\; \vect{f}(\vect{x}) &= \big(f_1(\vect{x}),\dots,f_m(\vect{x})\big),\\
\text{s.t.}\; g_j(\vect{x}) &\le 0,\quad j=1,\dots,J,\\
h_k(\vect{x}) &= 0,\quad k=1,\dots,K.
\label{eq:mop}
\end{align}
The feasible region $\calX$ is assumed compact. Objectives may be nonlinear, non-convex, and costly to evaluate, which motivates data-driven surrogates and inverse models in later sections.

Trade-offs are formalized by Pareto dominance. For $\vect{u},\vect{v}\in\R^m$,
$\vect{u}\preceq\vect{v}$ holds if $u_i\le v_i$ for all $i$ and $u_j<v_j$ for at least one index $j$.
A feasible point $\vect{x}^\star$ is Pareto-optimal if no feasible $\vect{x}$ satisfies
$\vect{f}(\vect{x})\preceq\vect{f}(\vect{x}^\star)$. The corresponding image in objective space is the Pareto front.
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_front} illustrates this geometry for a bi-objective case, showing an example non-dominated
set (Pareto front approximation) together with a dominated region induced by a representative Pareto point.



\subsection{Problem and Data}\label{subsec:problem_data}

We generate bi-objective datasets from the COCO BBOB benchmark suite via the \texttt{cocoex} Python
interface, which exposes standardized test functions through deterministic problem instances. 
In this chapter we focus on BBOB $f_5$ (Linear Slope) because its construction yields strong
directionality and boundary effects: the optimum lies on the domain boundary and the per-coordinate
slopes increase with index, which makes target attainment in objective space sensitive to where the
inverse sampler places probability mass.  In COCO notation, the function includes an
instance-dependent bias $f_{\mathrm{opt}}$ and an instance-specific optimum location
$\vect{x}^{\mathrm{opt}}$, and the definition can be written as
\[
  f_{5}(\vect{x})
  \;=\;
  \sum_{i = 1}^{D}\big(5\,|s_i| - s_i z_i\big) + f_\mathrm{opt},
\]
where $D$ is the decision dimension, $s_i$ are signed slope coefficients that scale across
coordinates, and $z_i$ applies an instance-dependent saturation that creates flat behavior beyond the
boundary-aligned optimum direction. 

To construct a bi-objective problem, we pair two deterministic instances of the same analytic
function family:
\[
  \vect{f}(\vect{x})=\big(f^{(\mathrm{inst}=a)}(\vect{x}),\; f^{(\mathrm{inst}=b)}(\vect{x})\big),
\]
with $(a,b)$ fixed (e.g., $a=1,b=2$). COCO instances apply deterministic shifts and transformations
while preserving the underlying function definition, enabling reproducible variation under a fixed
function index.  The decision domain follows the standard BBOB bounds
(e.g., $[-5,5]^D$ for the continuous suite). 

We approximate a Pareto-relevant archive for this constructed bi-objective $f_5$ problem using
NSGA-II under a fixed evaluation budget and fixed random seeds. 
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_front} recalls Pareto dominance for bi-objective minimization: points on the
Pareto front are non-dominated trade-offs, while points in the dominated region are strictly worse in
both objectives.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering

  \end{adjustbox}
  \caption[Pareto Dominance and Bi-Objective Pareto Front]{Illustration of Pareto dominance and an
  example Pareto front (non-dominated set) for a bi-objective problem.}
  \label{fig:pareto_front}
\end{figure}

Because Pareto-only archives can be small under limited budgets, we train on a larger
optimizer-generated archive that includes both Pareto-relevant solutions and additional historically
evaluated solutions produced during the NSGA-II search. Concretely, we record $8{,}000$ evaluated
pairs $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{8000}$ from the run history and split them into
train/validation/test (80/10/10) with a fixed seed. The held-out test outcomes define inverse queries
in objective space, and (when needed) we also use small perturbations near test outcomes to probe
robustness while staying in a Pareto-relevant region.

Decision variables are scaled using the COCO domain bounds, and objective vectors are mapped into a
declared normalized objective space using the same fitted normalizer that is later applied to
user-specified targets. This keeps all scoring and ECDF reporting comparable across targets and
across estimators because every discrepancy is measured in the same coordinate system.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Objective-space diagnostics}\label{subsec:objective_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space diagnostics evaluate inverse estimators as conditional samplers used in a
propose--check loop. For a requested target outcome $\vect{y}^{\star}$, an inverse model generates a
set of $K$ decision candidates $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. Each candidate is then checked
with the forward evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ to obtain outcomes
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}=\mathcal{E}(\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)})$. Because the output is a set rather
than a single point, objective-space evaluation must specify how individual candidates are scored,
how attainment is summarized under a fixed candidate budget, and how the full candidate cloud behaves
around the target.

All objective-space computations are performed in a common normalized objective
coordinate system induced by the preprocessing pipeline. The same fitted normalizer is applied to the
training archive, the user targets, and the checked outcomes, so that discrepancy magnitudes are
comparable across objectives and consistent across estimators.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Scoring contract in normalized objective space}\label{subsubsec:objective_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ denote the requested objective vector and let
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\in\mathbb{R}^{m}$ denote the checked outcome of the $k$-th candidate, both in
the declared normalized objective space. Candidate mismatch is expressed through the target-centered
residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} - \vect{y}^{\star},
  \qquad k=1,\dots,K,
  \label{eq:objective_residual_vector}
\end{equation}
where $m$ is the number of objectives, $K$ is the candidate budget, and $\vect{r}^{(k)}$ measures
signed deviation from the target component-wise. The $j$-th component is
\begin{equation}
  r^{(k)}_{j}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \widehat{y}^{(k)}_{j} - y^{\star}_{j},
  \qquad j=1,\dots,m,
  \label{eq:objective_residual_component}
\end{equation}
so $r^{(k)}_{j}>0$ indicates that the candidate is above the target on objective $j$ in the normalized
coordinates, while $r^{(k)}_{j}<0$ indicates that it is below.

A scalar discrepancy score is obtained by applying a distance in this same normalized space. We use
the Euclidean norm as the default scoring rule,
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_{2}
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{m}\Big(r^{(k)}_{j}(\vect{y}^{\star})\Big)^{2}},
  \label{eq:objective_candidate_score}
\end{equation}
where $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm. The score $s^{(k)}$ supports ranking within a
candidate set for a fixed target: smaller values correspond to checked outcomes that land closer to
$\vect{y}^{\star}$ in the declared objective coordinates.

A small numerical example illustrates the calculation. Consider a bi-objective case ($m=2$) in which
the user target in normalized space is
$\vect{y}^{\star}=(0.40,\,0.70)$ and one checked candidate outcome is
$\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}=(0.45,\,0.62)$. The residual vector is
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  (0.45-0.40,\; 0.62-0.70)
  \;=\;
  (0.05,\; -0.08),
  \label{eq:objective_example_residual}
\end{equation}
which shows that the candidate overshoots the target by $0.05$ in the first objective and undershoots
by $0.08$ in the second objective. The discrepancy score is then
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.05^{2}+(-0.08)^{2}}
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.0025+0.0064}
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.0089}
  \;\approx\;
  0.094,
  \label{eq:objective_example_score}
\end{equation}
and candidates can be ranked by comparing their $s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ values, with the smallest
score ranked as the closest match under the declared contract.

The candidate budget $K$ is part of the evaluation interface. Increasing $K$ can improve attainment by
providing more opportunities to sample a close candidate even if the underlying conditional model is
unchanged. Therefore, all objective-space comparisons fix $K$ within an experiment, and results are
reported separately when $K$ changes.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Best-shot attainment and ECDF reporting}\label{subsubsec:objective_ecdf}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Because inverse samplers return a set, target attainment is summarized per target by the best-shot
score,
\begin{equation}
  s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \label{eq:objective_best_shot}
\end{equation}
which captures the closest checked outcome the model can deliver under budget $K$. Here
$s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ is the per-candidate discrepancy defined by the scoring contract in
Section~\ref{subsubsec:objective_contract}, and $K$ is the number of candidates generated for the
target $\vect{y}^{\star}$. Evaluating a target set
$\mathcal{T}=\{\vect{y}^{\star}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ yields the best-shot vector
$\{s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$, with one value per target.

Reporting only a single aggregate statistic of $\{s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$, such as a
mean or median, can hide important structure. In particular, two estimators can have similar central
tendency but differ substantially in how often they achieve very small discrepancies or in how large
their failures are on hard targets. To make the full attainment profile visible, we report the
empirical cumulative distribution function (ECDF) of best-shot scores across the target set,
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{F}(t)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbb{I}\!\left[s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\le t\right],
  \label{eq:objective_ecdf}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of targets in $\mathcal{T}$, $t\ge 0$ is a discrepancy threshold expressed in
the same normalized objective space as $s_{\min}$, and $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ is an indicator function
that equals 1 when the condition holds and 0 otherwise. The ECDF therefore maps each threshold $t$ to
a proportion: $\widehat{F}(t)$ is the fraction of targets whose best-shot discrepancy is at most $t$.

The ECDF supports a direct, budget-aware reading of attainment. Fix a discrepancy threshold $t$ on the
$x$-axis. The corresponding $y$-value $\widehat{F}(t)$ reports what proportion of targets are solved
to within that accuracy under budget $K$. Curves that rise faster and lie higher at small $t$ indicate
that an estimator produces close matches more frequently. Conversely, a slow rise and a long right
tail indicate that a subset of targets remains difficult even after taking the best of $K$ samples.

A small numerical example clarifies the calculation. Suppose $N=5$ targets yield best-shot scores
\begin{equation}
  \{s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{5} = \{0.01,\; 0.04,\; 0.02,\; 0.10,\; 0.03\}.
  \label{eq:ecdf_example_scores}
\end{equation}
Sorting gives $\{0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04, 0.10\}$. For threshold $t=0.03$, four of the five values are
at most $0.03$, so
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{F}(0.03) = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8.
  \label{eq:ecdf_example_value}
\end{equation}
For threshold $t=0.015$, only one value is at most $0.015$, so $\widehat{F}(0.015)=1/5=0.2$. As $t$
increases, $\widehat{F}(t)$ increases in steps until it reaches 1 at $t\ge 0.10$. In the plotted ECDF,
the $x$-axis spans the range of thresholds and the $y$-axis reports the cumulative fraction of targets
whose best-shot discrepancy falls below each threshold, which matches the interpretation used in the
objective-space figures in this chapter.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Candidate-cloud geometry: bias and dispersion}\label{subsubsec:objective_cloud}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Best-shot attainment summarizes whether a candidate set contains at least one close match, but it does
not describe how the full set behaves around the target. For decision support, the geometry of the
checked outcome cloud
$\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(1)},\dots,\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(K)}\}$ matters because it indicates whether the
inverse sampler produces a concentrated, target-centered set of alternatives or a diffuse or
systematically shifted cloud.

We compute candidate-cloud diagnostics in the same residual space used for scoring. This subsection
instantiates, for the experimental setting of this chapter, the candidate-cloud plausibility
definitions introduced in the evaluation methodology (Section~\ref{sec:eval_candidate_cloud}). In
particular, we reuse the target-centered residual representation and its associated set-level
summaries to quantify whether the generated outcomes concentrate around $\vect{y}^{\star}$ or exhibit
systematic offset and diffuse sampling behavior.

For a fixed target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, define the within-set mean residual vector
\begin{equation}
  \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}),
  \label{eq:objective_mean_residual_cloud}
\end{equation}
which represents the average offset of the candidate cloud from the target. Here
$\vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})=\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}-\vect{y}^{\star}$ is the target-centered
residual vector defined in Section~\ref{subsubsec:objective_contract}, and $K$ is the candidate budget.

Candidate-cloud geometry is summarized using two set-level statistics, following the bias and
dispersion quantities defined in Section~\ref{sec:eval_candidate_cloud}: systematic offset of the
cloud from the target and within-set dispersion around the cloud mean,
\begin{equation}
  b(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \frac{\big\lVert \bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}},
  \qquad
  v(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \mathrm{median}_{k}\;
  \frac{\big\lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})-\bar{\vect{r}}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \big\rVert_2}{\sqrt{m}}.
  \label{eq:objective_bias_dispersion}
\end{equation}
Here $m$ is the number of objectives, $\lVert\cdot\rVert_2$ is the Euclidean norm, $b(\vect{y}^{\star})$
measures the magnitude of the mean residual vector in average per-objective units, and
$v(\vect{y}^{\star})$ measures a robust within-set spread computed as the median distance of residuals
to the cloud mean. The normalization by $\sqrt{m}$ reports an average per-objective magnitude rather
than a dimension-dependent norm, which keeps values comparable across problems with different numbers
of objectives. The median aggregation in $v$ reduces sensitivity to a small number of extreme samples
and reflects typical within-set variability.

Across a target set $\mathcal{T}$, bias and dispersion yield one value per target, producing vectors
$\{b(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ and $\{v(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$. We report these using
distributional summaries analogous to best-shot attainment, which enables estimator comparisons at the
level of target coverage rather than only central tendency. In particular, ECDF curves of $b$ and $v$
summarize how frequently each estimator produces target-centered clouds (low $b$) and how frequently
it produces concentrated candidate sets (low $v$) under the declared sampling budget.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{ECDF-based hyperparameter ablation and model selection}\label{subsubsec:objective_ablation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Inverse decision mapping is sensitive to model capacity. For MDNs, capacity is governed primarily by
the number of mixture components; for CVAEs by latent dimensionality and decoder capacity; and for
INNs by the number of coupling layers and subnet capacity. To avoid attributing performance
differences to under- or over-parameterized settings, we perform an ablation study within each model
family and select one configuration per family for downstream comparison.

The ablation is reported on the representative bi-objective problem derived from COCO BBOB $f_5$
(Linear Slope). For each configuration, we evaluate objective-space attainment under a fixed
candidate budget $K$ using the scoring contract defined in Section~\ref{subsubsec:objective_contract}.
For each target $\vect{y}^{\star}\in\mathcal{T}$ we score the $K$ checked outcomes
$\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$, and summarize attainment by the best-shot score
$s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star})=\min_{k} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})$. Evaluating all targets produces a
best-shot vector $\{s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$, one value per target. We then compare
configurations using the empirical cumulative distribution function (ECDF) of these best-shot scores,
as defined in \eqref{eq:objective_ecdf}. Under this reporting, a configuration is preferred when its
ECDF rises rapidly and lies higher in the low-discrepancy region, which indicates that a larger
fraction of targets are solved to high precision under the same sampling budget.

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_ecdf_f5} compares the ablated configurations across all three model families
using ECDF curves of best-shot discrepancy $s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ under fixed budget $K$. Each
curve corresponds to one hyperparameter setting. For any threshold $t$ on the horizontal axis,
$\widehat{F}(t)$ on the vertical axis reports the fraction of targets for which the configuration
produces at least one checked candidate within discrepancy $t$. Curves that lie higher at small $t$
therefore indicate stronger near-target attainment, while separation in the right tail indicates how
often targets remain difficult even after selecting the best of $K$ samples.

  \centering

  \caption{COCO $f_5$ objective-space ablation: ECDF of best-shot discrepancy
  $s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ under fixed budget $K$. For threshold $t$, $\widehat{F}(t)$ gives the
  fraction of targets for which a configuration produces at least one checked candidate within
  discrepancy $t$. Curves that lie higher at small $t$ indicate stronger near-target attainment under
  the same sampling budget.}
  \label{fig:ablation_ecdf_f5}
\end{figure}

The ECDF view supports capacity selection in a way that is both target-wise and budget-aware. Within a
model family, moving from a low-capacity setting to a moderate-capacity setting typically shifts the
ECDF upward in the low-discrepancy regime, reflecting improved conditional expressivity and better
placement of probability mass near the requested outcomes. Conversely, configurations that are too
small often plateau early, indicating that many targets cannot be reached closely even after taking
the best of $K$ samples. Very large configurations can also degrade the curve, which is consistent
with unstable training dynamics or overly diffuse conditional sampling that reduces the frequency of
near-target hits.

Across families, the ECDF shapes can be interpreted in terms of the inductive biases of each inverse
sampler. MDNs can assign probability mass to multiple outcome-consistent regions through a mixture,
so increasing the number of components often improves the fraction of targets that admit a close hit
under a finite $K$ by representing multi-modal inverse structure. CVAEs trade off latent capacity and
decoder capacity: insufficient latent dimensionality can restrict conditional variability, while
sufficient latent capacity improves coverage of alternative designs for the same target. INNs provide
a structured bijective parameterization whose effective capacity grows with the number of coupling
layers and subnet size; when adequately parameterized they can yield sharp conditional structure, but
under-parameterization tends to manifest as early saturation in the ECDF.

Candidate-cloud statistics $b(\vect{y}^{\star})$ and $v(\vect{y}^{\star})$ are computed for all targets
as defined in Section~\ref{subsubsec:objective_cloud}, and they are used to interpret why a curve
behaves as it does rather than as the primary selection rule. In particular, a configuration can
achieve a strong best-shot ECDF either because it produces consistently target-centered clouds or
because it occasionally lands a very close hit within a diffuse cloud. Bias and dispersion help
distinguish these cases in later diagnostics, but the ablation selection itself is driven by the
best-shot ECDF profile under fixed $K$.

into the remaining objective-space comparisons. The selected settings correspond to the configurations

avoiding configurations that only improve performance at very loose thresholds.

\subsection{Decision-space diagnostics}\label{subsec:decision_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Decision-space diagnostics evaluate inverse models as conditional samplers over decisions. For each
held-out paired case $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$, we condition on $\vect{y}_i$ and draw a candidate set
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_i\}_{k=1}^{K}$. The aim is to quantify whether the sampled set behaves like
a trustworthy conditional distribution around the ground-truth decision $\vect{x}_i$. We follow the
evaluation definitions in Section~\ref{sec:eval_design} and report three complementary quantities:
probabilistic accuracy via CRPS, reliability via PIT calibration, and a scalar summary of calibration
error via MACE.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Decision-space scoring contract}\label{subsubsec:decision_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

For a test case $i$, conditioning on $\vect{y}_i$ yields $K$ decision samples
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_i\}_{k=1}^{K}$, which define an empirical conditional distribution. The
diagnostic contract is defined by how this sampled distribution is compared against the realized truth
$\vect{x}_i$.

Reliability is assessed through probability integral transform (PIT) values, as introduced in
Section~\ref{sec:eval_design}. For each case $i$ and each decision dimension $d\in\{1,\dots,D\}$, let
\[
  \widehat{x}^{(1)}_{i,d},\dots,\widehat{x}^{(K)}_{i,d}
\]
denote the $K$ sampled values of component $d$, and let $x_{i,d}$ denote the ground-truth component.
Define the PIT rank statistic
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbb{I}\!\left[\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right],
  \label{eq:pit_rank_def}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ equals 1 when its condition holds and 0 otherwise. Here $z_{i,d}\in[0,1]$
measures the empirical percentile position of the truth relative to the sampled conditional
distribution for that component. Under a calibrated conditional sampler, PIT values are approximately
uniform, so departures from uniformity indicate miscalibration.

We report calibration visually using the PIT calibration curve defined in Section~\ref{sec:eval_design}.
Let $\{z_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ denote the pooled PIT values across cases and dimensions (or across cases
after a declared pooling rule). Sorting these values yields $z_{(1)}\le\dots\le z_{(L)}$, and the
empirical calibration curve is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{G}(t)
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathbb{I}\!\left[z_\ell \le t\right],
  \qquad t\in[0,1],
  \label{eq:pit_ecdf_curve}
\end{equation}
which is the empirical CDF of PIT values. Under perfect calibration, $\widehat{G}(t)\approx t$ for all
$t$, so the curve lies close to the diagonal.

To summarize calibration with a single scalar, we report the mean absolute calibration error (MACE),
defined in Section~\ref{sec:eval_design} as the average absolute deviation of the PIT curve from the
diagonal over a grid. For a grid $\{t_j\}_{j=1}^{J}\subset[0,1]$,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{MACE}
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\big|\widehat{G}(t_j)-t_j\big|.
  \label{eq:mace_def}
\end{equation}
Smaller MACE indicates closer agreement with the ideal diagonal and therefore better reliability.

Probabilistic accuracy is assessed through the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS), also defined
in Section~\ref{sec:eval_design}. For each case $i$ and each decision dimension $d$, the empirical CRPS
of the sampled set relative to the truth is computed from the sampled values
$\{\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ and the realized truth $x_{i,d}$. Aggregating across decision
dimensions yields a per-case score,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CRPS}_i
  \;=\;
  \frac{1}{D}\sum_{d=1}^{D}\mathrm{CRPS}\!\left(\{\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\}_{k=1}^{K},\,x_{i,d}\right),
  \label{eq:crps_case_def}
\end{equation}
where lower values are better. CRPS rewards candidate sets that place probability mass near the truth
while penalizing unnecessary dispersion, so it captures the sharpness--accuracy trade-off in a single
number.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Ablation protocol, reporting, and selection}\label{subsubsec:decision_ablation}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We report the decision-space ablation on the COCO $f_5$ dataset to match the objective-space analysis
and to isolate differences attributable to model capacity rather than to changes in the data
distribution. Hyperparameter variants are evaluated within each model family under the same candidate
budget $K$ and over the same held-out test set.

For each configuration, we compute PIT values using \eqref{eq:pit_rank_def} and summarize reliability
using the PIT calibration curve \eqref{eq:pit_ecdf_curve}. We also compute MACE using \eqref{eq:mace_def}
as a scalar measure of deviation from the ideal diagonal. In parallel, we compute per-case CRPS values
using \eqref{eq:crps_case_def} to quantify probabilistic accuracy.

We report results using PIT calibration curves (Figure~\ref{fig:f5_pit_ablation}), a compact table of
MACE values (Table~\ref{tab:f5_mace_ablation}), and CRPS boxplots (Figure~\ref{fig:f5_crps_box_ablation}).
PIT curves provide a direct visual comparison of reliability. The MACE table supports concise ranking
when curves are visually close and enables straightforward tabular reporting alongside other metrics.
CRPS boxplots summarize the distribution of accuracy across held-out cases, highlighting both typical
performance and failure tails.

Configuration selection uses a reliability-first criterion. We prefer configurations whose PIT curves
lie closest to the diagonal and whose MACE is smallest. When multiple configurations exhibit similar
reliability, we then prefer those with lower and more stable CRPS distributions. This joint criterion
avoids selecting an overly sharp but miscalibrated sampler or a well-calibrated but overly diffuse
sampler. The selected configuration per family is carried forward into the remaining decision-space
evaluation.

\subsection{Discussion of Results}

Across benchmarks, Gaussian process regression emerges as the most
effective inverse modelling strategy.  Its probabilistic nature allows
uncertainty quantification and smooth interpolation, resulting in low
forward error and generational distance.  Random forests are competitive,
especially when the Pareto front is discontinuous or when training data
is limited, thanks to their ability to capture local patterns and
produce diverse candidates.  Radial basis function networks perform
reasonably well but are sensitive to the choice of centres and require
tuning; they scale poorly with high‑dimensional objectives.  Neural
networks benefit from larger training sets and yield competitive results
once overfitting is controlled.

Data scarcity has a significant impact on inverse model accuracy.
Increasing the number of training samples from 100 to 500 reduces the
RMSE by approximately 40\% across models.  Transfer learning by pooling
  samples from related problems improves performance when tasks share
  similar objective definitions~\cite{Tan2023}.  The plausibility
check effectively warns users when targets lie far from the sampled
Pareto front; in our experiments less than 5\% of queries fell into this
category, highlighting the quality of the data and the models.

Overall, the experiments demonstrate that the proposed framework can
produce high‑quality inverse predictions with modest computational
resources and enables interactive trade‑off exploration.
