\chapter{Experiments}\label{chap:experiments}

\section{Bi-Objective Case}\label{sec:exp_coco}
The selection of a bi-objective configuration for this study is motivated by the need to rigorously evaluate the inverse models' ability to capture the Pareto front while maintaining interpretability. Bi-objective problems serve as a fundamental benchmark in multi-objective optimization, providing a non-trivial trade-off surface that remains amenable to high-fidelity visualization. This dimensionality allows for a direct, intuitive assessment of how both parametric networks and geometric frameworks explore the decision space relative to the objective-space conflict. 

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data Acquisition}\label{sec:exp_coco_data}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To evaluate the inverse models, we construct a bi-objective optimization problem (MOP) using the COCO BBOB benchmark suite \cite{cocoexDocs, cocoBBOBOverview}. This section describes the problem formulation and the procedure for generating the heavily constrained, data-sparse training and testing archives.

\subsubsection{Problem Formulation ($f_5$ Sphere/Sharp Ridge)}

A general MOP involves finding a decision vector $\vect{x} \in \calX \subset \R^n$ that minimizes an objective vector $\vect{f}(\vect{x}) = (f_1(\vect{x}), \dots, f_m(\vect{x}))$. In this study, we focus on the bi-objective problem $f_5$ from the BBOB-biobj suite, which is constructed by combining two distinct unimodal landscapes: the Sphere function ($f_1$) and the Sharp Ridge function ($f_{13}$). We select this problem because it creates a fundamental tension between a smooth, separable objective and a non-separable, non-differentiable ridge. The objective vector is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \vect{f}(\vect{x}) = \big(f_1(\vect{z}),\; f_{13}(\vect{z})\big) + \vect{f}_{\mathrm{opt}},
\end{equation}

where $\vect{z}$ represents the decision vector $\vect{x}$ after applying the deterministic COCO transformations (rotation and shifting). The individual components are formulated as:

\begin{align}
    f_{1}(\vect{z}) &= \sum_{i = 1}^{D} z_i^2 \\
    f_{13}(\vect{z}) &= z_1^2 + 100 \sqrt{\sum_{i = 2}^{D} z_i^2},
\end{align}

where $D$ is the decision dimension. Unlike problems with simple trade-offs, $f_5$ is characterized by a sharp ridge in the second objective where the gradient remains constant even as the ridge is approached. This property makes the inverse task particularly difficult. Parametric samplers (like CVAEs) must accurately resolve a non-smooth manifold, while geometric frameworks (like GBPI) must successfully navigate topological voids near the ill-conditioned ridge without hallucinating impossible physics.

\subsubsection{Data Generation via NSGA-II and Extreme Sparsity}

The construction of our dataset begins with a formal definition of the optimization landscape. A multi-objective optimization problem (MOP) is characterized by decision variables $\vect{x}=(x_1,\dots,x_n)\in\calX\subset\R^n$ and an objective vector $\vect{f}=(f_1,\dots,f_m)\colon \calX\to\calY\subset\R^m$. In this study, we consider the simultaneous minimization of all objectives:
\begin{align}
\min_{\vect{x}\in\calX}\; \vect{f}(\vect{x}) &= \big(f_1(\vect{x}),\dots,f_m(\vect{x})\big),\\
\text{s.t.}\; g_j(\vect{x}) &\le 0,\quad j=1,\dots,J,\\
h_k(\vect{x}) &= 0,\quad k=1,\dots,K.
\label{eq:mop}
\end{align}
Trade-offs between conflicting objectives are formalized through the principle of Pareto dominance. For two objective vectors $\vect{u},\vect{v}\in\R^m$, we say $\vect{u}$ dominates $\vect{v}$ ($\vect{u}\preceq\vect{v}$) if $u_i\le v_i$ for all $i$ and $u_j < v_j$ for at least one index $j$. A feasible point $\vect{x}^\star$ is considered Pareto-optimal if no other feasible point exists that dominates it. The collection of these points in the objective space constitutes the Pareto front.

To generate the necessary data for our inverse models, we utilize the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) as a data-generation tool for the bi-objective $f_5$ problem. Rather than allowing the optimizer to run to full convergence, we strictly limit the evaluation budget to assemble an explicitly sparse archive of just $N=100$ evaluated pairs $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{100}$. 

As illustrated conceptually in Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_front}, the distribution of this sparse archive is categorized into:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Non-dominated solutions: The empirical Pareto front (represented in blue) which serves as the primary target for inverse queries.
    \item Exploratory solutions: Dominated points (represented in gray) that reflect the limited search history.
\end{itemize}

The characteristic structure of the data collected during this heavily restricted process is visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:data_distribution}. The left panel illustrates the Raw Decision Space ($x_1$ vs. $x_2$), where the optimized solutions (orange) represent the Pareto set. The right panel depicts the Raw Objective Space ($y_1$ vs. $y_2$), showing the convergence of the search history toward the non-dominated Pareto front at the lower-left boundary.

A central objective of this experimental design is to replicate the severe data sparsity commonly encountered in real-world engineering, where evaluating a forward simulator or conducting physical experiments is highly expensive. By deliberately restricting our training archive to a sparse regime of just 100 samples, we establish a rigorous stress test for all evaluated models. This heavily constrained workspace forces the deep learning baselines (MDN, CVAE, INN) to operate far below their optimal data density, allowing us to explicitly evaluate their robustness and safety under extreme limitations. Simultaneously, it provides the exact conditions needed to test whether the proposed geometric framework (GBPI) can successfully leverage spatial boundaries to navigate inverse design safely when physical data is fundamentally limited.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing and Normalization}

The sparse archive of $N=100$ paired observations is partitioned strictly for model training and validation (utilizing an 80/20 split) to ensure the deep learning baselines can be properly monitored for overfitting during optimization. However, rigorously evaluating an inverse model requires assessing its candidate generation across the entire continuous trade-off surface. Therefore, testing is conducted on an independent, high-density query set. Once trained, the models are queried using $N_{test} \ge 1000$ synthetically generated, uniformly spaced target objectives ($y^*$) spanning the true empirical Pareto front. This deliberate decoupling of the sparse training environment from the dense evaluation grid allows for a comprehensive assessment of both objective attainment and generative diversity.

Furthermore, while Figure~\ref{fig:data_distribution} visualizes the raw geometric manifolds, inverse modeling necessitates numerical stability and comparable scoring across different physical dimensions. To achieve this, we apply the \texttt{HypercubeNormalizer} defined in Section~\ref{sec:normalization}. The normalization bounds ($T_x$ and $T_y$) are estimated strictly from the training split, mapping both decision and objective spaces into a consistent $[0,1]$ coordinate system. All subsequent inverse queries and evaluation metrics (discussed in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}) are performed within this normalized framework, ensuring that deployment discrepancies are measured on a standardized, dimensionless scale.

\subsection{Modeling Parameter Rationale}

To provide a transparent basis for the comparative analysis of the $f_5$ inverse problem, the configurations for both the deep generative baselines and the proposed geometric framework were carefully established. Rather than treating hyperparameter selection as an arbitrary tuning exercise, structural choices were explicitly tied to the challenge of preventing overfitting and hallucination in the extreme sparse-data regime.

For the parametric deep generative baselines, which include the MDN, CVAE, and INN architectures, the primary objective was to balance representational capacity against the severe risk of overfitting on a limited budget of $N=100$ samples. In feedforward networks and Mixture Density Networks, the structural depth ($L \in \{2,3\}$ hidden layers) and mixture resolution ($M$) were deliberately constrained; while larger architectures enhance non-linear mapping, they rapidly memorize sparse datasets. In latent-variable formulations like the CVAE, the latent dimension ($d_z$) and the Kullback--Leibler divergence weight ($\beta$) were tuned to prioritize robust prior regularization, preventing posterior collapse while maintaining a compressed representation of the inverse manifold. For Invertible Neural Networks, the number of coupling transformations ($T$) was restricted to prevent the bijective mapping from heavily warping the base distribution around isolated data points. Across all neural baselines, optimization parameters such as learning rate ($\eta$) and epoch limits were strictly monitored against the validation set to halt training before generative hallucination could occur.

In distinct contrast to these parametric baselines, the proposed Geometrically Bounded Probabilistic Inversion (GBPI) framework does not require gradient-based optimization or latent space compression. Instead, its configuration is governed by explicitly geometric and probabilistic thresholds. The most critical hyperparameter is the Dynamic Coherence Threshold ($D_{max}$), mathematically derived from the 95th percentile of local $K$-Nearest Neighbor distances ($\mathcal{D}_{KNN}$) within the training data. This threshold dictates the framework's internal routing logic. For safe queries inside the topological bounds, the generative pathway utilizes a Dirichlet concentration parameter ($\alpha$) set to uniformly sample the barycentric simplex, with the generation budget fixed to $K=100$ candidates per query to match the sampling throughput of the neural networks. Conversely, for out-of-distribution queries that trigger a $D_{max}$ violation, the optimization pathway anchors the Trust Region boundary radius directly to the $D_{max}$ distance. This ensures that the L-BFGS-B optimizer is strictly bounded to physically known regions of the decision space. By defining both the parametric and geometric constraints explicitly, this study establishes a rigorous foundation for attributing performance differences to the underlying architectures rather than to hyperparameter advantages.

\subsection{Objective-Space Diagnostics}\label{subsec:objective_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space diagnostics evaluate inverse estimators as conditional samplers within the propose--verify loop introduced in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. For a given target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the model generates a candidate set $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$, which is subsequently mapped to the outcome space via the evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ to obtain checked outcomes $\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. This section assesses the accuracy of these outcomes to determine which framework can most reliably satisfy user queries under the strict $N=100$ data budget.

As established in the framework (Section~\ref{sec:framework}), all computations are performed in a common normalized coordinate system. This ensures that discrepancy magnitudes are comparable across objectives and that the performance metrics remain consistent across different estimator families.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Scoring Contract and Best-Shot Attainment}\label{subsubsec:objective_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Following the evaluation framework defined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, we quantify the performance of individual candidates using the target-centered residual vector $\vect{r}^{(k)}$ and the Euclidean discrepancy score $s^{(k)}$. As specified in Eq.~\ref{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid}, these are defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} - \vect{y}^{\star}, \qquad s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \rVert_{2} = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{m}\Big(r^{(k)}_{j}(\vect{y}^{\star})\Big)^{2}}.
\end{equation}
The $j$-th component $r^{(k)}_{j} > 0$ indicates that the candidate overshoots the target on objective $j$, while $r^{(k)}_{j} < 0$ indicates an undershoot.

To illustrate the application of this contract, consider a bi-objective scenario ($m=2$) where the user requests a target $\vect{y}^{\star}=(0.40,\,0.70)$. If a checked candidate outcome is $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}=(0.45,\,0.62)$, the residual vector is $\vect{r}^{(k)} = (0.05, -0.08)$. The resulting scalar discrepancy score is:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.05^{2}+(-0.08)^{2}}
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.0089}
  \;\approx\;
  0.094.
\end{equation}

Since inverse samplers return a set rather than a single point, we summarize attainment per target using the best-shot score:
\begin{equation}
  s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}) 
  \;=\; 
  \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}).
  \label{eq:objective_best_shot}
\end{equation}
This score identifies the most accurate match within the generation budget $K$. Because increasing $K$ provides more opportunities to sample a close candidate by pure chance, all comparative experiments in this chapter fix $K=100$ to ensure a fair evaluation of the models' underlying capabilities.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Empirical Cumulative Distribution Functions (ECDF)}\label{subsubsec:objective_ecdf}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

While individual residuals provide local snapshots of performance, we require a global diagnostic to assess the consistency of the inverse estimators across the entire target space. To this end, we employ the Empirical Cumulative Distribution Function (ECDF). By aggregating best-shot scores across the entire dense target set ($N_{test} \ge 1000$), the ECDF reveals how robustly a model can satisfy specific accuracy requirements under the extreme constraints of the sparse training regime. 

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:ecdf_demo}, the ECDF provides a non-parametric view of cumulative probability. In the context of inverse design, it specifically measures two critical dimensions of performance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item High-Precision Accuracy: How quickly the curve rises on the left-hand side, indicating the model's ability to identify candidates within extremely tight tolerances (e.g., $10^{-3}$).
    \item Global Reliability: The asymptotic height of the curve on the right-hand side, representing the total fraction of targets the model is capable of solving before failing completely.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \begin{adjustbox}{width=0.85\linewidth}
        \input{figures/ecdf_illustration.tex} 
    \end{adjustbox}
    \caption[Analyzing an ECDF Chart]{Illustration of an Empirical Cumulative Distribution Function (ECDF) for a sample of $n=5$. The step function rises by exactly $1/n$ at each sorted data point, allowing robust, non-parametric percentile estimations (e.g., tracking the median) independent of theoretical assumptions.}
    \label{fig:ecdf_demo}
\end{figure}

For a set of $N_{test}$ queries, the mathematical formulation of the ECDF is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{F}(t) 
  \;=\; 
  \frac{1}{N_{test}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{test}}\mathbb{I}\!\left[s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\le t\right], \quad \forall t \in \mathcal{T},
  \label{eq:objective_ecdf_definition}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function, and $\mathcal{T}$ represents the spectrum of discrepancy thresholds under evaluation. In our experiments, rather than assessing a single arbitrary tolerance, we define $\mathcal{T}$ as a continuous range (or logarithmically spaced grid) spanning from extreme high-precision requirements to loose approximations (e.g., $t \in [10^{-5}, 10^{0}]$). By sweeping the evaluation across $\mathcal{T}$, the ECDF generates a complete, non-parametric profile of the model's attainment behavior across all possible strictness levels.

\subsubsection{Objective Attainment Results: GBPI vs. Parametric Baselines}

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_ecdf_f5} presents the ECDF curves for the proposed GBPI framework alongside the highest-performing configurations of the CVAE, MDN, and INN baselines, all trained on the heavily restricted $N=100$ dataset. Plotted on a logarithmic scale, the results visually confirm the central hypothesis of this thesis: parametric neural networks suffer severe structural degradation in data-sparse environments, while the geometrically bounded framework successfully maintains rigorous mathematical precision.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/accuracy_ecdf_best_shot.png}
  \caption[Objective Attainment ECDF (Sparse Regime)]{Objective-space performance: ECDF of the best-shot discrepancy $s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star})$ for GBPI and the deep learning baselines trained on $N=100$. The logarithmic x-axis acts as a microscope on the micro-precision regime. Curves that climb rapidly on the far left indicate superior, high-fidelity target attainment.}
  \label{fig:ablation_ecdf_f5}
\end{figure}

The ECDF profiles reveal a stark divergence in high-precision capability. The proposed GBPI framework emerges as the undisputed frontrunner in the extreme micro-precision regime (discrepancy $< 10^{-3}$). By shifting the inverse design paradigm from global probabilistic approximation to local geometric anchoring, GBPI locks onto verified physical coordinates via exact Delaunay meshing (Pathway A) and strictly bounded Trust-Region optimization (Pathway B). Consequently, its attainment curve shoots upward almost immediately, registering exact matches well below the $1\mu$ scale. At a rigorous error tolerance of $100\mu$ ($10^{-4}$), GBPI has already successfully solved nearly 40\% of the target queries.

In severe contrast, the deep generative models exhibit massive attainment lag at high fidelities. At the $100\mu$ threshold where GBPI thrives, the MDN and INN remain entirely uncompetitive (below 5\% attainment). The CVAE displays the most profound degradation; forced to compress only 100 data points into a continuous Gaussian prior, its latent space collapses. The CVAE curve remains entirely flat (0\% attainment) until the error tolerance is drastically relaxed past $10^{-3}$, indicating a complete failure to resolve the fine-grained, non-linear boundaries of the $f_5$ landscape.

Crucially, the logarithmic plot captures a highly instructive trade-off between the mathematical paradigms at looser tolerances. As the discrepancy threshold relaxes toward $0.003$, the MDN and INN curves experience a sharp vertical rise, eventually overtaking GBPI. This crossover behavior perfectly illustrates the fundamental difference between geometric safety and parametric approximation. Because neural networks lack strict spatial boundaries, they freely extrapolate, scattering "blurry" candidate guesses across the entire domain. They eventually solve the remaining targets, but only if macroscopic, low-fidelity errors are deemed acceptable by the user. Conversely, GBPI's curve exhibits a deliberate plateau in the mid-range. This is not a failure, but a designed fail-safe: GBPI trades macroscopic guessing for microscopic certainty. Its Trust Region logic actively blocks the framework from hallucinating impossible physics when navigating severe topological voids, choosing to halt rather than output an unsafe design.

\subsubsection{Scalability and the Curse of Dimensionality ($D=5$)}\label{subsubsec:dimensionality_scaling}

To rigorously stress-test the inverse estimators under extreme sparsity, the experimental complexity was escalated by expanding the decision space dimensionality from $D=2$ to $D=5$, while strictly maintaining the data budget of $N=100$. The objective space remains bi-objective ($m=2$). This configuration deliberately introduces the \textit{curse of dimensionality}: as the volume of the decision space grows exponentially, the spatial density of the 100 training points drops drastically, creating vast topological voids separated by sparse, disjointed manifolds.

Figure~\ref{fig:ecdf_5d} presents the resulting ECDF profiles for the 5D configuration. The plot reveals a dramatic exacerbation of the performance gap, severely penalizing the parametric architectures while validating GBPI's geometric robustness.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/accuracy_ecdf_best_shot_5d.png}
  \caption[Objective Attainment ECDF (5D Decision Space)]{Objective-space performance in a 5D decision space ($N=100$). The curse of dimensionality severely degrades the parametric baselines (shifting them rightward into the low-fidelity regime), while GBPI maintains its micro-precision by leveraging local dimensional invariance.}
  \label{fig:ecdf_5d}
\end{figure}

The most prominent feature of the 5D analysis is the complete high-precision collapse of the neural network baselines. While the INN and MDN demonstrated marginal capability in the 2D micro-precision regime, their 5D attainment curves do not even begin to register until the discrepancy threshold is relaxed past $500\mu$ ($0.0005$). The CVAE again exhibits the most severe degradation, failing to surpass 20\% attainment until the error tolerance reaches $0.01$. 

This collapse is not a consequence of poor hyperparameter tuning, but a fundamental information-theoretic limitation. Global density estimators require dense, overlapping data to parameterize high-dimensional probability distributions. In the 5D void, 100 points do not contain enough spatial information to support these global mappings. Consequently, their latent spaces become overwhelmingly diffuse, resulting in severely "diluted" candidates that fail to map precisely to the 2D target boundaries.

In stark contrast, the proposed GBPI framework demonstrates extraordinary dimensional resilience. The GBPI curve (orange) continues to dominate the high-fidelity domain, successfully registering exact target matches at the nanometer scale ($10n$ to $100n$) and solving over 60\% of targets at a strict $1\mu$ tolerance. 

This resilience is deeply rooted in GBPI's local architectural focus. Unlike neural networks that attempt to learn the entirety of the vast 5D volume, GBPI relies on exact, localized geometric connections. If a target query falls within a validated Delaunay simplex, the exact barycentric coordinates accurately preserve the physical trade-offs entirely independent of the surrounding empty global space. Furthermore, if a query falls outside the mesh, the Trust Region boundary actively tethered to known anchor points prevents the optimizer from wandering into the unknown 5D void. Consequently, this stress test definitively proves that in severely data-constrained engineering environments, enforcing strict local geometric boundaries is mathematically superior to scaling up global parametric model capacity.

This behavior reinforces the conclusion that even with expanded latent capacities and inverse mapping, the inherent pressure to match a Gaussian prior in CVAEs limits their ability to resolve the fine-grained multi-objective boundaries of the $f_5$ landscape.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Decision-Space Diagnostics}\label{subsec:decision_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

While objective-space diagnostics evaluate whether an inverse model can satisfy a user's performance target, decision-space diagnostics analyze the quality, safety, and distribution of the generated physical configurations $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. For each held-out case $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$, we condition on $\vect{y}_i$ and draw a candidate set. In highly constrained engineering problems, hitting a performance target in the objective space is insufficient if the proposed decision variables are physically impossible, non-diverse, or extrapolated far beyond the known boundaries of the training data.

Evaluating the decision space in this study focuses on diagnosing the probabilistic health of the deep learning baselines (CVAE, MDN, INN). It is critical to note that the proposed GBPI framework is inherently exempt from these specific diagnostics. Because GBPI acts as a deterministic geometric router rather than a continuous density estimator, it does not map the decision space into continuous Gaussian or Laplace distributions. Consequently, it lacks a parametric likelihood or cumulative distribution function. Standard density-based metrics are mathematically incompatible with its architecture. Therefore, the following analysis serves strictly to evaluate the statistical reliability and calibration of the parametric baselines under the $N=100$ data budget.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Statistical Reliability and Probabilistic Accuracy}\label{subsubsec:prob_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Traditionally, generative inverse models are evaluated using density-based probabilistic metrics that assess how well the model's predicted continuous probability density function matches the true underlying distribution of the data. To evaluate the statistical reliability of the conditional neural samplers, we use the Probability Integral Transform (PIT) rank statistic. This allows us to diagnose whether a model is overconfident (underestimating error) or underconfident (overestimating error). Following Eq.~\eqref{eq:pit_empirical}, for a test case $i$, the PIT value is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    z_{i,d} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbb{I}\!\left[\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right].
\end{equation}
Here, $z_{i,d}$ represents the local quantile of the true decision value $x_{i,d}$ within the model's generated sample set. To move from local ranks to a global diagnostic, we compute the empirical calibration curve $\widehat{G}(t)$ according to Eq.~\eqref{eq:pit_ecdf}:
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{G}(t) = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathbb{I}\!\left[z_\ell \le t\right], \qquad t\in[0,1].
\end{equation}
Under perfect calibration, the ranks are uniform, and the CDF closely follows the diagonal $\widehat{G}(t) = t$. We summarize this calibration with a single scalar, the Mean Absolute Calibration Error (MACE), defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mace_def} as the average absolute deviation of the PIT curve from the diagonal:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{MACE} = \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\big|\widehat{G}(t_j)-t_j\big|.
    \label{eq:mace_def}
\end{equation}

Furthermore, to evaluate the sharpness and accuracy of these probability distributions, we employ the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS). CRPS accounts for the placement of all generated candidates, effectively penalizing both over-dispersion and systematic bias. As per Eq.~\eqref{eq:crps_expectation}, the score can be decomposed into two distinct physical quantities:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CRPS}_{i,d}
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\text{MAE}(X, x_{i,d})}_{\text{Accuracy}}
  \;-\;
  \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\text{MD}(X, X')}_{\text{Dispersion}},
  \label{eq:crps_decomposed}
\end{equation}
where $X$ and $X'$ represent independent samples drawn from the model's posterior.

Applying these metrics to the deep generative models reveals how data sparsity corrupts their internal uncertainty estimates. To clearly illustrate this degradation, we first establish the baseline calibration in the 2D decision space before examining the severe pathologies induced by the curse of dimensionality in the 5D space.

Figure~\ref{fig:pit_2d} presents the PIT calibration curves for the baseline 2D decision space. In this lower-dimensional scenario, the parametric models show varying degrees of calibration error but largely manage to approximate the ideal diagonal. The INN tracks the diagonal moderately well, indicating that its predicted uncertainty is relatively consistent with its actual errors. The CVAE and MDN display early signs of miscalibration, yet their distributions remain somewhat functional because the 2D topological voids between the 100 training points are not vast enough to trigger complete posterior collapse.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{images/pit_calibration_2d.png}
    \caption[PIT calibration curves (2D Decision Space)]{PIT calibration curves for the 2D decision space ($N=100$). The INN tracks the ideal diagonal moderately well, while the CVAE and MDN display early signs of miscalibration due to data sparsity.}
    \label{fig:pit_2d}
\end{figure}

However, when the experimental complexity is escalated to the 5D decision space, the calibration pathologies become glaringly apparent. Figure~\ref{fig:pit_5d} demonstrates how the curse of dimensionality heavily distorts the model distributions, forcing them into states of extreme overconfidence or underconfidence. 

The MDN configuration exhibits underconfidence in this high-dimensional void. Its PIT curve bulges heavily above the diagonal before dropping below it, forming an inverse S-shape. This signature indicates that the model's predicted variance is far too wide. Because the 5D latent space is starved of data, the MDN smears its probability mass across the empty topological voids, resulting in blurry, highly dispersed candidates and poor CRPS accuracy.

Conversely, the CVAE configuration displays a pronounced S-shape, dipping below the diagonal early on and crossing above it later. This classic signature indicates severe overconfidence. In an attempt to map the sparse 5D data to a continuous Gaussian prior, the CVAE's latent space collapses. It predicts a distribution that is too narrow, entirely missing the true physical variance of the inverse manifold. Finally, while the INN maintains a relatively stable diagonal track in 5D—indicating that its internal uncertainty estimates are statistically honest—it still fundamentally relies on continuous probability manifolds that, as previously proven in the objective-space diagnostics, fail to achieve exact micro-precision.
