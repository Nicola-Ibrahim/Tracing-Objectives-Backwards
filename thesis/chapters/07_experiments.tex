\chapter{Experiments}\label{chap:experiments}

\section{bi-objective case}\label{sec:exp_coco}
The selection of a bi-objective configuration for this study is motivated by the need to rigorously evaluate the inverse model's ability to capture the Pareto front while maintaining interpretability. Bi-objective problems serve as a fundamental benchmark in multi-objective optimization, providing a non-trivial trade-off surface that remains amenable to high-fidelity visualization. This dimensionality allows for a direct, intuitive assessment of how the model explores the decision space relative to the objective-space conflict. 


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data Acquisition}\label{sec:exp_coco}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To evaluate the inverse models, we construct a bi-objective optimization problem (MOP) using the COCO BBOB benchmark suite. This section describes the problem formulation and the procedure for generating the training and testing archives.

\subsubsection{Problem Formulation ($f_5$ Linear Slope)}

A general MOP involves finding a decision vector $\vect{x} \in \calX \subset \R^n$ that minimizes an objective vector $\vect{f}(\vect{x}) = (f_1(\vect{x}), \dots, f_m(\vect{x}))$. In this study, we focus on a bi-objective case ($m=2$) derived from the BBOB $f_5$ (Linear Slope) function family. We select $f_5$ due to its strong directionality and boundary effects, where the optimum lies on the domain boundary. The function is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    f_{5}(\vect{x}) = \sum_{i = 1}^{D}\big(5\,|s_i| - s_i z_i\big) + f_\mathrm{opt},
\end{equation}

where $D$ is the decision dimension and $s_i$ are signed slope coefficients. We construct our bi-objective problem by pairing two deterministic instances $(a, b)$ of this family:
\begin{equation}
    \vect{f}(\vect{x})=\big(f^{(\mathrm{inst}=a)}(\vect{x}),\; f^{(\mathrm{inst}=b)}(\vect{x})\big).
\end{equation}
COCO instances apply deterministic shifts and transformations while preserving the underlying landscape, providing a reproducible yet non-trivial trade-off surface.


\subsubsection{Data Generation via NSGA-II}

The construction of our dataset begins with a formal definition of the optimization landscape. A multi-objective optimization problem (MOP) is characterized by decision variables $\vect{x}=(x_1,\dots,x_n)\in\calX\subset\R^n$ and an objective vector $\vect{f}=(f_1,\dots,f_m)\colon \calX\to\calY\subset\R^m$. In this study, we consider the simultaneous minimization of all objectives:
\begin{align}
\min_{\vect{x}\in\calX}\; \vect{f}(\vect{x}) &= \big(f_1(\vect{x}),\dots,f_m(\vect{x})\big),\\
\text{s.t.}\; g_j(\vect{x}) &\le 0,\quad j=1,\dots,J,\\
h_k(\vect{x}) &= 0,\quad k=1,\dots,K.
\label{eq:mop}
\end{align}
Trade-offs between conflicting objectives are formalized through the principle of Pareto dominance. For two objective vectors $\vect{u},\vect{v}\in\R^m$, we say $\vect{u}$ dominates $\vect{v}$ ($\vect{u}\preceq\vect{v}$) if $u_i\le v_i$ for all $i$ and $u_j < v_j$ for at least one index $j$. A feasible point $\vect{x}^\star$ is considered Pareto-optimal if no other feasible point exists that dominates it. The collection of these points in the objective space constitutes the Pareto front.

To generate the necessary data for our inverse models, we utilize the NSGA-II algorithm as a data-generation tool for the bi-objective $f_5$ problem. Rather than retaining only the final converged Pareto set, we record the comprehensive search history to assemble an archive of $8,000$ evaluated pairs $\{(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{8000}$. This historical data provides the model with a dense and varied characterization of the objective space, spanning from high-performance Pareto-optimal solutions to sub-optimal exploratory points.

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pareto_front}, the raw distribution of this archive is categorized into:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Non-dominated solutions: The empirical Pareto front (represented in blue) which serves as the primary target for inverse queries.
    \item Exploratory solutions: Dominated points (represented in gray) that reflect the broader search history, providing the necessary context for the model to learn the global mapping $\calY \to \calX$.
\end{itemize}


The characteristic structure of the Pareto data collected during this process is visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:data_distribution}. The left panel illustrates the Raw Decision Space ($x_1$ vs. $x_2$), where the optimized solutions (orange) represent the Pareto set. These points form a distinct manifold, demonstrating the specific coordinate-wise dependencies inherent in the $f_5$ linear slope construction. The right panel depicts the Raw Objective Space ($y_1$ vs. $y_2$), where the non-dominated solutions (blue) clearly delineate the **Pareto front** at the lower-left boundary of the search history. This visual separation between the broad exploration cloud (gray) and the converged, Pareto-relevant set highlights the specific target regions our inverse model is designed to characterize before any normalization is applied.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing and Normalization}

The archive is split into training, validation, and test sets (80/10/10) using a fixed seed. While Figure~\ref{fig:data_distribution} visualizes the raw geometric manifolds and original scales of the problem, inverse modeling necessitates numerical stability and comparable scoring across different decision dimensions. To achieve this, we apply the \texttt{HypercubeNormalizer} as defined in Section~\ref{sec:normalization}. 

The normalization maps $T_x$ and $T_y$ are estimated solely from the training split, mapping both spaces into a consistent $[0,1]$ coordinate system. All subsequent evaluation metrics discussed in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} are performed within this normalized framework. This ensures that discrepancies are measured on a standardized scale regardless of the original function ranges of the decision variables or objectives visible in the raw data plots.

\subsection{Modeling Parameter Rationale}

To provide a transparent basis for the comparative analysis of the $f_5$ inverse problem, model configurations were varied systematically across architectural and optimization dimensions. No architectural component was held universally fixed across versions; instead, structural depth, width, latent dimensionality, transformation complexity, mixture resolution, and optimization trajectories were treated as tunable elements. This approach ensures that performance patterns are attributed to identifiable structural and regularization choices rather than to arbitrary hyperparameter settings.

For feedforward density-parameterizing architectures, structural capacity and distributional resolution were varied jointly. The number of hidden layers $L$ was explored within $L \in \{2,3\}$, with the number of neurons per layer $H_\ell$ adjusted to modulate representational capacity. While larger $H_\ell$ enhances the approximation of highly non-linear conditional relationships between $\vect{y}$ and $\vect{x}$, reduced $L$ or $H_\ell$ imposes a stronger inductive bias. In parallel, the number of mixture components $M$ served as a variable for expressive granularity in approximating $p(\vect{x}\mid\vect{y})$. Moderate values of $M$ encourage smoother, aggregated representations of the inverse manifold, while higher values enable the resolution of the finer, more numerous modes characteristic of complex multi-objective landscapes. The distribution family $\mathcal{K}$ of the mixture kernels was also varied, contrasting Gaussian components ($\mathcal{K}=\mathcal{N}$) for gradient stability with Laplace components ($\mathcal{K}=\mathrm{Lap}$), whose heavier tails can improve coverage in high-precision regimes.


In latent-variable formulations, the latent dimension $d_z$ was tuned across multiple values to regulate the capacity of the compressed representation. A larger $d_z$ enables a richer encoding of the inverse solution manifold and facilitates diversity in generated candidates, while a smaller $d_z$ imposes stronger compression to improve regularity. This structural choice was evaluated in conjunction with the architectural depth of the encoder and decoder networks, where the number of hidden layers $L$ was varied to modulate the complexity of the non-linear mappings into and out of the latent space. Furthermore, the weighting coefficient $\beta$ applied to the Kullback--Leibler divergence term was varied to control the strength of prior regularization. Lower values of $\beta$ prioritize reconstruction fidelity and reduce the risk of posterior collapse, whereas higher $\beta$ values enforce stricter adherence to the prior distribution, directly influencing the calibration and dispersion properties of the resulting sampler.

For invertible architectures, the number of coupling transformations $T$ was adjusted to vary the flexibility of the bijective mapping. Configurations with smaller $T$ provide a more constrained warping of the base distribution, whereas larger $T$ allows for increasingly complex transformations of probability mass. The internal hidden dimension $H_c$ of the transformation subnetworks was likewise varied to study its effect on capturing fine-grained posterior structures without excessive computational burden.

Optimization parameters were similarly treated as non-static. The learning rate $\eta$ was explored within $\eta \in \{10^{-4}, 10^{-3}, 10^{-2}\}$ to balance convergence speed with stability across different architectural settings. Furthermore, the number of training epochs $E \in \{100, 150, 200\}$ and mini-batch size $B$ were tuned to regulate the trade-off between optimization stability and the implicit regularization afforded by stochastic gradient noise. By allowing the parameter vector $(L, H_\ell, M, \mathcal{K}, d_z, \beta, T, H_c, \eta, E, B)$ to vary, the study establishes a rigorous framework for interpreting downstream differences in attainment, calibration, and discrepancy metrics.

\subsection{Objective-space diagnostics}\label{subsec:objective_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Objective-space diagnostics evaluate inverse estimators as conditional samplers within the propose--check loop introduced in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. For a given target $\vect{y}^{\star}$, the model generates a candidate set $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$, which is subsequently mapped to the outcome space via the evaluator $\mathcal{E}$ to obtain checked outcomes $\{\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{K}$. This section assesses the accuracy of these outcomes and the geometric structure of the resulting candidate clouds.

As established in the framework (Section~\ref{sec:framework}), all computations are performed in a common normalized coordinate system. This ensures that discrepancy magnitudes are comparable across objectives and that the performance metrics remain consistent across different estimator families.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Scoring contract and best-shot attainment}\label{subsubsec:objective_contract}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Following the evaluation framework defined in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, we quantify the performance of individual candidates using the target-centered residual vector $\vect{r}^{(k)}$ and the Euclidean discrepancy score $s^{(k)}$. As specified in Eq.~\ref{eq:discrepancy_residual_euclid}, these are defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)} - \vect{y}^{\star}, \qquad s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \;=\; \lVert \vect{r}^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}) \rVert_{2} = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{m}\Big(r^{(k)}_{j}(\vect{y}^{\star})\Big)^{2}}.
\end{equation}
The $j$-th component $r^{(k)}_{j} > 0$ indicates that the candidate overshoots the target on objective $j$, while $r^{(k)}_{j} < 0$ indicates an undershoot.

To illustrate the application of this contract, consider a bi-objective scenario ($m=2$) where the user requests a target $\vect{y}^{\star}=(0.40,\,0.70)$. If a checked candidate outcome is $\widehat{\vect{y}}^{(k)}=(0.45,\,0.62)$, the residual vector is $\vect{r}^{(k)} = (0.05, -0.08)$. The resulting scalar discrepancy score is:
\begin{equation}
  s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star})
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.05^{2}+(-0.08)^{2}}
  \;=\;
  \sqrt{0.0089}
  \;\approx\;
  0.094.
\end{equation}

Since inverse samplers return a set rather than a point, we summarize attainment per target using the best-shot score:
\begin{equation}
  s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}) 
  \;=\; 
  \min_{k\in\{1,\dots,K\}} s^{(k)}(\vect{y}^{\star}).
  \label{eq:objective_best_shot}
\end{equation}
This score identifies the most accurate match within the budget $K$. Because increasing $K$ provides more opportunities to sample a close candidate, all comparative experiments in this chapter fix $K$ to ensure a fair evaluation of the models' underlying conditional distributions.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Empirical Cumulative Distribution Functions (ECDF)}\label{subsubsec:objective_ecdf}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

To provide a complete profile of estimator reliability across a target set $\mathcal{T}$, we employ the Empirical Cumulative Distribution Function (ECDF). For a set of $N$ targets, the ECDF is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{F}(t) 
  \;=\; 
  \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathbb{I}\!\left[s_{\min}(\vect{y}^{\star}_i)\le t\right],
  \label{eq:objective_ecdf_definition}
\end{equation}
where $t \ge 0$ is a discrepancy threshold and $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function. The value $\widehat{F}(t)$ reports the proportion of targets solved to within accuracy $t$ under budget $K$. For example, if $N=5$ targets yield sorted best-shot scores $\{0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04, 0.10\}$, the ECDF at threshold $t=0.03$ is $\widehat{F}(0.03) = 4/5 = 0.8$.

We conducted a hyperparameter ablation study on the COCO $f_5$ (Linear Slope) problem to identify optimal configurations for the CVAE, MDN, and INN families. Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_ecdf_f5} presents the resulting ECDF curves. Visually, curves that rise faster (higher initial slope) indicate high precision, while the asymptotic behavior in the right tail indicates the fraction of targets that remain "hard" to solve.

Curves that lie higher at small $t$ indicate a more reliable near-target attainment, demonstrating the model's ability to generate at least one candidate within a specific tolerance of the conditioning target.}

The ECDF profiles on the logarithmic discrepancy scale provide a high-resolution view of model performance, particularly at the extreme precision limits required for complex inverse tasks. The \texttt{mdn (v10)} and \texttt{inn (v1)} configurations emerge as the clear frontrunners, with their curves rising significantly earlier than the competition. At a discrepancy threshold of $10\mu$ ($10^{-5}$), these models have already successfully solved approximately $25\%$ of the targets, whereas the CVAE models have yet to register a single successful attainment. This indicates that the combination of MDN’s multi-modal density estimation and the INN’s bijective mapping provides the necessary "resolution" to place candidates in the immediate vicinity of the ground truth.

A closer look at the architectural variants reveals that deeper models do not always equate to higher precision. In the INN family, the 6-layer \texttt{inn (v1)} consistently outperforms the 10-layer \texttt{inn (v5)} across the entire range of thresholds, suggesting that the simpler architecture is more effective at capturing the sharp peaks of the posterior distribution without introducing unnecessary training instability. Similarly, while \texttt{mdn (v8)} showed the best average probabilistic accuracy in earlier metrics, \texttt{mdn (v10)} demonstrates a slight advantage in the high-precision regime. This suggests that the use of Laplace kernels or increased mixture components ($K_{mix}=20$) in \texttt{v10} may provide better "best-shot" coverage for specific targets, even if the overall distribution is slightly less sharp than \texttt{v8}.

The performance gap is most pronounced when examining the Conditional Variational Autoencoders. The \texttt{cvae (v2)} model displays a massive "attainment lag," with its curve only beginning to rise steeply around the $10^{-3}$ threshold—a point where the MDN and INN models have already solved over $90\%$ of the targets. Even the more expressive \texttt{cvae (v1)} remains significantly behind the leading architectures, only crossing the $50\%$ attainment mark at a discrepancy roughly ten times larger than that of the leading MDN. This behavior reinforces the conclusion that the latent bottleneck and the inherent pressure to match a Gaussian prior in CVAEs limit their ability to resolve the fine-grained multi-objective boundaries of the $f_5$ landscape, making them less suitable for tasks requiring high-fidelity inverse solutions.


The ECDF profiles highlight distinct architectural biases. The INN configurations demonstrate the steepest initial rise, reflecting the high-precision target matching enabled by their bijective nature. In contrast, the CVAE models often plateau earlier, suggesting that the latent bottleneck may limit precision in specific target regions. For MDNs, increasing the number of mixture components generally shifts the ECDF curve upward, reflecting better coverage of multi-modal inverse solutions.

\subsection{Decision-space diagnostics}\label{subsec:decision_diagnostics}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Decision-space diagnostics evaluate inverse models as conditional samplers over decisions. For each
held-out paired case $(\vect{x}_i,\vect{y}_i)$, we condition on $\vect{y}_i$ and draw a candidate set
$\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_i\}_{k=1}^{K}$. 
Unlike objective-space metrics that measure the proximity of outcomes to a target, these diagnostics quantify whether the generated candidate set $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_i\}_{k=1}^{K}$ accurately reflects the distribution of the ground-truth decision $\vect{x}_i$ when conditioned on the observation $\vect{y}_i$. The objective is to determine if the model behaves as a statistically trustworthy estimator of the inverse mapping.


%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Statistical Reliability: PIT, Calibration Curves, and MACE}\label{subsubsec:reliability}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The reliability of a model—defined as the consistency between predicted uncertainty and observed errors—is assessed via the Probability Integral Transform (PIT) rank statistic. For a test case $i$, conditioning on $\vect{y}_i$ yields $K$ decision samples $\{\widehat{\vect{x}}^{(k)}_i\}_{k=1}^{K}$. Following Eq.~\eqref{eq:pit_empirical}, the PIT value is calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    z_{i,d} = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\mathbb{I}\!\left[\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\le x_{i,d}\right].
\end{equation}
For a perfectly calibrated conditional sampler, these values are uniformly distributed. Deviations from this uniformity provide a diagnostic signature of specific model deficiencies, such as over- or under-estimation of the posterior variance.

The calibration of the inverse models is visually analyzed using the PIT calibration curve, following the formal definition established in Section~\ref{sec:eval_design}. By aggregating the PIT values across test cases and decision dimensions to form the pooled set $\{z_\ell\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$, we compute the empirical calibration curve according to Eq.~\eqref{eq:pit_ecdf}:
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{G}(t) = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{\ell=1}^{L}\mathbb{I}\!\left[z_\ell \le t\right], \qquad t\in[0,1].
\end{equation}
This curve represents the empirical CDF of the PIT values and serves as a primary diagnostic tool for reliability. Under the condition of perfect calibration, the curve should closely follow the diagonal $\widehat{G}(t) = t$. In the context of this experiment, departures from this diagonal allow us to identify whether specific configurations lead to overconfident or underconfident conditional samplers.

To summarize calibration with a single scalar, we report the mean absolute calibration error (MACE), defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mace_def} as the average absolute deviation of the PIT curve from the diagonal over a grid $\{t_j\}_{j=1}^{J}\subset[0,1]$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{MACE} = \frac{1}{J}\sum_{j=1}^{J}\big|\widehat{G}(t_j)-t_j\big|.
    \label{eq:mace_def}
\end{equation}
A lower MACE value indicates that the PIT curve resides closer to the ideal diagonal, signifying superior statistical reliability.

The empirical results for the COCO $f_5$ dataset reveal a sharp contrast in how different architectural philosophies navigate the complexities of multi-objective inverse problems. The data clearly distinguishes between models that merely learn a simplified mapping and those that truly capture the underlying distribution.

The \texttt{mdn (v8)} configuration serves as a high-capacity powerhouse, achieving a remarkably low Mean Absolute Calibration Error (MACE $\approx$ 0.031). However, the \texttt{inn (v5)} configuration, utilizing 10 coupling layers, emerges as the most statistically reliable model in this study, tracking the ideal diagonal with the highest precision (MACE $\approx$ 0.028). Parallel to this, the \texttt{inn (v1)} model remains a strong contender, yielding a competitive MACE of 0.044. While \texttt{mdn (v10)} remains highly reliable, the slight increase in error compared to \texttt{v8} suggests that further increasing mixture complexity to 20 or changing noise kernels to Laplace may reach a point of diminishing returns.

In contrast, the CVAE models exhibit significant calibration pathologies. The \texttt{cvae (v2)} configuration, in particular, suffers from a severe overconfidence problem. This is strikingly visible in its "S-shaped" PIT curve, which deviates most aggressively from the diagonal. This signature indicates that the model's predicted distributions are far too narrow. While \texttt{cvae (v1)} performs better than its counterpart, it still exhibits a MACE (0.116) that is nearly triple that of the MDN and INN leaders.

The results for the Invertible Neural Networks show that depth is a key driver for calibration reliability in this landscape. Increasing the complexity from \texttt{inn (v1)} to \texttt{inn (v5)} (10 layers) led to the lowest observed calibration error. Ultimately, the success of the MDN and INN variants validates that capturing the "honesty" of a distribution requires an architecture that respects the underlying probabilistic geometry.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Probabilistic Accuracy: CRPS}\label{subsubsec:accuracy}
%-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Finally, the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) is employed to measure the accuracy and sharpness of the distributions. As per Eq.~\eqref{eq:crps_expectation}, for each case $i$ and each decision dimension $d$, the empirical CRPS of the sampled set relative to the truth is computed from the sampled values $\{\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\}_{k=1}^{K}$ and the realized truth $x_{i,d}$. Aggregating across decision dimensions yields a per-case score:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CRPS}_i = \frac{1}{D}\sum_{d=1}^{D}\mathrm{CRPS}\!\left(\{\widehat{x}^{(k)}_{i,d}\}_{k=1}^{K},\,x_{i,d}\right),
\end{equation}
where lower values are better. CRPS rewards models that concentrate probability mass near the ground truth while penalizing excessive dispersion, thus capturing the essential trade-off between precision and accuracy.

We evaluate probabilistic accuracy via CRPS bar charts (Figure~\ref{fig:f5_crps_ablation}). These charts summarize the typical performance across held-out cases. Hyperparameter variants are evaluated within each model family under the same candidate budget $K$ and over the same held-out test set.

The distribution of CRPS values across the various architectures provides a rigorous metric for evaluating how effectively each model concentrates its probability mass near the ground truth. In this regard, the \texttt{mdn (v8)} configuration and the \texttt{inn (v1)} configuration demonstrate clear superiority, achieving the lowest CRPS values.

The Mixture Density Network family shows that high-capacity hidden architectures are essential for minimizing probabilistic error. \texttt{mdn (v8)} stands as the most precise configuration in the study (CRPS $\approx$ 0.021). By utilizing three hidden layers of 256 units with 8 mixture components, the model provides sufficient expressive bandwidth to represent the decision space with sharp components. In contrast, the \texttt{mdn (v10)} configuration yields a slightly higher CRPS ($\approx$ 0.023).

The Invertible Neural Network configurations, \texttt{inn (v1)} and \texttt{inn (v5)}, show the impact of model depth on precision. While \texttt{inn (v1)}, with its six coupling layers, effectively transforms the latent distribution into a sharp representation of the target posterior ($\approx 0.026$), the 10-layer \texttt{inn (v5)} exhibits a slightly higher CRPS ($\approx 0.030$). This suggests a state of diminishing returns; for a 2D decision space, the six-layer architecture provides a superior balance of precision compared to the more complex 10-layer variant.

In contrast, the Conditional Variational Autoencoders exhibit the poorest accuracy metrics. \texttt{cvae (v2)}, utilizing a higher $\beta=0.5$ and a constrained latent dimension of $z=4$, suffers from a pronounced variational bottleneck that leads to overly dispersed samples and the highest observed CRPS ($\approx$ 0.064). While \texttt{cvae (v1)} results in an improved score ($\approx$ 0.034), it still fails to reach the precision levels of the MDN or INN counterparts. The higher error in both CVAE configurations confirms that the expressive mixture modeling of \texttt{mdn (v8)} and the direct bijective mapping of the INN models are the most effective strategies for high-precision inverse exploration.
